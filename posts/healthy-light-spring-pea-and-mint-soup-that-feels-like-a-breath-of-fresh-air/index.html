Healthy Light Spring Pea and Mint Soup That Feels Like a Breath of Fresh Air

Molly Dupont
Published on April 3, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
4 Servings
Easy Difficulty

This spring pea and mint soup is fresh and light, perfect for warmer days. The fresh mint adds a cool twist that lifts the sweet peas beautifully. Great as a starter or light lunch when you want something healthy but satisfying.

Pro Tips

Use fresh or frozen peas for best flavor and color.
Fresh peas provide natural sweetness and vibrant color, while frozen peas are picked and frozen at peak freshness, retaining most nutrients and flavor. Canned peas tend to be mushy and bland, which can ruin the texture and taste of the soup.
Don’t overcook the peas to keep their bright green color.
Overcooking peas causes them to lose their vibrant green hue and become mushy, which diminishes the soup’s appeal. Cooking just until tender preserves the bright color and fresh flavor, making the soup more visually appealing and tasty.
Add mint at the end off the heat to avoid bitterness.
Mint’s flavor is delicate and can turn bitter if cooked too long or at too high a temperature. Adding it after removing the soup from heat allows the fresh, cooling flavor to infuse without any harshness.
Use an immersion blender for easy, safe pureeing.
An immersion blender lets you puree the soup directly in the pot, reducing mess and risk of spills. It also allows you to control the texture easily, from slightly chunky to completely smooth, depending on your preference.
Season gradually and taste often.
Adding salt and lemon juice slowly helps you avoid over-seasoning. Tasting as you go ensures a balanced flavor where the sweetness of peas, brightness of lemon, and freshness of mint all shine through.
Serve with a dollop of Greek yogurt for creaminess.
The yogurt adds a creamy, tangy contrast that enhances the soup’s flavor and texture. It also adds protein and makes the soup feel more filling without adding heaviness.
Garnish with fresh mint leaves for visual appeal.
A few whole mint leaves on top add a pop of color and hint at the soup’s fresh flavor, making it more inviting. It also reinforces the minty aroma as you eat.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overcooking the peas until mushy and dull in color

Why it happens Many cooks assume peas need to be very soft for soup, but overcooking breaks down their structure and dulls flavor.

Fix Cook peas just until tender and bright green, about 10 minutes. Keep an eye on color and test texture frequently to avoid mushiness.

Adding mint too early and cooking it for too long

Why it happens Some think cooking mint longer will intensify flavor, but it actually turns bitter and loses brightness.

Fix Add fresh mint leaves after removing soup from heat and let them steep briefly, preserving their fresh, cool flavor.

Skipping the lemon juice or adding it before cooking

Why it happens Lemon juice added too early loses its brightness and can cook off, while skipping it leaves soup flat.

Fix Add fresh lemon juice at the end to brighten and balance flavors effectively.

Using canned peas instead of fresh or frozen

Why it happens Canned peas are convenient but often mushy and lack the fresh sweetness needed.

Fix Use fresh or frozen peas for best texture and flavor; they cook quickly and retain sweetness.

Not blending the soup enough for smooth texture

Why it happens Some prefer chunkier soups, but this recipe shines with a silky smooth finish.

Fix Use an immersion blender or regular blender to puree until completely smooth for the best mouthfeel.

Variations

Creamy Coconut Pea and Mint Soup

Swap out vegetable broth for coconut milk to add a subtle tropical creaminess. The coconut pairs beautifully with mint and peas, giving the soup a rich yet light texture and a slightly sweet flavor. It’s a great dairy-free alternative.

Note: Reduce the olive oil slightly and add coconut milk after cooking peas; blend as usual. The soup will be creamier and less tangy.

Spicy Pea and Mint Soup

Add a pinch of red pepper flakes or a small diced chili when sautéing onions and garlic for a gentle heat that contrasts nicely with the cool mint. This variation adds warmth and complexity, perfect for cooler days.

Note: Adjust spice level to taste, and add chili early in the sauté step to infuse the oil with heat.

Pea, Mint, and Basil Soup

Introduce fresh basil leaves along with mint for a herbaceous twist. Basil adds a sweet, slightly peppery flavor that complements the peas and mint, creating a more layered herb profile.

Note: Add basil leaves at the same time as mint to preserve freshness and avoid bitterness.

Pea and Mint Soup with Crispy Pancetta

For a non-vegetarian twist, top the soup with crispy pancetta bits. The salty, crunchy pancetta contrasts wonderfully with the smooth, fresh soup, adding texture and umami flavor.

Note: Cook pancetta separately until crisp and sprinkle on top just before serving to maintain crunch.

Chilled Pea and Mint Soup

Serve the soup chilled as a refreshing summer starter. This version is especially cooling and light, perfect for hot days or as a palate cleanser between courses.

Note: After blending, chill the soup in the fridge for at least 2 hours before serving. Garnish with mint and yogurt as usual.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

This soup can safely be kept at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered. Beyond that, bacteria can grow, so it’s best to refrigerate promptly.

Refrigerator

Store the soup in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. The flavor may mellow over time, and the texture can thicken slightly. Reheat gently before serving to restore the fresh taste.

Freezer

Freeze the soup in airtight containers or heavy-duty freezer bags for up to 2 months. To prevent freezer burn, remove as much air as possible before sealing. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat gently.

Reheating

Reheat over low heat on the stovetop, stirring occasionally. Add a splash of vegetable broth or water if it’s too thick. Avoid boiling to preserve the fresh flavors. Alternatively, microwave in short bursts, stirring in between.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the soup base (sauté onions and garlic, cook peas in broth) 1-2 days ahead and store it in the fridge. Add mint, lemon juice, and blend just before serving to keep the flavors fresh and vibrant.

FAQ

Can I use frozen peas instead of fresh?
Absolutely! Frozen peas are a great substitute and often just as flavorful because they’re frozen at peak freshness. Just add them directly to the hot broth without thawing and cook until tender, about 10 minutes.
What if I don’t have fresh mint? Can I use dried mint?
Fresh mint is best for its bright, cooling flavor. Dried mint tends to be less vibrant and can taste bitter when cooked. If you must use dried, add it sparingly at the end and adjust to taste, but the soup won’t be quite the same.
Can I make this soup vegan?
Yes! This soup is naturally vegan as long as you skip the optional Greek yogurt garnish or use a plant-based yogurt alternative. The olive oil and vegetable broth keep it plant-based and flavorful.
How do I store leftovers?
Keep leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. The soup may thicken when chilled; just reheat gently and add a little broth or water to reach your desired consistency.
Can I freeze this soup?
Yes, this soup freezes well for up to 2 months. Freeze in airtight containers, leaving some headspace for expansion. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat gently before serving.
Should I peel the peas before cooking?
No need to peel fresh peas for this soup. The skins add fiber and nutrients, and blending smooths out the texture. If you prefer a very silky soup, you can strain it after blending, but it’s not necessary.
What can I substitute for Greek yogurt garnish?
You can use sour cream, crème fraîche, or a plant-based yogurt alternative for a similar creamy tang. If you prefer no garnish, a drizzle of good olive oil or a few fresh mint leaves also work nicely.
How can I make the soup thicker or thinner?
For a thicker soup, use less broth or add a small potato when cooking peas to add body. For a thinner soup, add more vegetable broth or water until you reach the desired consistency.

What You'll Need

Approximately 4 servings

  • 1 tablespoon olive oil, extra virgin
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 4 cups fresh or frozen green peas
  • 4 cups vegetable broth
  • 1/2 cup fresh mint leaves, packed
  • Salt to taste
  • Freshly ground black pepper to taste
  • Juice of half a lemon
  • Optional: 1/4 cup plain Greek yogurt for garnish

How To Do It

Step 1
Heat the olive oil in a large pot over medium heat. Add the chopped onion and cook gently for about 5-7 minutes until soft and translucent but not browned. This slow cooking helps develop sweetness and depth without bitterness, which complements the peas later. Avoid turning the heat too high to prevent the onions from burning, as that would add an unpleasant bitter note to the soup.
⏱ Time: 7 minutes
✓ Doneness: Onions should be soft, translucent, and slightly fragrant but not browned
Step 2
Add the minced garlic to the pot and cook for 1-2 minutes, stirring frequently. Garlic cooks quickly and can burn easily, so keep an eye on it. This step adds a subtle aromatic base that enhances the overall flavor without overpowering the delicate peas and mint.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Garlic should be fragrant and lightly softened, not browned
Step 3
Pour in the vegetable broth and bring the mixture to a gentle boil. Adding the broth now allows the flavors to meld and gives the peas a liquid base to cook in. A gentle boil helps soften the peas evenly without breaking them down too quickly.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broth is simmering steadily with small bubbles
Step 4
Add the fresh or frozen peas to the pot. If using frozen peas, no need to thaw; they’ll cook quickly in the hot broth. Simmer for about 10 minutes until the peas are tender but still bright green. Overcooking can dull the color and flavor, so watch the timing.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Peas should be tender when pierced with a fork and vibrant green
Step 5
Remove the pot from heat and add the fresh mint leaves. Let them steep in the hot soup for 2-3 minutes to infuse their flavor without cooking them, which can make the mint taste bitter.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mint leaves will wilt but retain their bright green color
Step 6
Using an immersion blender, puree the soup directly in the pot until smooth and creamy. If you don’t have an immersion blender, carefully transfer the soup in batches to a regular blender. Blending creates a silky texture that makes the soup feel luxurious despite its simplicity.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is completely smooth with no visible pea chunks
Step 7
Stir in the lemon juice, salt, and freshly ground black pepper to taste. The lemon juice brightens the flavors and balances the sweetness of the peas, while seasoning enhances all the subtle notes. Taste and adjust seasoning gradually to avoid over-salting.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup tastes bright, balanced, and well-seasoned
Step 8
Reheat the soup gently over low heat if needed, stirring occasionally to prevent sticking. Avoid boiling again to keep the fresh flavors intact. Heating gently preserves the vibrant color and fresh taste.
⏱ Time: 3-5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is hot but not boiling
Step 9
Serve the soup warm with an optional dollop of plain Greek yogurt on top for creaminess and a little extra tang. You can also garnish with a few fresh mint leaves for a pretty presentation. Enjoy immediately for the best flavor and texture.
⏱ Time: 0 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup is creamy, smooth, and garnished attractively
