Simple 5 Ingredient Chocolate Cake with Whipped Cream Frosting You'll Love

Published on April 17, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
8 Servings
Easy Difficulty

This chocolate cake is a dream for anyone who loves rich, moist cake without fuss. Just five ingredients and a fluffy whipped cream frosting make it perfect for last-minute celebrations or cozy weekend treats.

Pro Tips

Always sift your dry ingredients before mixing.
Sifting aerates the flour and cocoa powder, which prevents lumps and helps the cake rise evenly. It also ensures the baking powder is distributed uniformly, avoiding dense spots or uneven texture.
Use room temperature eggs for better mixing.
Room temperature eggs emulsify better with the fats and liquids, creating a smoother batter with more air incorporated. Cold eggs can cause the batter to curdle or mix unevenly, affecting the cake’s texture.
Don’t overmix the batter once wet and dry ingredients are combined.
Overmixing develops gluten, which makes the cake tough rather than tender. Gentle folding keeps the crumb light and soft by preserving the air bubbles you worked to incorporate.
Chill your bowl and beaters before whipping cream.
Cold equipment helps the cream whip faster and hold air better, resulting in a fluffier, more stable frosting. Warm utensils can cause the cream to become runny or overwhip quickly.
Use parchment paper on the bottom of your cake pan.
Parchment prevents sticking and makes it easier to remove the cake without breaking it. Greasing alone might not be enough, especially for delicate cakes like this one.
Let the cake cool completely before frosting.
Frosting a warm cake will cause the whipped cream to melt and slide off, ruining the texture and appearance. Patience here ensures a neat and stable finish.
Tap the cake pan gently on the counter before baking.
This releases large air bubbles trapped in the batter, preventing holes or tunnels in the finished cake and contributing to an even crumb.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Skipping sifting of dry ingredients.

Why it happens Sometimes cooks want to save time or don’t realize lumps affect texture.

Fix Always take the extra minute to sift flour and cocoa powder. This prevents lumps and ensures even mixing, which leads to a smooth, tender cake.

Overmixing the batter after adding dry ingredients.

Why it happens It’s easy to want to mix everything thoroughly, but this develops gluten.

Fix Fold gently until just combined. Stop mixing as soon as you see no more streaks of flour to keep the cake tender.

Frosting the cake while it’s still warm.

Why it happens Impatience or rushing to serve can lead to this.

Fix Let the cake cool completely on a wire rack before frosting. If the cake is warm, the whipped cream will melt and slide off.

Using warm cream for frosting.

Why it happens Not chilling the bowl or cream can happen if you forget in the rush.

Fix Chill your mixing bowl and beaters for at least 15 minutes before whipping cream to get stable, fluffy peaks.

Opening the oven door too early during baking.

Why it happens Curiosity or timing mistakes can cause this.

Fix Wait at least 20 minutes before checking. Opening the door early can cause the cake to sink due to temperature shock.

Variations

Add Espresso Powder for a Mocha Twist

Stir 1 teaspoon of instant espresso powder into the dry ingredients to deepen the chocolate flavor with a subtle coffee note. This adds complexity without overpowering the cake’s sweetness. Great for coffee lovers!

Note: Espresso powder dissolves easily and won’t affect batter texture or rise; just mix it in with cocoa powder.

Use Almond Milk for a Dairy-Free Version

Swap whole milk for unsweetened almond milk and top with coconut whipped cream instead of dairy whipped cream. This keeps the cake moist and tasty while accommodating dairy-free diets.

Note: Almond milk is lighter, so the cake might be slightly less rich but still moist and tender.

Mix in Chocolate Chips for Extra Texture

Fold 1/2 cup semi-sweet chocolate chips into the batter before baking. The chips melt slightly, giving pockets of gooey chocolate throughout the cake, making each bite more indulgent.

Note: Coat chips lightly with flour before folding to prevent sinking to the bottom.

Top with Fresh Berries and Chocolate Shavings

After frosting, decorate with fresh raspberries or strawberries and sprinkle with dark chocolate shavings. This adds freshness and a pretty presentation, perfect for special occasions.

Note: Add berries just before serving to keep them fresh and prevent sogginess.

Make a Layered Cake with Jam Filling

Slice the cake horizontally and spread raspberry or cherry jam between layers before frosting. This adds a fruity contrast to the rich chocolate and creamy frosting, elevating the flavor profile.

Note: Chill the cake before slicing to get clean layers and prevent crumbling.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Keep the cake covered loosely with a cake dome or plastic wrap at room temperature for up to 1 day. The whipped cream frosting is best fresh but will hold for a short time without refrigeration if the room is cool.

Refrigerator

Store the cake in an airtight container or cover with plastic wrap in the fridge for up to 3 days. The whipped cream may firm up and the cake slightly dry out, so let it sit at room temperature for 15-20 minutes before serving.

Freezer

Freeze the unfrosted cake wrapped tightly in plastic wrap and aluminum foil for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator and whip fresh cream to frost after thawing to maintain the best texture.

Reheating

This cake is best served at room temperature. Avoid reheating as the whipped cream frosting will melt. If needed, warm slices gently in a microwave for 10-15 seconds without frosting.

Make Ahead

You can bake the cake layers 1 day ahead and store wrapped tightly at room temperature or refrigerated. Whip and apply the cream frosting on the day you plan to serve for the freshest taste and texture.

FAQ

Can I use a different type of flour for this cake?
All-purpose flour works best for this recipe because it has the right protein content to create a tender yet structured crumb. Using cake flour will make the cake more delicate but might affect the rise, while bread flour will make it denser. If you want to try cake flour, reduce the amount slightly and sift well.
How do I know when the cake is fully baked?
Insert a toothpick into the center of the cake; it should come out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter. The cake should spring back lightly when you gently press the top. Avoid opening the oven too early to prevent sinking.
Can I make this cake vegan?
To make a vegan version, substitute eggs with flax eggs (1 tablespoon ground flaxseed + 3 tablespoons water per egg) and use plant-based milk and vegan butter or oil. For the frosting, try coconut cream whipped until fluffy. The texture will differ slightly but still delicious.
Why is my whipped cream frosting not holding peaks?
Make sure your cream, bowl, and beaters are well chilled before whipping. Also, use heavy cream with at least 36% fat. Overwhipping can cause it to break down, so stop once soft or medium peaks form depending on your preference.
Can I use a different frosting instead of whipped cream?
Yes, buttercream or cream cheese frosting can be used if you prefer something richer or more stable for warmer climates. Keep in mind that whipped cream is lighter and balances the cake’s density better.
How should I store leftover cake with whipped cream frosting?
Store leftovers in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 3 days. The cake may firm up and the cream may slightly lose its fluffiness, so bring to room temperature before serving for best texture.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, doubling the ingredients works well. Use two 8-inch pans or one larger pan, but adjust baking time accordingly. Start checking for doneness at the original bake time and add more time as needed.
What if I don’t have a mixer to whip the cream?
You can whip cream by hand using a whisk, but it requires more effort and time. Chill your bowl and whisk, then whisk vigorously in a circular motion until soft peaks form. It’s a great arm workout!

What You'll Need

One 8-inch round cake, approximately 8 servings

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1/2 cup whole milk
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, melted
  • 1 cup heavy whipping cream, chilled
  • 2 tablespoons powdered sugar
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and grease an 8-inch round cake pan, then line it with parchment paper. This prep ensures your cake releases easily and bakes evenly. Make sure to grease the sides well to avoid sticking. Don’t skip the parchment on the bottom, as it helps lift the cake out cleanly once baked.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Pan is evenly greased with parchment paper smoothly placed
Step 2
In a large bowl, sift together the flour, cocoa powder, and baking powder. Sifting removes lumps and aerates the dry ingredients, which helps the cake rise nicely and have a smooth texture. Mixing these first also ensures the leavening is evenly distributed throughout the batter.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are lump-free and well combined
Step 3
In a separate bowl, whisk the eggs and sugar together until light and slightly frothy. This step incorporates air into the eggs, which contributes to the cake’s lightness. Whisking thoroughly but gently is key; you want the mixture to be well combined but not overly thick.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Eggs and sugar form a pale, slightly frothy mixture
Step 4
Add the melted butter and milk to the egg mixture, stirring gently to combine. The temperature of the melted butter should be warm, not hot, to avoid cooking the eggs. Mixing gently keeps the batter tender and prevents gluten overdevelopment, which can make the cake tough.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Wet ingredients are smooth and well incorporated
Step 5
Slowly add the wet ingredients to the dry ingredients, folding carefully with a spatula until just combined. Overmixing at this stage can develop gluten, making the cake dense. Folding gently preserves the air bubbles introduced earlier, ensuring a fluffy texture.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is smooth with no visible streaks of flour
Step 6
Pour the batter into the prepared pan and smooth the top with a spatula. This helps the cake bake evenly and prevents cracking. Tap the pan lightly on the counter to release any large air bubbles trapped in the batter.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter surface is even and free of large air pockets
Step 7
Bake for 25-30 minutes until a toothpick inserted in the center comes out with a few moist crumbs. Avoid opening the oven door too early to prevent the cake from sinking. The edges should start to pull away slightly from the pan when done.
⏱ Time: 25-30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake springs back lightly when touched, toothpick is mostly clean
Step 8
Let the cake cool in the pan for 10 minutes, then invert onto a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling in the pan too long can cause sogginess, while cooling upside down prevents the cake from drying out and helps maintain moisture.
⏱ Time: 20-30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake is fully cooled and firm to the touch
Step 9
For the frosting, whip the chilled heavy cream with powdered sugar and vanilla extract until soft peaks form. Be careful not to overwhip or it will turn grainy and start to separate. Soft peaks give a creamy, light texture that complements the rich chocolate cake perfectly.
⏱ Time: 5-7 minutes
✓ Doneness: Whipped cream holds soft peaks and is smooth
Step 10
Spread the whipped cream frosting evenly over the cooled cake. You can use a spatula or a piping bag for a prettier finish. The light frosting balances the dense chocolate cake, making each bite melt in your mouth.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Frosting is smooth and evenly coated on the cake
