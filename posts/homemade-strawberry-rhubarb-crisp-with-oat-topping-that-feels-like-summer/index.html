Homemade Strawberry Rhubarb Crisp with Oat Topping That Feels Like Summer

Molly Dupont
Published on March 28, 2026
20 min Prep Time
40 min Time
6 Servings
Medium Difficulty

This strawberry rhubarb crisp blends tart rhubarb with sweet berries under a crunchy oat topping. It’s a cozy dessert that’s perfect for spring and summer evenings. The oat topping adds a lovely texture contrast!

Pro Tips

Use cold butter and keep it chilled until mixing with the oats.
Cold butter melts slowly in the oven, creating steam pockets that make the topping flaky and crisp. If the butter is too warm, it mixes too thoroughly and the topping turns dense and greasy instead of crumbly.
Don’t overmix the fruit filling after adding cornstarch.
Overmixing breaks down the fruit, releasing too much juice and making the filling mushy. Gentle tossing ensures the fruit pieces stay intact and the thickener works effectively.
Let the crisp cool for at least 20 minutes before serving.
Cooling allows the filling to thicken and set, preventing it from running off the spoon. It also helps the topping stay crisp rather than soggy from hot steam.
Use a baking dish with straight sides for even cooking.
Straight-sided dishes allow heat to circulate evenly, so the topping browns uniformly and the fruit cooks consistently without drying out.
Add a splash of lemon juice or zest to the fruit mixture.
Lemon brightens the flavors and balances the sweetness, enhancing the natural tartness of rhubarb and strawberries for a more vibrant dessert.
Avoid using frozen fruit unless you drain excess liquid well.
Frozen fruit releases more water during baking, which can make the filling watery. Draining or lightly cooking frozen fruit beforehand helps maintain the crisp texture.
Serve with vanilla ice cream or whipped cream for contrast.
The creamy cold topping contrasts with the warm, tart fruit and crunchy topping, creating a perfect balance of textures and temperatures.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using warm butter in the oat topping

Why it happens Many cooks don’t chill the butter properly or start mixing too soon, causing it to soften and blend too much with the flour and oats.

Fix Keep the butter in the fridge until just before mixing and use a pastry cutter or fingers to cut it in quickly. If it gets too soft, chill the mixture before baking.

Not thickening the fruit filling enough

Why it happens Skipping or under-measuring cornstarch leads to a runny filling that won’t set properly after baking.

Fix Measure cornstarch carefully and toss it thoroughly with sugar before adding to fruit. This ensures even distribution and proper thickening.

Overbaking the crisp

Why it happens Leaving the crisp in too long dries out the topping and makes the fruit filling tough.

Fix Watch for bubbling juices and golden topping as cues to remove from oven. Use a timer and check 5 minutes before expected finish time.

Mixing the oat topping too much

Why it happens Overmixing breaks down the butter chunks and results in a dense, cakey topping rather than crumbly.

Fix Mix just until pea-sized butter pieces remain visible. Stop as soon as the mixture resembles coarse crumbs.

Using underripe or overripe fruit

Why it happens Underripe fruit is too tart and firm; overripe fruit becomes mushy and overly sweet, affecting texture and flavor.

Fix Pick fruit that is ripe but still firm to the touch for the best balance of sweetness and texture.

Variations

Peach Rhubarb Crisp

Swap strawberries for sliced fresh peaches for a sweeter, juicier twist. The peach’s natural sweetness pairs wonderfully with tart rhubarb and the oat topping.

Note: Peaches release more juice, so consider adding an extra tablespoon of cornstarch to thicken the filling.

Gluten-Free Crisp

Use gluten-free rolled oats and a gluten-free flour blend in the topping to make this crisp safe for gluten-sensitive eaters without sacrificing texture.

Note: Gluten-free flours absorb moisture differently, so monitor the topping’s consistency and add a bit more flour if too wet.

Vegan Crisp

Replace butter with a firm coconut oil or vegan butter substitute. This keeps the topping crumbly and adds a subtle coconut flavor that complements the fruit.

Note: Use chilled coconut oil and mix quickly to mimic the cold butter technique for best crumble texture.

Nutty Crisp

Add 1/2 cup chopped pecans or walnuts to the oat topping for extra crunch and a toasty flavor dimension that pairs well with the fruit filling.

Note: Toast nuts lightly before adding to release oils and deepen flavor without making topping greasy.

Spiced Crisp

Stir in 1/2 teaspoon ground ginger and a pinch of nutmeg to the fruit mixture for a warm, spicy note that enhances the natural tartness of rhubarb.

Note: Add spices carefully to avoid overpowering; they should complement, not mask, the fruit flavors.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

The crisp can safely keep at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered loosely with foil or a clean kitchen towel. Beyond that, it’s best to refrigerate to maintain freshness and prevent spoilage.

Refrigerator

Store leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. The topping may soften slightly, so reheat gently in the oven to restore crispness before serving. It’s still delicious cold but the texture changes.

Freezer

Freeze the crisp in a tightly wrapped container or foil for up to 3 months. To prevent freezer burn, wrap it well and place in a freezer bag. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat in the oven until warmed through and topping is crisp again.

Reheating

Reheat in a 350°F oven for 15-20 minutes until warm and topping crisps up. Avoid microwaving as it softens the topping. If needed, add a splash of water or juice to the filling to keep it moist during reheating.

Make Ahead

You can assemble the crisp up to 24 hours ahead and keep it covered in the fridge. Bake it fresh just before serving for best topping texture and flavor. Alternatively, bake it fully and reheat gently when ready to serve.

FAQ

Can I use frozen strawberries and rhubarb for this crisp?
Yes, but frozen fruit releases more water during baking, which can make the crisp watery. To avoid this, thaw the fruit and drain excess liquid before mixing with sugar and cornstarch. You might also want to add a bit more cornstarch to help thicken the extra juices.
What can I substitute for cornstarch if I don’t have any?
You can substitute tapioca starch or arrowroot powder in equal amounts. These thickeners work similarly to cornstarch by absorbing liquid and creating a gel during baking. Avoid flour as a thickener here since it can make the filling cloudy and less glossy.
How do I keep the oat topping crunchy and not soggy?
Using cold butter and mixing it into the dry ingredients until coarse crumbs form creates a crumbly texture that crisps well. Also, avoid pressing the topping down on the fruit so it stays loose and bakes crisp. Baking at the right temperature and cooling the crisp before serving helps maintain crunch too.
Can I make this crisp gluten-free?
Absolutely! Use certified gluten-free rolled oats and substitute the all-purpose flour with a gluten-free blend. Be aware that gluten-free flours absorb moisture differently, so you may need to adjust the topping’s consistency slightly by adding a touch more flour or oats.
What’s the best way to reheat leftover crisp?
Reheat in a 350°F oven for 15-20 minutes to warm through and restore topping crispness. Avoid microwaving as it tends to make the topping soggy. If the fruit looks dry, add a splash of water or juice before reheating to keep it moist.
Can I prepare the crisp the day before serving?
Yes, you can assemble it the day before and keep it covered in the fridge. Bake it fresh on serving day for the best texture. Alternatively, bake it the day before and reheat gently in the oven before serving.
Why is my fruit filling too runny after baking?
This usually happens if there’s not enough thickener like cornstarch or if the fruit releases excess water (common with frozen fruit). Make sure to measure cornstarch correctly and toss it well with sugar before adding. Also, avoid overripe or watery fruit.
Can I add nuts to the oat topping?
Definitely! Chopped pecans or walnuts add great crunch and flavor. Toast them lightly before mixing into the topping for a deeper, nuttier taste. Just be sure not to add too many or it might overwhelm the oat mixture’s texture.

What You'll Need

Approximately 8 cups, 6 servings

  • 4 cups fresh strawberries, hulled and halved
  • 3 cups rhubarb stalks, chopped into 1/2 inch pieces
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/4 cup cornstarch
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1 cup rolled oats
  • 3/4 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar, packed
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, cold and cut into cubes

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). In a large mixing bowl, combine the chopped rhubarb and halved strawberries. Toss them gently with granulated sugar, cornstarch, and vanilla extract. This mix helps the fruit release its natural juices and thickens the filling as it bakes, preventing it from becoming watery. Be careful not to overmix, or the fruit might get mushy before baking.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Fruit pieces are evenly coated and the mixture looks glossy but not watery.
Step 2
In a separate bowl, mix together the rolled oats, all-purpose flour, brown sugar, ground cinnamon, and salt. Add the cold cubed butter and use a pastry cutter or your fingers to work it into the dry ingredients until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs with some pea-sized bits. The cold butter creates pockets of steam during baking, which helps the topping become crisp and flaky rather than dense.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Topping mixture holds together loosely when pressed but still has crumbly texture.
Step 3
Pour the fruit mixture into a greased 9x13 inch baking dish, spreading it out evenly. Then sprinkle the oat topping evenly over the fruit layer. Don’t press down the topping; letting it stay crumbly ensures a nice crunchy texture after baking.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Fruit is fully covered by an even layer of oat topping.
Step 4
Place the baking dish on the middle rack of your preheated oven. Bake for about 40 minutes, or until the topping is golden brown and the fruit filling is bubbling around the edges. The bubbling indicates the cornstarch has thickened the juices properly.
⏱ Time: 40 minutes
✓ Doneness: Topping is golden and crisp; fruit filling bubbles visibly.
Step 5
Once baked, remove the crisp from the oven and let it cool for at least 20 minutes. This resting time allows the filling to set up so it’s easier to serve and the flavors meld beautifully. Resist the urge to dig in immediately, even though it smells incredible!
⏱ Time: 20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Filling thickens and topping remains crisp upon cooling.
Step 6
Serve the crisp warm, ideally with a scoop of vanilla ice cream or a dollop of whipped cream. The cold cream contrasts perfectly with the warm fruity filling, making each bite a delightful balance of temperatures and textures.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Ice cream starts to melt slightly over warm crisp.
Step 7
If you want to prepare ahead, assemble the crisp but don’t bake it. Cover tightly with plastic wrap and refrigerate for up to 24 hours. When ready, bake as directed, adding a few extra minutes if baking straight from the fridge.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes prep
✓ Doneness: Topping remains crumbly and fruit mixture is chilled but not frozen.
Step 8
For a deeper flavor, you can add a tablespoon of lemon juice or a teaspoon of lemon zest to the fruit mixture. This brightens the tartness and complements the rhubarb beautifully. Just fold it in gently before adding the topping.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Fruit mixture has a fresh citrus aroma.
Step 9
To avoid a soggy bottom, make sure your baking dish is well greased or lined with parchment paper. Also, using fresh rhubarb and ripe strawberries ensures the filling cooks evenly and thickens properly without excess water.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Bottom of the crisp is firm and not watery after baking.
