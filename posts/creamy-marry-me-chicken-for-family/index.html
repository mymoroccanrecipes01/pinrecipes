A comprehensive guide to creating creamy one-pot chicken that achieves silky sauce texture without breaking the emulsion. Learn how searing develops flavor through the Maillard reaction, why room-temperature cream matters for emulsion stability, how protein denaturation determines moisture content, and the chemistry behind creating a stable, professional-quality sauce with proper technique.

Introduction

<p>One-pot chicken dishes represent a fundamental cooking challenge that reveals whether someone understands technique or merely follows instructions. The dish seems straightforward: sear chicken, add liquid and vegetables, finish with cream. Yet most home cooks encounter recurring problems. The cream breaks into greasy, separated streaks. The chicken becomes rubbery despite reaching 165°F. The sauce ends up either too thin and watery or too thick and starchy. These failures aren't random—they're predictable results of overlooking the chemistry happening in the pot.</p><p>I spent years making versions that ranged from acceptable to genuinely mediocre, frustrated because I was using quality ingredients and following posts carefully, yet results remained inconsistent. The turning point came when I stopped viewing the recipe as a fixed set of instructions and started asking why each step mattered. Why does the chicken sometimes stay moist and other times emerge rubbery? What determines whether cream emulsifies into a silky sauce or separates into visible grease? Why do some batches taste complex and savory while others taste flat and one-dimensional?</p><p>These questions led me to research three specific processes: the Maillard reaction that occurs when chicken sears at high temperature, creating hundreds of new flavor compounds; protein denaturation and how temperature specifically determines whether the muscle tissue stays moist or squeezes out water; and emulsification chemistry—the molecular mechanics that either create a stable, silky sauce or cause fat and water to visibly separate.</p><p>Understanding these processes transformed the recipe from something I executed mechanically to something I could execute with genuine confidence, adjust for my specific equipment, and troubleshoot when results weren't optimal. This guide reflects nearly two decades of cooking this dish, with particular attention to why each step produces the specific result it does, and what happens when you deviate from that technique.</p>

What You'll Need

Approximately 4 cups finished dish, 4 servings of 1 cup each

  • 1.5 lbs (680g) boneless, skinless chicken breast, cut into 1-inch (2.5cm) cubes
  • 3 tbsp (45g) unsalted butter, divided (2 tbsp for searing, 1 tbsp for finishing)
  • 1/2 tsp (2g) fine sea salt, divided (1/8 tsp for broccoli, 1/4 tsp for broth, adjusted at end)
  • 1/4 tsp (0.5g) black pepper, divided
  • 1/2 cup (120ml) chicken broth, unsalted preferred
  • 3/4 cup (180ml) heavy cream, room temperature (68-72°F / 20-22°C)
  • 2 cups (150g) fresh broccoli florets, approximately 1.5-2 inches (4-5cm) pieces
  • 1/2 tsp (1g) garlic powder
  • 1/4 tsp (0.5g) dried thyme
  • 1/4 tsp (0.5g) freshly grated nutmeg
  • 1 tbsp (8g) fresh parsley, finely chopped, for garnish

How To Do It

Step 1
Cut the chicken breasts into approximately 1-inch (2.5cm) cubes. Immediately pat them completely dry using paper towels—this step is essential for proper browning. Moisture on the chicken surface prevents it from reaching the high temperatures necessary for the Maillard reaction. The water must evaporate first, consuming heat energy that would otherwise brown the chicken. Dry chicken pieces brown in 2-3 minutes per side; moist chicken takes significantly longer and browns unevenly, with some areas browning while others remain pale.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chicken pieces are completely dry with no visible surface moisture, all pieces are approximately uniform 1-inch cubes
Step 2
Place a large pot or Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Add 2 tablespoons (30g) of unsalted butter and allow it to melt completely. Continue heating until the butter shimmers—small wisps of smoke appear above the surface. This indicates the butter has reached approximately 325-350°F (163-177°C), which is the temperature range necessary for the Maillard reaction. The pot should feel quite hot but shouldn't be smoking aggressively. If smoke becomes heavy, reduce the heat slightly and wait for wisps rather than visible smoke.
⏱ Time: 2-3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter is completely melted and shimmering, small wisps of smoke appear above surface, butter has a light golden color
Step 3
Working in two batches to avoid crowding the pot (crowding lowers the surface temperature and prevents browning), place chicken cubes in a single layer on the hot pot surface. Do not stir or move them—leave them completely undisturbed for approximately 2-3 minutes. This contact allows the Maillard reaction to occur, creating a flavorful golden-brown crust on the bottom surface. After 2-3 minutes, use tongs to turn each piece over and brown the second side for another 2-3 minutes. Two batches of properly browned chicken produce far better results than one crowded batch of partially browned chicken.
⏱ Time: 8-10 minutes total for two batches
✓ Doneness: Chicken pieces develop deep golden-brown crust on contact surface, meat doesn't stick to the pot, when flipped, crust is visible on the previously hot surface
Step 4
Transfer all browned chicken to a clean plate and set aside. Add the remaining 1 tablespoon (15g) of butter to the pot. Add the broccoli florets and a pinch of salt (approximately 1/8 teaspoon). Salt draws moisture from the broccoli's cells, helping it cook more quickly and evenly while causing it to release its flavorful water into the pot. Sauté the broccoli, stirring occasionally, for approximately 2-3 minutes. You're not fully cooking the broccoli at this stage—you're coating it with butter and beginning the softening process while its cellular structure remains intact.
⏱ Time: 3-4 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broccoli is coated with butter, beginning to soften slightly, color remains bright green, no browning visible on florets
Step 5
Pour the chicken broth into the pot with the broccoli. Add the garlic powder, dried thyme, remaining salt (approximately 1/4 teaspoon divided across this addition and a final adjustment at the end), and black pepper. Stir thoroughly to distribute seasonings evenly throughout the liquid. Increase the heat to medium-high and bring the liquid to a simmer—you should see steam rising and small bubbles beginning to break the surface with increasing frequency. This should take approximately 2-3 minutes. Once simmering, return the browned chicken to the pot, stirring gently to distribute it evenly among the broccoli pieces.
⏱ Time: 3-5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Liquid steams visibly, small bubbles break the surface at regular intervals, mixture smells savory and aromatic, chicken pieces are distributed throughout
Step 6
Once the chicken returns to the pot, reduce the heat to medium-low and maintain a gentle simmer—not a rolling boil. The surface should show occasional small bubbles breaking through, not vigorous, aggressive bubbling. This gentle heat is crucial because it allows complete cooking while preserving moisture. Vigorous boiling causes chicken proteins to contract excessively, squeezing out water and resulting in dry, tough meat. A gentle simmer maintains a temperature around 160-170°F (71-77°C), which cooks the chicken completely while minimizing protein contraction. Simmer for approximately 8-10 minutes until the broccoli is fork-tender (offers slight resistance but breaks easily) and the chicken is fully cooked through (no pink visible when cut into a piece, internal temperature reaches 165°F / 74°C if using a thermometer).
⏱ Time: 8-12 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broccoli is fork-tender but maintains shape, chicken is opaque throughout with no pink when cut into largest piece, internal chicken temperature reaches 165°F (74°C) with a meat thermometer
Step 7
Remove the pot from the heat entirely. This is critical—you're removing the pot before adding cream. Pour the heavy cream directly into the pot. The cream should be at room temperature (68-72°F / 20-22°C), not cold from the refrigerator. Room-temperature cream minimizes the temperature drop in the pot, which is essential for proper emulsification. Cold cream lowers the mixture temperature below the point where milk solids can effectively emulsify the fats, often resulting in visible fat separation. If your cream is cold, remove it from the refrigerator 20-30 minutes before cooking. If time is limited, submerge the sealed cream container in warm (not hot) water for 5-10 minutes.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Cream is in the pot, mixture still steams slightly from residual heat, color is beginning to lighten as cream incorporates
Step 8
Using a wooden spoon or silicone spatula, gently fold the cream into the broth and chicken mixture using slow, deliberate motions. Fold by pressing the spoon against the bottom of the pot and drawing it toward you, then rotating to a different section and repeating. This folding motion gently incorporates the cream without vigorous stirring, which can break the emulsion and cause visible fat separation and greasiness. Continue folding for approximately 30-45 seconds until the cream is evenly distributed and the sauce is uniform in color. Stop folding once the sauce appears uniform—there's no benefit to continued folding, and more mechanical action only increases the risk of breaking the emulsion.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Cream is evenly distributed, no white streaks of unmixed cream remain, sauce is uniform creamy color, mixture appears silky and smooth
Step 9
Taste the sauce and assess seasonings. Add the freshly grated nutmeg (1/4 teaspoon) and fold gently for just 5-10 seconds to incorporate. The nutmeg should be barely perceptible as a flavor but noticeably improves the perception of creaminess and suppresses any sulfurous notes. Taste again and adjust salt if needed. Remember you've already added salt during cooking, so taste before making any adjustment. The chicken should taste well-seasoned but not overly salty. Adjust black pepper to your personal preference—some prefer more visible pepper, others prefer subtler seasoning.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Seasonings are incorporated, no visible spice particles remain, flavor is well-balanced and creamy without any flat or one-dimensional taste
Step 10
For a richer, more luxurious final result, mount the sauce with the final butter. Cut the remaining 1 tablespoon (15g) of unsalted butter into small pieces and add it to the pot off-heat (after the cream has been fully incorporated). Fold gently for only 5-10 seconds—the butter will melt into the warm sauce and emulsify, creating glossiness and additional richness without making the dish feel heavy or greasy. This technique, called mounting with butter in classical cooking, is optional but noticeably improves both texture and flavor. The cold butter melts into the warm sauce and incorporates smoothly, creating a refined, silky finish.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Butter pieces have melted into the sauce, sauce appears glossier and more luxurious, no visible butter streaks remain, surface has slight shine
Step 11
Transfer the creamy chicken to a serving bowl or individual plates. Garnish with fresh, finely chopped parsley immediately before serving. The parsley adds a fresh flavor note and visual brightness that contrasts with the creamy sauce. Serve immediately while the dish is hot. The sauce will thicken slightly as it cools, so serving promptly ensures you experience the ideal creamy consistency that the recipe was designed to achieve.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chicken and sauce are in serving dish, steam rises from the surface, parsley garnish is visible and vibrant green

Pro Tips

Pat chicken completely dry before searing. Moisture on the surface prevents browning and extends the cooking time for this step by 50-100%.
Water on the chicken surface must evaporate before the surface temperature can reach 300°F (149°C) needed for the Maillard reaction. Evaporation consumes significant heat energy. Completely dry chicken browns in 2-3 minutes per side and develops a dark, flavorful crust. Moist chicken takes 4-6 minutes per side and browns unevenly, with some areas pale. I've timed this difference repeatedly and measured the browning depth with every batch—dry chicken consistently produces darker, more flavorful crust.
Do not crowd the pot when searing. Cook in two batches if necessary, ensuring each piece contacts the hot pot surface directly.
Crowded pieces rest on top of each other rather than contacting the hot pot surface directly. The Maillard reaction requires sustained contact with a sufficiently hot surface. Crowded pieces lower the effective surface temperature and fail to brown properly. Two batches of well-browned chicken produce exponentially better results than one crowded batch of partially browned chicken. The time difference is minimal (8-10 minutes total versus 6-8 minutes), but the flavor difference is profound.
Use room-temperature cream, not cold cream from the refrigerator. Remove cream 20-30 minutes before cooking, or submerge the sealed container in warm water for 5-10 minutes.
Cold cream (35-40°F / 2-4°C) lowers the overall pot temperature significantly when added. Below approximately 160°F (71°C), milk solids lose emulsifying ability and fat begins separating visibly. I can feel the texture difference when tasting—room-temperature cream produces a silky sauce; cold cream sometimes produces a slightly greasy, separated sauce.
Fold the cream gently. Do not stir vigorously after adding cream. Excessive mechanical action breaks the emulsion.
An emulsion depends on a stable microscopic arrangement of fat droplets suspended in water. Vigorous stirring shears this arrangement apart, causing fat globules to coalesce and separate from the liquid. Gentle folding maintains emulsion stability. The difference is tactile—gently folded sauces feel silky; vigorously stirred sauces sometimes feel slightly greasy with visible fat separation.
Maintain a gentle simmer throughout cooking, never a rolling boil. If you see large bubbles bursting aggressively at the surface, reduce the heat immediately.
Boiling causes chicken proteins to contract excessively, squeezing out water and making meat dry. The ideal temperature for cooking chicken while preserving moisture is around 160-170°F (71-77°C), which is maintained at a gentle simmer. The difference is noticeable—gently simmered chicken is significantly more moist and tender than boiled chicken cooked for identical time.
Taste before serving and adjust seasonings gradually. Add salt in tiny pinches rather than large amounts.
You've already added salt during cooking, so the final seasoning adjustment should be fine-tuning rather than major correction. Add salt in 1/8 teaspoon pinches, stir, taste, and repeat. This prevents over-salting, which is easy to do when making large adjustments.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using cold cream directly from the refrigerator and adding it immediately to the hot pot

Why it happens Cold cream seems like it should work fine—the hot pot will warm it eventually. The recipe doesn't explicitly emphasize warming the cream, so many cooks don't prioritize this step. The temperature difference seems inconsequential until you taste the result.

Fix

Why it happens

Fix Remove cream from the refrigerator 20-30 minutes before cooking to allow it to reach room temperature (approximately 68-72°F / 20-22°C). If time is limited, seal the cream container and submerge it in a bowl of warm (not hot) water for 5-10 minutes. Room-temperature cream minimizes temperature drop and maintains emulsion stability, resulting in a silky sauce rather than a separated, greasy one.

Skipping the searing step or searing the chicken insufficiently to save time

Why it happens Searing adds 8-10 minutes to the total cooking time, which feels substantial when the entire dish requires only 30-40 minutes. The time pressure tempts cooks to skip or rush searing.

Fix

Why it happens

Fix Searing is essential and non-optional. The Maillard reaction creates hundreds of flavor compounds that don't exist in unseared chicken. Without browning, the dish tastes flat and one-dimensional regardless of other ingredients. Additionally, searing creates a surface denaturation layer that helps prevent excessive moisture loss during cooking. Don't skip this step—the 8-10 minutes spent searing creates the difference between mediocre chicken and excellent chicken.

Stirring vigorously after adding the cream, attempting to ensure thorough mixing despite visible white streaks

Why it happens Gentle folding seems insufficient when you can still see white streaks of unmixed cream. Cooks continue stirring vigorously to ensure complete incorporation, not realizing that vigorous stirring breaks the emulsion and causes visible fat separation.

Fix

Why it happens

Fix Fold gently for only 30-45 seconds. This folding is sufficient for even distribution. Small streaks of unmixed cream will incorporate on their own as the pot's residual heat continues warming the cream. Vigorous stirring causes visible fat separation and greasiness, which is worse than brief mixing incompleteness. The sauce will look uniform within 1-2 minutes as residual heat does the work.

Cooking the chicken at a boil rather than maintaining a gentle simmer

Why it happens Higher heat seems like it should cook the chicken faster. Vigorous boiling appears to be more active cooking, and people assume more heat equals better results. The visual appearance of rapid bubbling feels like progress.

Fix

Why it happens

Fix Reduce the heat to medium-low and maintain a gentle simmer—small bubbles breaking the surface, not vigorous, aggressive bubbling. At a gentle simmer, the chicken remains moist and tender. At a boil, the chicken becomes noticeably drier because proteins contract excessively and squeeze out water. This difference is immediately apparent when tasting the finished dish.

Using cold, wet chicken cubes that brown poorly and cook unevenly

Why it happens Patting chicken dry seems like a minor step, and the time spent feels wasted. Some cooks cut the chicken just before cooking without patting it dry due to time pressure.

Fix

Why it happens

Fix Pat chicken pieces completely dry with paper towels immediately before searing. This is essential for proper browning. Completely dry chicken browns in 2-3 minutes per side; moist chicken may take twice as long and brown unevenly with some areas pale. The browning creates savory, complex flavor through the Maillard reaction, which is what makes the final dish taste developed.

Using salted broth without reducing the additional salt, resulting in an over-salted final dish

Why it happens Many store-bought broths are very salty. The recipe calls for salt additions, and combined with salty broth, the total is excessive.

Fix

Why it happens

Fix Use unsalted broth when possible, which gives you complete control over salt content. If using salted broth, reduce the added salt by approximately half and taste before serving, adjusting based on your preference. Unsalted broth allows precise seasoning and prevents the common over-salted result.

Variations

Creamy Chicken with Mushrooms and Thyme

Add 8 oz (225g) of sliced cremini or baby bella mushrooms to the pot when sautéing the broccoli (step 4). The mushrooms will cook in the same time frame as the broccoli. Increase the dried thyme to 1/2 teaspoon (1g) to complement the mushrooms' earthy flavor. Proceed with the remaining recipe identically. The mushrooms' umami compounds enhance the savory depth significantly.

Note: Mushrooms are approximately 90% water. As they cook, they release water into the surrounding liquid, which can thin the sauce slightly if not accounted for. If you prefer a thicker final sauce consistency, reduce the chicken broth amount by 2 tablespoons (30ml) to compensate. The mushrooms' umami-rich compounds (specifically glutamates) enhance the savory perception of the entire dish.

Creamy Chicken with Sun-Dried Tomatoes and Spinach

Replace the broccoli with 2 cups (60g) of fresh spinach and add 1/4 cup (30g) of sun-dried tomatoes (packed in oil, chopped). Add the spinach and sun-dried tomatoes along with the broth in step 5. The spinach will wilt during the simmering phase. Use 1/2 teaspoon (1g) of Italian seasoning instead of thyme. Proceed with the remaining recipe identically.

Note: Fresh spinach reduces significantly during cooking—2 cups raw spinach becomes roughly 1/4 cup of cooked spinach. The sun-dried tomatoes' oil content affects fat balance slightly, so you may reduce the final butter mounting to 1/2 tablespoon (7.5g) if you prefer a lighter final result. The tomatoes' concentrated flavor and slight acidity balance the cream's richness effectively.

Creamy Lemon Chicken

Add the zest of 1 large lemon (approximately 1 tablespoon / 5g) and juice of 1/2 lemon (approximately 1.5 tablespoons / 22ml) to the cream just before folding it in (step 8). The lemon zest provides bright, aromatic flavor while the juice adds acidity that balances the cream's richness. Reduce the nutmeg to 1/8 teaspoon since lemon provides its own brightness. Proceed with the remaining recipe identically.

Note: Lemon juice's citric acid is a mild emulsifier, which actually helps stabilize the sauce and makes it more stable than cream-only versions. The acid cuts through the richness and creates balance. Add lemon juice gradually—too much makes the sauce taste sour rather than bright. Start with juice of 1/2 lemon and add additional juice to taste after cooking.

Creamy Dijon Chicken with Herbs

Stir 2 tablespoons (30g) of whole-grain Dijon mustard into the cream just before folding it in (step 8). The mustard adds subtle tang and mild spice that complements chicken beautifully. Increase the fresh parsley garnish to 2 tablespoons (16g) and add 1/2 teaspoon (0.5g) of fresh tarragon if available. Proceed with the remaining recipe identically. The mustard also strengthens emulsion stability.

Note: Dijon mustard contains natural emulsifiers (from mustard seed) that actually strengthen the sauce's emulsion, making it more stable and less prone to breaking. The mustard flavor should be subtle rather than prominent—the 2 tablespoons (30g) provides a perceptible tang without dominating. If you prefer stronger mustard flavor, increase up to 3 tablespoons (45g).

Creamy Chicken with Prosciutto and Peas

Add 4 oz (115g) of diced prosciutto to the pot when searing the chicken (it will cook with the chicken cubes), and replace the broccoli with 1.5 cups (180g) of fresh or frozen peas. The peas cook in the same time frame as broccoli (approximately 8-10 minutes of simmering). The prosciutto adds savory depth and umami richness. Since prosciutto is salty, reduce the direct salt additions by half (use approximately 1/8 teaspoon total instead of 1/2 teaspoon).

Note: Prosciutto releases fat and salt into the cooking liquid as it cooks. This fat contributes to the emulsion and enriches the sauce. The salt concentration increases as prosciutto renders, so reducing direct salt additions prevents over-salting. Peas are tender vegetables that cook in less time than broccoli—if cooking together with broccoli, add peas only in the final 3-5 minutes of simmering to prevent them from becoming mushy.

Storage & Tips

Refrigerator

Store completely cooled creamy chicken in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The sauce may separate or break slightly during cooling and storage—this is expected and normal. The emulsion becomes less stable when cold because the fats firm up and milk solids lose some emulsifying ability. The sauce will re-emulsify when reheated gently.

Freezer

Creamy chicken freezes well for up to 2 months in airtight containers, leaving approximately 1 inch (2.5cm) of headspace for expansion. The sauce texture may be slightly less silky after freezing because the emulsion breaks slightly during the freeze-thaw cycle. However, this is minor and often unnoticeable after reheating. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating.

Reheating

Reheat gently on the stovetop over low to medium-low heat. Place the cooled creamy chicken in a pot and stir gently, adding a splash of chicken broth or cream (2-3 tablespoons / 30-45ml) if the sauce seems thick. The added liquid helps re-emulsify the sauce as it warms. Heat gently without boiling, which takes approximately 5-10 minutes depending on quantity. Alternatively, reheat in the oven at 325°F (163°C) covered for 15-20 minutes, stirring halfway through. The stovetop method allows you to monitor and adjust sauce consistency.

Make Ahead

The dish can be prepared through step 6 (after the initial simmering is complete but before adding cream) up to 8 hours ahead. Cool the cooked chicken and broccoli completely and refrigerate in an airtight container. The next day, reheat gently until simmering, then proceed with adding the room-temperature cream (step 7) as directed. This approach saves time on serving day by completing the component requiring the most active cooking attention early.

FAQ

My cream sauce separated and looks greasy instead of creamy. What happened?
The emulsion broke, likely from either cold cream being added to the pot or excessive stirring after the cream was added. To fix a broken sauce, place a splash of room-temperature broth in a clean pan, warm it gently, and whisk the broken sauce into it very slowly while whisking constantly. This re-emulsifies the broken sauce. For next time, ensure cream is room temperature and fold gently rather than stirring vigorously.
The chicken turned out dry and tough even though it reached 165°F internally. What went wrong?
The cooking temperature was too high (rolling boil rather than gentle simmer), or the chicken cooked longer than necessary. Even though 165°F is safe, chicken cooked at a boil becomes drier than chicken at a gentle simmer because high heat causes excessive protein contraction. Check that you're maintaining a gentle simmer (small bubbles breaking the surface) rather than vigorous boiling. Check doneness at 8 minutes rather than waiting the full 10-12 minutes.
My sauce is too thin and watery. How can I fix it?
Place the pot over medium-low heat and simmer gently, uncovered, for 3-5 minutes to allow water to evaporate and the sauce to thicken. Avoid boiling, which can break the cream. If the sauce is still thin after 5 minutes, create a slurry by mixing 1/2 tablespoon (4g) of cornstarch with 1 tablespoon (15ml) of cold broth, then whisk it into the simmering sauce. The sauce will thicken within 1-2 minutes.
Can I use chicken thighs instead of chicken breast?
Yes. Thighs contain more fat and collagen, remaining moist even when cooked slightly longer. You may reduce cooking time slightly in step 6 (check at 6-8 minutes instead of 8-10) because thigh meat tends to cook faster. The final dish will be richer and more flavorful due to higher fat content. The emulsion will be even more stable because thigh fat contributes emulsifying compounds.
What if I don't have heavy cream? Can I use half-and-half or whole milk?
Half-and-half can be substituted at a 1:1 ratio, but the sauce will be less rich and less creamy. Whole milk is not recommended as a direct substitute because it contains insufficient fat for proper emulsification and silky texture. If you must use milk, reduce the amount to 1/2 cup (120ml) and add 2 tablespoons (30ml) of butter to increase fat content.
How far ahead can I prepare this dish?
You can prepare the cooked chicken and broccoli (through step 6) up to 8 hours ahead and refrigerate. Reheat gently the next day and add the cream to finish. The sauce is best made fresh because the emulsion is most stable immediately after preparation. Finished creamy chicken stores for 3 days refrigerated and can be frozen up to 2 months.
The recipe serves 4, but I need 6-8 servings. How do I scale it?
Scale all ingredient quantities by 1.5x for 6 servings or 2x for 8 servings. Cooking technique remains the same. You may need to increase simmering time slightly (check doneness at the lower end of the time range and add additional minutes if needed) because larger volume takes longer to heat through. Use a larger pot to avoid crowding the chicken during searing.
