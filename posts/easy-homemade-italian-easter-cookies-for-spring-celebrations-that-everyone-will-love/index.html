Easy Homemade Italian Easter Cookies for Spring Celebrations That Everyone Will Love

Molly Dupont
Published on April 7, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
45 min Total Time
24 Servings
Easy Difficulty

These soft Italian Easter cookies are a springtime classic, bursting with citrus zest and a hint of anise. They're easy to make and perfect for sharing at family gatherings or festive brunches. You'll love how tender and fragrant they turn out!

Pro Tips

Make sure your butter is softened to room temperature before creaming.
Softened butter creams more easily with sugar, trapping air that helps create a light, tender cookie. If the butter is too cold, it won’t blend well, resulting in dense dough and less fluffy cookies.
Sift your flour and leavening agents together before adding.
Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking powder and baking soda. This prevents lumps and ensures the cookies rise evenly, giving a consistent texture throughout.
Don’t overmix the dough once you add the flour.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which can make cookies tough and chewy instead of soft. Mix just until the flour disappears to keep the texture tender.
Use fresh citrus zest for the best flavor.
The oils in fresh zest are volatile and provide a bright, aromatic note that dried zest or extracts can’t replicate. This freshness really makes the cookies pop with spring flavors.
Chill the dough briefly if it feels too sticky to handle.
Chilling firms up the dough, making it easier to scoop and shape. It also helps control spreading during baking, so the cookies keep a nice shape and texture.
Bake cookies until edges are just golden, centers still soft.
This ensures the cookies are fully cooked but remain tender and moist inside. Overbaking dries them out, losing that signature soft texture.
Dust with powdered sugar only after cookies have fully cooled.
If you dust while warm, the sugar can melt and become sticky instead of creating a pretty, delicate coating. Cooling preserves the powdery finish.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using melted butter instead of softened butter

Why it happens Some cooks think melted butter will make mixing easier or the cookies moister, but this changes the dough’s texture.

Fix Always use butter softened to room temperature, not melted. Softened butter creams with sugar to trap air, which is essential for a tender crumb. Melted butter leads to dense, flat cookies.

Overmixing after adding flour

Why it happens In a rush to combine ingredients, it's easy to keep mixing until the dough feels smooth, but this develops gluten.

Fix Mix just until the flour disappears. A few streaks of flour are okay. This keeps the cookies soft and tender rather than tough.

Skipping the sifting step

Why it happens Sifting is sometimes seen as an unnecessary extra step.

Fix Don’t skip it! Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes leavening, preventing lumps and ensuring a light texture.

Baking at too high a temperature

Why it happens Some ovens run hot or cooks increase heat to speed baking.

Fix Stick to 350°F. Too hot and the cookies brown too fast on the edges but remain raw inside, ruining texture.

Dusting powdered sugar too early

Why it happens Impatience leads to dusting warm cookies.

Fix Wait until cookies are completely cool before dusting to keep the sugar powdery and pretty.

Variations

Almond Italian Easter Cookies

Swap half the flour for finely ground almond flour and add 1/2 teaspoon almond extract for a nutty twist. These cookies will be slightly denser but have a lovely moist texture and rich flavor.

Note: Almond flour absorbs moisture differently, so reduce milk slightly to keep dough manageable.

Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies

Use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend in place of regular flour. Add 1/4 teaspoon xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t contain it to help with structure. The cookies will stay soft but may be a bit more crumbly.

Note: Mix dough gently and chill before baking to help maintain shape and texture.

Lemon-Only Zest Cookies

Skip the orange zest and double the lemon zest for a bright, tangy flavor. This variation highlights the fresh citrus and pairs well with a lemon glaze drizzle after baking.

Note: Lemon zest is more acidic, so watch dough texture and add a splash more milk if too dry.

Anise Seed Italian Easter Cookies

Instead of anise extract, toast 1 tablespoon of whole anise seeds and grind them finely to add to the dough. This gives a more rustic, aromatic flavor and a slight crunch.

Note: Toasting intensifies flavor; grind seeds finely to avoid gritty texture.

Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies

After baking and cooling, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate. Let them set on parchment paper for a decadent finish. This adds a rich contrast to the citrus and anise flavors.

Note: Use good-quality chocolate and cool cookies completely before dipping to prevent melting.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat sources to prevent them from becoming too soft or sticky. Covering well helps maintain their fresh, tender texture.

Refrigerator

You can refrigerate the cookies in an airtight container for up to a week. Note that refrigeration may slightly firm them up; bring to room temperature before serving for best softness and flavor.

Freezer

Freeze the cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe bag or container for up to 3 months. Wrap tightly to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before serving to restore their tender texture.

Reheating

If you want to warm the cookies, heat them in a 300°F oven for 5 minutes or microwave briefly (10-15 seconds). Avoid overheating to prevent drying out. A light sprinkle of powdered sugar after warming freshens their appearance.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the dough 1-2 days in advance and keep it wrapped tightly in the fridge. Shape and bake the cookies on the day you plan to serve them for the freshest taste and texture.

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract with something else?
Yes! If you don’t have anise extract, you can use fennel seeds ground finely or even a small amount of star anise powder. Keep in mind the flavor will be slightly different but still complementary. Avoid using too much to prevent overpowering the cookies.
Why are my cookies turning out dry?
Dry cookies usually result from overbaking or using too much flour. Make sure to measure flour correctly (spoon and level) and bake just until edges are lightly golden. Also, don’t skip the milk, which adds moisture to the dough.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
You can try substituting eggs with flax eggs (1 tablespoon ground flaxseed + 3 tablespoons water per egg) and use a vegan butter alternative. The texture might be slightly different, but it should still work well with these adjustments.
What’s the best way to store leftover cookies?
Store them in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. For longer storage, freeze them in a sealed container or bag for up to 3 months. Thaw at room temperature before eating.
Can I add nuts to this recipe?
Absolutely! Chopped almonds or walnuts can be folded into the dough for extra texture and flavor. Toasting the nuts first enhances their nuttiness. Just be mindful that nuts add crunch and may change the soft texture slightly.
My cookies spread too much and are flat. Why?
This can happen if the dough is too warm or if too much butter is used. Chill the dough before baking to help cookies hold their shape. Also, check your oven temperature with an oven thermometer to ensure it’s accurate.
Can I use dried citrus peel instead of fresh zest?
Fresh zest is preferred because it contains natural oils that give bright flavor and aroma. Dried peel lacks this intensity and can make the cookies less flavorful and slightly dry.
How do I get a nice powdered sugar coating?
Make sure cookies are completely cooled before dusting with powdered sugar. Use a fine sieve and dust evenly. If you want a thicker coating, dust once, let it settle, then dust again.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies

  • 3 1/2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 3 large eggs
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1 tablespoon anise extract
  • Zest of 1 lemon
  • Zest of 1 orange
  • 1/4 cup milk
  • Powdered sugar, for dusting

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C) and lining baking sheets with parchment paper. This ensures the cookies bake evenly and don’t stick. While the oven warms up, sift together the flour, baking powder, and baking soda in a bowl. Sifting helps aerate the flour and evenly distributes the leavening agents, which prevents dense or uneven cookies. It also helps avoid lumps that could create uneven texture in the dough. Set this bowl aside for later.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flour mixture is light, airy, and free of lumps
Step 2
In a large mixing bowl, cream the softened butter and granulated sugar together using a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed. Beat until the mixture is pale, fluffy, and smooth—this usually takes about 3 to 5 minutes. Creaming incorporates air into the butter, which helps create a tender crumb in the cookies. If you rush this step, the cookies can turn out dense and heavy, so take your time here.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter and sugar mixture is light in color and fluffy in texture
Step 3
Add the eggs one at a time to the creamed butter and sugar, beating well after each addition. This helps emulsify the mixture, ensuring the ingredients combine smoothly and the dough has a consistent texture. Then stir in the anise extract, lemon zest, and orange zest. These aromatics are key to the cookie’s signature flavor, so don’t skip them!
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture looks smooth and fragrant with evenly distributed zest
Step 4
Gradually add the sifted flour mixture to the wet ingredients, alternating with the milk. Start and end with the flour mixture, mixing gently after each addition. This technique prevents overmixing, which can develop gluten and make the cookies tough. Mix just until combined; a few streaks of flour are okay. The dough should be soft but not sticky.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough is soft, slightly sticky, and holds together well
Step 5
Using a small cookie scoop or tablespoon, drop rounded balls of dough onto the prepared baking sheets, spacing them about 2 inches apart. This spacing allows the cookies room to spread slightly without merging. If the dough feels too soft to shape, chill it for 10 minutes to firm up.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookie dough balls are uniform in size and spaced evenly
Step 6
Bake the cookies in the preheated oven for 12 to 15 minutes, or until the edges just start to turn golden but the centers remain soft. Watch carefully after 10 minutes to avoid overbaking, which can dry out the cookies. The right baking time ensures a tender, melt-in-your-mouth texture.
⏱ Time: 12-15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Edges are lightly golden, centers look set but soft
Step 7
Remove the baking sheets from the oven and let the cookies cool on the sheet for 5 minutes before transferring them to a wire rack. This resting time allows the cookies to firm up slightly, making them easier to move without breaking. Cooling on a wire rack prevents sogginess by letting air circulate around the cookies.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies are firm enough to handle without crumbling
Step 8
Once completely cooled, dust the cookies generously with powdered sugar. This adds a delicate sweetness and a pretty finishing touch that’s traditional for Italian Easter cookies. Use a fine sieve for an even dusting.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies have a light, even coating of powdered sugar
Step 9
Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. They’re best enjoyed fresh but will keep their softness and flavor well for a couple of days. If you want to keep them longer, freezing is a good option (see storage tips).
⏱ Time: N/A
✓ Doneness: Cookies remain soft and fragrant after storage
