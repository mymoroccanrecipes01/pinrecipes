Moist 5 Ingredient Chocolate Cake with Ganache Frosting That Melts in Your Mouth

Molly Dupont Published on May 30, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 8 servings

This chocolate cake is insanely moist and super simple with just five ingredients. The silky ganache frosting is the secret that takes it over the top. Perfect for birthdays or any sweet craving!

Why This Works

The secret to this cake’s moistness lies in the balance of fat, liquid, and leavening. Using melted butter, rather than creamed, coats the flour proteins with fat, which limits gluten formation and keeps the crumb tender. The whole milk adds moisture and richness, while room temperature eggs help emulsify the batter, creating a smooth texture that traps air for lift.

Sifting the cocoa powder and flour not only breaks up lumps but also aerates the dry ingredients, which contributes to a lighter cake. The baking powder provides the necessary rise by releasing carbon dioxide bubbles during baking, which expand and create a soft crumb. Folding the wet and dry ingredients gently ensures you don’t overdevelop gluten, which would make the cake tough.

The ganache frosting is a simple emulsion of fat and chocolate. Heating the cream just to a simmer melts the chocolate gently, allowing it to blend into a glossy, silky mixture. The fat in the cream gives the ganache its smooth texture and sheen, while the chocolate provides intense flavor. Pouring the ganache over a cooled cake allows it to set with a beautiful shine and luscious mouthfeel.

Each step in this recipe is designed to maximize moisture retention and flavor. From ingredient temperature to mixing technique, everything works together to produce a cake that’s moist, tender, and rich with a decadent ganache finish.

  • Choose a good quality, unbleached all-purpose flour with moderate protein content (around 10-11%). This ensures enough gluten to give structure without making the cake dense. Sifting before use is important to aerate and remove lumps, which helps keep the crumb light.
  • Use natural unsweetened cocoa powder for a bright chocolate flavor and deep color. It’s important to sift it to avoid clumps, which can cause uneven texture. Avoid Dutch-processed cocoa here, as it can affect the leavening reaction with baking powder.
  • Use unsalted butter melted to coat the flour proteins and add richness. Salted butter can alter the flavor balance, so it’s better to control salt separately if needed. Melting butter also helps incorporate it evenly into the batter, contributing to moistness.
  • Room temperature eggs emulsify better with the other ingredients, helping trap air and moisture. Cold eggs can cause the batter to curdle or mix unevenly, affecting texture. Eggs also provide structure and richness.
  • For the ganache, use fresh heavy cream with at least 36% fat content. The fat is crucial for a smooth, creamy texture and shiny finish. Lower-fat creams won’t set properly and can result in a grainy texture.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 cup whole milk, room temperature
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, melted
  • 2 large eggs, room temperature
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • For ganache frosting:
  • 1 cup heavy cream
  • 8 ounces semi-sweet chocolate, chopped

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease and flour an 8-inch round cake pan or line it with parchment paper. This step is crucial because it prevents the cake from sticking and ensures an even rise. Make sure to cover every corner and tap out excess flour so the cake surface stays smooth. Skipping this can cause your cake to tear when unmolding.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Pan is evenly coated with no bare spots or clumps of flour
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, sift together the flour, sugar, cocoa powder, and baking powder. Sifting breaks up any lumps and aerates the dry ingredients, which helps create a lighter, more tender crumb. Mixing these thoroughly ensures even distribution of leavening agents, so your cake rises uniformly.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are well combined with no visible clumps
  3. Step 3

    In a separate bowl, whisk the eggs, melted butter, milk, and vanilla extract until smooth. Using room temperature ingredients helps the batter come together better and prevents it from curdling when combined with the dry mix. Whisking well also incorporates some air, contributing to a lighter texture.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Wet ingredients are homogenous and slightly frothy
  4. Step 4

    Slowly pour the wet ingredients into the dry ingredients while gently folding with a spatula. Avoid overmixing to prevent gluten development, which would make the cake dense and tough. Fold just until you see no streaks of flour. The batter should be smooth and slightly thick but pourable.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter is uniform in color and texture without lumps
  5. Step 5

    Pour the batter into your prepared pan and gently tap it on the counter to release any trapped air bubbles. This helps avoid large holes in the crumb and ensures even baking. Smooth the top with a spatula for an even surface.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Batter surface is level and smooth
  6. Step 6

    Bake for 25 to 30 minutes, or until a toothpick inserted in the center comes out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter. Oven temperatures vary, so start checking at 25 minutes. Overbaking dries the cake out, while underbaking leaves it gooey inside.

    ⏱ 25-30 minutes✓ Toothpick comes out mostly clean with a few moist crumbs
  7. Step 7

    Remove the cake from the oven and let it cool in the pan for 10 minutes. This resting time lets the structure set so the cake won’t crumble when you remove it. Then invert onto a wire rack to cool completely. Ganache spreads best on a fully cooled cake.

    ⏱ 40 minutes✓ Cake is cool to the touch and firm
  8. Step 8

    To make the ganache, heat the heavy cream in a small saucepan over medium heat until it just begins to simmer. Pour it over the chopped chocolate in a heatproof bowl. Let it sit for 2 minutes to melt the chocolate, then stir gently until smooth and glossy. This method ensures an even, silky ganache without overheating the chocolate.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Ganache is shiny, smooth, and pourable
  9. Step 9

    Pour the ganache evenly over the cooled cake, using a spatula to gently spread it to the edges. Let it set at room temperature for about 30 minutes or refrigerate briefly to firm up. The ganache should form a shiny, smooth coating that’s rich but not too thick.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Ganache is set with a glossy finish and slight firmness

Pro Tips

  • Always use room temperature eggs and milk. — Room temperature ingredients blend more smoothly and emulsify better, leading to a uniform batter and lighter cake texture. Cold ingredients can cause the butter to seize or the batter to curdle, resulting in a dense or uneven crumb.
  • Sift your dry ingredients before mixing. — Sifting aerates the flour and cocoa powder, helping to prevent lumps and promoting a lighter, fluffier cake. It also ensures even distribution of baking powder, which is critical for proper rise.
  • Don’t overmix the batter once wet and dry ingredients combine. — Overmixing develops gluten, which makes the cake tough and chewy instead of tender. Fold gently just until combined to preserve a soft crumb.
  • Use a toothpick to check doneness but expect a few moist crumbs. — Pulling the cake too early leaves raw batter inside, but waiting too long dries it out. A toothpick with a few moist crumbs means the cake is perfectly baked and will stay moist.
  • Let the cake cool completely before applying ganache. — Applying ganache on a warm cake causes it to melt and slide off. Cooling allows the ganache to set properly, forming that shiny, smooth finish.
  • Heat cream just until it simmers for ganache. — Overheating cream can cause it to boil and separate, which ruins the smooth texture. Just simmering melts the chocolate gently, creating a glossy emulsion.
  • Tap the cake pan gently after pouring batter. — Tapping releases trapped air bubbles that can cause holes or tunnels in the cake, ensuring a fine, even crumb.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping sifting of dry ingredients
    Why: Many cooks assume sifting is unnecessary or time-consuming, so they mix dry ingredients directly.
    Fix: Make it a habit to sift flour, cocoa, and baking powder to remove lumps and aerate the mixture. This prevents clumps and promotes an even rise and tender crumb.
  • Using cold eggs or milk
    Why: It’s easy to forget to take ingredients out ahead of time, especially when baking spontaneously.
    Fix: Plan ahead and bring eggs and milk to room temperature before mixing. This ensures smooth emulsification and prevents batter curdling.
  • Overmixing the batter
    Why: Some think mixing longer makes a smoother batter, but it actually develops gluten.
    Fix: Mix just until combined. Use gentle folding motions to keep the batter light and avoid toughness.
  • Baking at incorrect temperature
    Why: Oven temperatures vary and some don’t preheat properly or use inaccurate settings.
    Fix: Use an oven thermometer to verify temperature. Bake at 350°F and start checking doneness at 25 minutes to avoid drying out.
  • Applying ganache on warm cake
    Why: Impatience or lack of cooling space leads to frosting a warm cake.
    Fix: Wait until the cake is completely cool to the touch before pouring ganache. This prevents melting and slipping.

Variations

  • Add Espresso Powder for Deeper Chocolate Flavor: Stir 1 teaspoon of instant espresso powder into the dry ingredients. It enhances the chocolate notes without making the cake taste like coffee. Expect a richer, more complex flavor that pairs beautifully with the ganache.
    No change in baking time or texture; just mix espresso powder with cocoa and flour.
  • Swap Ganache for Whipped Cream Frosting: For a lighter topping, replace ganache with fresh whipped cream sweetened with a touch of powdered sugar and vanilla. This gives a fluffy contrast to the moist cake and makes it less rich.
    Whipped cream frosting is less stable; best served same day and kept refrigerated.
  • Make It Gluten-Free: Use a 1:1 gluten-free baking flour blend instead of all-purpose flour. The cake will be just as moist but may have a slightly different crumb texture. Ensure your baking powder is gluten-free.
    No change in liquid ratios; watch baking time closely as gluten-free flours can brown faster.
  • Add Fresh Berries Between Layers: Slice the cake horizontally and spread fresh raspberries or strawberries between layers before frosting with ganache. It adds a bright, tart contrast to the rich chocolate and makes it extra special.
    Use slightly thicker ganache to hold berries in place and prevent sliding.
  • Use Dark Chocolate Ganache for Intense Flavor: Swap semi-sweet chocolate for 70% dark chocolate in the ganache for a more bittersweet finish. This pairs well if you prefer less sweetness and a more adult flavor profile.
    Dark chocolate ganache may set firmer; allow slightly longer to soften before spreading.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: The cake can be kept covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Make sure it’s in an airtight container or wrapped well to prevent drying out. Ganache frosting helps seal in moisture, so it stays soft.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store the cake in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 5 days. The ganache will firm up and the cake may become a bit denser. Bring to room temperature before serving for best texture and flavor.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cake tightly in plastic wrap and foil, then freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge and then bring to room temperature. Ganache may lose some shine but will remain delicious.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat slices gently in the microwave for 10-15 seconds to soften ganache if chilled. Avoid overheating which can melt frosting unevenly. Alternatively, serve at room temperature for best experience.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can bake the cake layers 1-2 days ahead and store wrapped at room temperature. Make ganache the day of serving for freshest shine. Assemble and frost just before serving to keep ganache glossy.

FAQ

Can I use oil instead of butter in this cake?
You can substitute melted oil for butter, but the flavor and texture will change slightly. Butter adds richness and a tender crumb, while oil makes the cake moist but less flavorful. If using oil, choose a neutral one like vegetable or canola and keep the quantity the same.
What if I don’t have heavy cream for ganache?
Heavy cream is key for the smooth, glossy texture of ganache. If unavailable, you can try full-fat coconut milk as a dairy-free alternative, but the flavor will differ. Avoid low-fat milk as it won’t create the right consistency.
How do I store leftover ganache?
Ganache can be stored in an airtight container in the fridge for up to a week. Reheat gently in a microwave or double boiler before using again. Stir well to restore its smooth texture.
Can I make this cake vegan?
This recipe relies on eggs, butter, and cream, so it’s not vegan as is. You could experiment with egg replacers and plant-based butter and cream alternatives, but the texture and flavor will differ. For a vegan chocolate cake, look for recipes specifically developed for that.
Why is my cake dry sometimes?
Dryness usually comes from overbaking or overmixing. Check your oven temperature with a thermometer and start testing for doneness early. Also, mix the batter just until combined to avoid tough gluten development.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, doubling works well. Use two pans to avoid a thick batter layer that won’t bake evenly. Adjust baking time slightly and test doneness with a toothpick.
How do I get a smooth ganache finish?
Pour warm ganache over a completely cooled cake and spread gently with a spatula. Let it set at room temperature without disturbance. Avoid chilling immediately to keep the glossy finish.
What’s the best way to slice this cake cleanly?
Use a sharp knife warmed in hot water and wiped dry before slicing. Cut with a gentle sawing motion to avoid tearing the ganache or crumb.
