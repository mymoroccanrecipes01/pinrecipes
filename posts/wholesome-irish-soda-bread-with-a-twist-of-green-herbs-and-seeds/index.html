Wholesome Irish Soda Bread with a Twist of Green Herbs and Seeds

Molly Dupont Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 40 min cook 8 servings

This hearty Irish soda bread is packed with fresh herbs and nutty seeds, making it a fresh take on a classic. It’s quick to whip up and perfect for St. Paddy’s Day brunch or any cozy weekend breakfast.

Introduction

Irish soda bread has always been one of those comforting breads I turn to when I want something rustic and quick without the fuss of yeast. I first tried making it years ago when my Irish friend invited me over for a St. Patrick’s Day celebration. Her version was simple but delicious, and I wanted to put my own spin on it. Adding fresh herbs like parsley and chives gives the bread a bright, fresh flavor that cuts through the dense crumb. Plus, the seeds add a lovely crunch and nuttiness that makes each bite more interesting. I’ve found that using cold buttermilk helps activate the baking soda just right, so the bread rises beautifully with a tender crumb. It’s perfect served warm with butter or alongside a hearty stew. This bread is a little different from the usual soda bread you see because it feels lighter and fresher, making it a crowd-pleaser even for those who don’t usually love dense breads. I hope you enjoy baking and sharing this one as much as I do!

Why This Works

Irish soda bread relies on a chemical leavening reaction rather than yeast fermentation. The baking soda reacts with the acidity in the buttermilk to produce carbon dioxide bubbles, which cause the dough to rise quickly. This reaction is temperature-sensitive, which is why cold buttermilk and prompt baking are essential. If the mixture sits too long before baking, the gas escapes and the bread won’t rise properly.

The choice of flour affects texture significantly. Using all-purpose flour provides enough gluten to hold the structure, but since the dough is minimally kneaded, the bread remains tender rather than chewy. Overworking the dough develops gluten strands that make the bread tough, so gentle handling is key.

Incorporating fresh herbs like parsley and chives adds moisture and a vibrant flavor that cuts through the dense crumb. The seeds contribute not only texture but also a toasty nuttiness, complementing the bread’s rustic character. Brushing with melted butter before baking promotes a golden crust through the Maillard reaction, which enhances flavor and mouthfeel.

The scoring of the loaf is both functional and traditional. It allows steam to escape, preventing cracks, and helps the heat penetrate the center evenly. The result is a loaf with a crisp crust and soft, flavorful crumb that’s perfect for slicing and sharing.

  • Look for a good-quality all-purpose flour with moderate protein content (around 10-11%). This balance ensures enough gluten development for structure without making the bread tough. Avoid self-rising flour here because it already contains leavening agents that can interfere with the soda and buttermilk reaction.
  • Use fresh baking soda for best results; old or expired baking soda loses its potency and won’t produce enough rise. Baking soda is the leavening agent that reacts with the acidic buttermilk to create carbon dioxide bubbles, which help the bread rise quickly without yeast.
  • Traditional buttermilk is acidic and helps activate the baking soda. If you can’t find buttermilk, you can make a substitute by adding 1 tablespoon of lemon juice or white vinegar to 1 1/2 cups of milk and letting it sit for 5-10 minutes. Cold buttermilk slows the reaction, giving you time to shape the dough before baking.
  • Fresh parsley and chives add brightness and a subtle oniony flavor that balances the bread’s richness. Using fresh herbs rather than dried ensures the flavors are vibrant and not overpowering. Chop finely so they distribute evenly without creating large pockets of moisture.
  • A combination of pumpkin, sunflower, and sesame seeds adds a pleasant crunch and nutty flavor. Seeds should be raw or lightly toasted for best texture. They also contribute healthy fats and nutrients, elevating the bread from simple to special.

Ingredients

  • 3 1/2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 2 tablespoons sugar
  • 1 1/2 cups buttermilk, cold
  • 1/4 cup fresh parsley, finely chopped
  • 2 tablespoons fresh chives, finely chopped
  • 2 tablespoons mixed seeds (pumpkin, sunflower, sesame)
  • 1 tablespoon melted butter, for brushing

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 425°F (220°C) and line a baking sheet with parchment paper. In a large mixing bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking soda, salt, and sugar. This step is crucial because evenly distributing the baking soda ensures the bread rises uniformly. Sifting helps aerate the flour, resulting in a lighter crumb. Avoid skipping sifting, as it can cause lumps and uneven texture.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Dry, evenly mixed flour mixture with no clumps
  2. Step 2

    Add the chopped parsley, chives, and mixed seeds to the dry ingredients and stir well to combine. The herbs will infuse the bread with a fresh aroma, while the seeds add texture. Mixing them in now ensures even distribution throughout the dough, so every slice has a bit of green and crunch.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Evenly speckled dry mix with herbs and seeds
  3. Step 3

    Make a well in the center of the dry ingredients and pour in the cold buttermilk. Using a wooden spoon or your hand, gently mix until a sticky dough forms. Don’t overmix here; overworking the dough can make the bread tough. The buttermilk reacts with baking soda to create bubbles that help the bread rise quickly without yeast.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Sticky, shaggy dough that holds together but is not smooth
  4. Step 4

    Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface and gently knead it just 3-4 times to bring it together. The trick is to handle it lightly — too much kneading will develop gluten and make it chewy rather than tender. Shape the dough into a round loaf about 7 inches in diameter.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Rounded dough ball that’s cohesive but still soft
  5. Step 5

    Place the loaf onto the prepared baking sheet. Using a sharp knife, score a deep cross about 1/2 inch deep on top of the dough. This traditional step helps the bread bake evenly and allows steam to escape, so you get a nice crust without cracks. It’s also a fun nod to Irish heritage.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Clearly scored cross on dough surface
  6. Step 6

    Brush the top of the loaf lightly with melted butter to encourage a golden crust. This step also adds a subtle richness to the flavor. Avoid brushing too heavily, as it can weigh down the crust and make it soggy.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Even sheen of butter on dough surface
  7. Step 7

    Bake in the preheated oven for about 35-40 minutes, until the crust is deep golden brown and the loaf sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom. The high temperature helps set the crust quickly, trapping moisture inside for a soft interior. Don’t open the oven door too often, or the temperature will drop and affect rising.

    ⏱ 35-40 minutes✓ Golden brown crust with hollow sound on tapping
  8. Step 8

    Remove the bread from the oven and transfer it to a wire rack to cool for at least 30 minutes before slicing. This resting time lets the crumb set properly. Cutting too soon can make the bread gummy or crumbly. Patience here really pays off.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Loaf cooled to room temperature with set crumb
  9. Step 9

    Serve warm or at room temperature with butter, jam, or alongside soups and stews. The fresh herbs and seeds make this bread versatile for many meals. Leftovers toast beautifully, bringing out the nutty flavors even more.

    ⏱ 0 minutes✓ Warm, fragrant slices ready to enjoy

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your flour and baking soda together. — Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking soda, which is crucial for uniform rising. Without sifting, you risk clumps of baking soda that can cause bitter spots or uneven texture.
  • Use cold buttermilk straight from the fridge. — Cold buttermilk slows down the chemical reaction with baking soda, giving you more time to mix and shape the dough before it starts to rise. Warm buttermilk can cause premature rising and a dense loaf.
  • Don’t overknead the dough. — Minimal kneading prevents too much gluten development, which keeps the bread tender and crumbly rather than tough and chewy. Gentle handling is key to the right texture.
  • Score the loaf deeply before baking. — Scoring allows steam to escape and helps the bread expand evenly. It prevents random cracks and creates that classic rustic look.
  • Brush the loaf with melted butter before baking. — This encourages a beautiful golden crust through the Maillard reaction, adding flavor and a slight richness to the bread’s exterior.
  • Cool the bread completely before slicing. — Resting lets the crumb set properly. Cutting too soon can make the bread gummy or crumbly because the interior hasn’t finished firming up.
  • Use a baking stone or preheated sheet if possible. — A hot surface helps the bread rise quickly from the bottom, promoting a crisp crust and even bake. If you don’t have one, a parchment-lined baking sheet works fine.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using expired baking soda
    Why: Many home cooks don’t check the freshness of their baking soda, assuming it’s always good. Over time, baking soda loses its leavening power.
    Fix: Test baking soda by adding a small pinch to vinegar; it should fizz vigorously. Replace if it doesn’t. Using fresh baking soda ensures the bread rises properly and isn’t dense.
  • Overmixing the dough
    Why: It’s natural to want to knead dough thoroughly, but with soda bread, too much mixing develops gluten, making the bread tough.
    Fix: Mix just until ingredients come together and knead gently 3-4 times. This keeps the crumb tender and light.
  • Not scoring the dough
    Why: Some skip scoring because it seems unnecessary or time-consuming.
    Fix: Use a sharp knife to cut a deep cross on top. It helps the bread expand evenly and prevents cracks.
  • Using warm buttermilk
    Why: Some think warm liquids speed up baking, but here it causes premature reaction.
    Fix: Always use cold buttermilk to slow the reaction and give you time to shape and bake.
  • Cutting bread too soon
    Why: It’s tempting to slice fresh bread immediately, but the crumb needs time to set.
    Fix: Wait at least 30 minutes after baking for the bread to cool completely for best texture.

Variations

  • Cheddar and Chive Soda Bread: Add 1 cup of shredded sharp cheddar cheese to the dough along with the chives for a savory, cheesy twist. It’s great for serving with soups or as a snack.
    Cheese adds moisture and fat, so reduce buttermilk slightly if dough feels too wet. Bake until cheese is melted and crust is golden.
  • Whole Wheat and Oat Soda Bread: Replace half the all-purpose flour with whole wheat and add 1/2 cup rolled oats for a nuttier, heartier loaf. This variation is more filling and has a denser texture.
    Whole wheat absorbs more liquid; you may need to add an extra tablespoon of buttermilk to keep dough moist.
  • Sweet Raisin and Herb Soda Bread: Add 1/2 cup raisins and 1 tablespoon honey to the dough for a subtly sweet bread with herbal notes. Perfect for breakfast or tea time.
    Honey adds sugar which browns faster, so watch the crust to avoid overbaking.
  • Gluten-Free Herb Soda Bread: Use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend and xanthan gum to replace regular flour. The texture will be slightly different but still tasty and herbaceous.
    Gluten-free dough is wetter and more fragile; handle gently and consider baking in a loaf pan for support.
  • Spicy Jalapeño and Herb Soda Bread: Fold in 1-2 finely chopped jalapeños with the herbs and seeds for a spicy kick. Great paired with chili or Mexican dishes.
    Spicy peppers add moisture; reduce buttermilk slightly to keep dough consistency right.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the bread covered loosely with a clean kitchen towel at room temperature for up to 2 days. This keeps the crust from getting too hard while preserving moisture inside.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Keep in an airtight container or wrapped tightly in plastic wrap for up to 5 days. Refrigeration may dry the bread out, so reheat slightly before serving to restore softness.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cooled bread tightly in plastic wrap and then foil, freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw at room temperature wrapped in foil to retain moisture, then warm in the oven to refresh the crust.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat slices in a toaster or oven at 350°F (175°C) for 5-7 minutes. For whole loaf, wrap in foil and warm for 10-15 minutes. Avoid microwave reheating which can make the bread rubbery.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can mix and shape the dough up to 12 hours ahead and refrigerate it covered. Bring to room temperature before baking to ensure even rising and bake as directed.

FAQ

Can I substitute milk for buttermilk?
Yes, but to mimic buttermilk’s acidity, add 1 tablespoon of lemon juice or vinegar per cup of milk and let it sit for 5-10 minutes before using. This acidity activates the baking soda, helping the bread rise properly.
What if my bread turns out dense or flat?
This usually happens if the baking soda is old or if the dough was overmixed or sat too long before baking. Use fresh baking soda, mix gently, and bake immediately after mixing to ensure a good rise.
Can I make this bread vegan?
You can substitute buttermilk with a plant-based milk plus 1 tablespoon lemon juice or vinegar. Use vegan butter or oil for brushing. The texture might be slightly different but still tasty.
How do I know when the bread is done baking?
The crust should be golden brown and the loaf should sound hollow when tapped on the bottom. An internal temperature of about 200°F (93°C) also indicates doneness.
Can I add other herbs or spices?
Absolutely! Rosemary, thyme, or dill work well. Just chop finely and add with the other herbs. Spices like caraway or fennel seeds also complement the bread nicely.
Why do I need to score the bread?
Scoring allows steam to escape and helps the bread expand evenly in the oven. It prevents random cracks and gives the bread a classic rustic look.
Can I freeze leftover bread?
Yes, wrap it tightly in plastic and foil to prevent freezer burn. Thaw wrapped at room temperature and refresh in the oven before serving.
Is it okay if the bread cracks on top?
Some cracking is normal due to steam escaping. Deep scoring helps control where cracks appear, but minor cracks don’t affect taste or texture.
