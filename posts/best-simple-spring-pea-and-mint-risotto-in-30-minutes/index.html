Best Simple Spring Pea and Mint Risotto in 30 Minutes

Molly Dupont Published on May 30, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 4 servings

This bright and creamy risotto bursts with fresh spring peas and fragrant mint, making it a perfect light dinner or side dish. The secret? Adding fresh mint right at the end to keep its vibrant flavor. Ready in just half an hour!

Introduction

I stumbled on this spring pea and mint risotto a few years ago when I wanted something quick, fresh, and comforting to brighten up a chilly evening. Risotto can often feel like a labor of love, but this version is surprisingly fast and simple—perfect for busy weeknights or when you want something special without fuss. What makes this recipe stand out is the use of fresh mint added right at the end, which gives the dish a refreshing lift that pairs beautifully with the sweetness of the peas. I also like to use warm broth and stir gently but steadily to coax the creaminess out of the rice without standing over the stove for too long. The result is a velvety risotto that’s bright green and bursting with flavor, not heavy or stodgy. It’s one of those dishes that feels fancy but is actually pretty forgiving, so even if you’re new to risotto, you’ll have success. Plus, it’s a lovely way to celebrate spring produce or add a pop of color to any meal. I hope you enjoy making this as much as I do!

Why This Works

Risotto’s signature creaminess comes from the unique starch content of Arborio rice, which releases amylopectin as it cooks. This starch thickens the cooking liquid, creating that luscious texture. The gradual addition of warm broth and continuous stirring are essential to coax out this starch without overcooking the rice. Adding all the liquid at once would boil the rice and make it mushy instead of creamy.

The use of fresh peas adds sweetness and a pop of color, while the fresh mint provides a bright herbal note that cuts through the richness. Adding mint at the end preserves its flavor and aroma, which would be lost if cooked too long. The Parmesan cheese and butter enrich the dish, balancing the fresh flavors with a silky finish.

Using warm broth is another key technique. Cold broth would shock the rice and slow cooking, resulting in uneven texture. The wine adds acidity, which brightens the overall flavor and balances the creamy mouthfeel. Skipping the wine is fine but changes the flavor profile slightly.

Overall, the recipe balances creamy, fresh, and bright elements with simple, straightforward technique. It’s approachable even for home cooks new to risotto but yields a restaurant-quality dish when the steps are followed carefully.

  • Arborio rice is the classic choice for risotto because of its high starch content, which creates that creamy texture. Look for short-grain rice labeled specifically as Arborio or risotto rice. Avoid long-grain rice, which won’t release enough starch and will result in a less creamy dish. Store in a cool, dry place and rinse only if the recipe calls for it, as rinsing removes starch.
  • Fresh peas bring natural sweetness and a vibrant green color. If peas aren’t in season, frozen peas are a good substitute but add them later in the cooking process to avoid mushiness. Fresh peas should be bright and firm, not shriveled or dull. Shell them just before cooking for the best flavor.
  • Mint is a delicate herb that adds a refreshing note to the risotto. Use fresh leaves, finely chopped, and add them at the end of cooking to preserve their bright aroma and flavor. Dried mint won’t have the same impact. If you want a stronger mint flavor, increase the amount slightly but be careful not to overpower the dish.
  • Parmesan adds a nutty, salty richness that rounds out the risotto. Use freshly grated Parmigiano-Reggiano for the best flavor and melting quality. Pre-grated cheese often contains anti-caking agents that affect texture. Add cheese off the heat to prevent it from clumping or separating.
  • A good quality vegetable broth enhances the risotto’s flavor without overpowering the delicate pea and mint notes. Use homemade or low-sodium store-bought broth so you can control salt levels. Keep the broth warm during cooking to maintain even temperature and cooking speed.

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon olive oil
  • 1 small onion, finely chopped
  • 2 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 cup Arborio rice
  • 1/2 cup dry white wine (optional)
  • 4 cups vegetable broth, kept warm
  • 1 1/2 cups fresh or frozen peas
  • 1/4 cup fresh mint leaves, chopped
  • 1/2 cup grated Parmesan cheese
  • 2 tablespoons unsalted butter
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Heat the olive oil in a large saucepan over medium heat. Add the finely chopped onion and cook gently for about 5 minutes until translucent but not browned. This slow cooking releases the onion's natural sweetness, which forms a flavorful base for your risotto. Avoid turning up the heat too high, as you want to soften the onion without caramelizing it, which could change the flavor balance.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Onion is soft and translucent, no browning
  2. Step 2

    Add the minced garlic and cook for another minute until fragrant, stirring constantly to prevent burning. Garlic cooks quickly and can develop bitterness if overcooked, so keep an eye on it. This step infuses the oil with garlic flavor, which will permeate the risotto.

    ⏱ 1 minute✓ Garlic aroma is noticeable, no browning
  3. Step 3

    Stir in the Arborio rice, coating the grains with the oil and onion mixture. Toast the rice for about 2 minutes, stirring frequently. This helps the rice absorb flavors and also starts the starch release that creates the creamy texture later. Don’t rush this step; the rice should look slightly translucent at the edges but still opaque in the center.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Rice grains are glossy and slightly translucent at edges
  4. Step 4

    Pour in the white wine if using, and stir until it’s mostly absorbed by the rice. The wine adds acidity and depth of flavor that balances the creaminess. If you skip the wine, start adding broth directly. Letting the alcohol cook off is important to avoid harsh flavors.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Wine is mostly evaporated, rice looks moist
  5. Step 5

    Begin adding the warm vegetable broth one ladle at a time, stirring gently and allowing each addition to be mostly absorbed before adding the next. This gradual absorption is key to coaxing out the rice’s starch and achieving that signature creamy risotto texture. Stirring also prevents sticking and helps even cooking. This process usually takes about 20 minutes.

    ⏱ 20 minutes✓ Rice is tender but still has a slight bite (al dente), risotto is creamy
  6. Step 6

    About halfway through the broth additions, stir in the peas so they cook gently with the rice. If using frozen peas, this timing ensures they cook through without getting mushy. Fresh peas will stay bright and tender, adding a lovely texture contrast.

    ⏱ 10 minutes into broth additions✓ Peas are bright green and tender
  7. Step 7

    Once the rice is cooked to al dente and the risotto is creamy, remove the pan from heat. Stir in the butter and grated Parmesan cheese until melted and combined. This enriches the risotto and adds a silky finish. Season with salt and pepper to taste, but go easy as Parmesan adds saltiness.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Risotto is glossy and creamy, cheese fully melted
  8. Step 8

    Finally, fold in the chopped fresh mint leaves just before serving. Adding the mint at the end keeps its flavor fresh and vibrant, providing a bright herbal note that complements the peas beautifully. If you add it too early, the mint can lose its punch and turn dull.

    ⏱ 1 minute✓ Mint is bright green and fragrant
  9. Step 9

    Serve the risotto immediately while warm and creamy. Risotto doesn’t hold well once cooled, so it’s best enjoyed right away for that perfect texture and flavor. Garnish with extra Parmesan or mint if you like. Enjoy your fresh, spring-inspired meal!

    ⏱ Serve immediately✓ Risotto is creamy, slightly loose, and steaming hot

Pro Tips

  • Keep your broth warm on the stove while cooking the risotto. — Adding cold broth to the rice slows cooking and can cause uneven texture. Warm broth helps the rice cook steadily and release starch properly, resulting in a creamy, consistent risotto.
  • Stir gently but consistently during broth additions. — Stirring prevents the rice from sticking and burning, and helps release starch gradually. However, vigorous stirring can break grains and make the risotto gluey, so keep it gentle.
  • Add the peas halfway through cooking to keep them tender and bright. — Adding peas too early can overcook them, turning them mushy and dull in color. Adding them halfway through ensures they cook just enough to be tender but still vibrant.
  • Use fresh mint and add it only at the end. — Mint loses its bright flavor if cooked too long. Adding it at the end preserves its fresh aroma and adds a lively note that balances the creamy risotto.
  • Toast the rice in oil and aromatics before adding liquid. — Toasting coats the grains with fat, which helps them cook evenly and adds flavor. It also starts releasing starch early, contributing to creaminess.
  • Don’t rush the broth additions; patience is key. — Slow absorption allows the rice to release starch gradually, creating the signature creamy texture. Pouring all broth at once leads to uneven cooking and mushy rice.
  • Finish with butter and Parmesan off the heat. — Adding these ingredients off heat prevents the cheese from clumping and helps create a glossy, silky finish that coats the rice beautifully.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Adding all the broth at once
    Why: Many cooks want to speed up the process and pour all the broth in one go, thinking it will cook faster.
    Fix: Add broth gradually, one ladle at a time, stirring gently and waiting for absorption before adding more. This technique allows the rice to release starch slowly, resulting in creamy risotto with perfect texture.
  • Using cold broth
    Why: It’s easy to forget to warm broth or assume it’s fine to add cold liquid.
    Fix: Keep the broth warm on a separate burner while cooking. Cold broth cools the rice and slows cooking, leading to uneven texture.
  • Overcooking the peas
    Why: Adding peas too early or cooking risotto too long can make peas mushy and dull in color.
    Fix: Add peas halfway through cooking to keep them tender and bright green. If using frozen peas, add them later to avoid overcooking.
  • Not stirring enough
    Why: Some think risotto can be left alone to cook like rice in a pot.
    Fix: Stir gently but regularly to prevent sticking and encourage starch release. This is essential for creamy texture.
  • Adding mint too early
    Why: Adding fresh herbs at the start is common but not ideal for delicate herbs like mint.
    Fix: Fold in fresh mint right at the end off heat to preserve its bright flavor and aroma.

Variations

  • Lemon Zest and Thyme Risotto: Swap mint for fresh thyme and add a teaspoon of lemon zest at the end. This gives the risotto a citrusy, earthy twist that’s just as fresh and vibrant. It’s great if you want a slightly different herbal note.
    Add thyme earlier with the onions to infuse flavor, and stir in lemon zest off heat with the butter and cheese.
  • Creamy Mushroom and Pea Risotto: Add sautéed mushrooms along with the peas for an earthier, heartier risotto. Use cremini or button mushrooms, cooked until golden before adding rice. This makes the dish more savory and filling.
    Cook mushrooms separately to avoid excess moisture, then fold in with peas during broth additions.
  • Vegan Pea and Mint Risotto: Replace butter and Parmesan with vegan butter and nutritional yeast for a dairy-free version. The nutritional yeast adds a cheesy flavor without dairy, keeping it creamy and delicious.
    Add vegan butter and nutritional yeast off heat to maintain creaminess without curdling.
  • Pea and Mint Risotto with Crispy Pancetta: Top the risotto with crispy pancetta or bacon bits for a salty crunch that contrasts beautifully with the fresh peas and mint. It adds a smoky, savory depth to the dish.
    Cook pancetta separately until crisp and sprinkle over the plated risotto just before serving.
  • Herbed Ricotta Pea Risotto: Stir in a dollop of fresh ricotta cheese at the end for extra creaminess and a mild tang. Mix in chopped basil along with mint for a more complex herb flavor.
    Add ricotta off heat to avoid curdling and fold in herbs last to keep flavors vibrant.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Risotto should not be left out at room temperature for more than 2 hours. Cover it loosely to avoid drying out if serving shortly after cooking.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftover risotto in an airtight container for up to 3 days. It thickens as it cools, so add a splash of broth or water when reheating to loosen the texture. Reheat gently to avoid drying out.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Risotto can be frozen for up to 1 month. Cool completely, then freeze in airtight containers or freezer bags. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat slowly with added liquid to restore creaminess. Texture may be slightly softer after freezing.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat risotto on the stovetop over low heat, stirring frequently with a splash of broth or water to bring back creaminess. Microwave works too but stir halfway through. Avoid high heat to prevent drying or burning.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the risotto base (onion, garlic, rice toasted) and warm broth ahead. Store separately and combine to finish cooking and add peas, mint, and cheese just before serving for maximum freshness.

FAQ

Can I use frozen peas instead of fresh?
Absolutely! Frozen peas work well and are a great option when fresh aren’t available. Add them halfway through cooking so they warm through without getting mushy. This keeps their texture and color vibrant.
What if I don’t have white wine?
You can skip the wine or replace it with an equal amount of broth or a splash of lemon juice for acidity. Wine adds depth but the risotto will still be delicious without it.
How do I know when the risotto is done?
The rice should be tender with a slight bite (al dente) and the texture creamy but not soupy. Taste a grain to check. If it’s too firm, continue adding broth and cooking a few more minutes.
Can I make this risotto vegan?
Yes! Substitute butter with vegan margarine or olive oil, and replace Parmesan with nutritional yeast or a vegan cheese alternative. Add these off heat to keep the creamy texture.
Why is my risotto too thick or dry?
This usually happens if not enough broth is added or it’s cooked over too high heat causing liquid to evaporate too fast. Add broth gradually and keep the heat medium-low for gentle cooking.
Can I prepare risotto in advance?
Risotto is best fresh, but you can prepare the base and broth ahead. Finish cooking and add fresh ingredients just before serving to keep flavors bright and texture perfect.
What’s the best way to store leftover risotto?
Store leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. It thickens when chilled, so reheat with a splash of broth to loosen it back up.
How important is stirring during risotto cooking?
Stirring helps release starch from the rice, creating creaminess, and prevents sticking. Gentle, consistent stirring is key; too vigorous can break grains, too little causes burning.
