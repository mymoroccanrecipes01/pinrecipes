Classic Irish Soda Bread with a Twist of Honey and Seeds

Molly Dupont Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 35 min cook 8 servings

This Irish soda bread is hearty and rustic with a subtle sweetness from honey and a crunchy seed topping. It’s quick to make and perfect for St. Patrick’s Day or any cozy weekend breakfast.

Introduction

I first tried my hand at Irish soda bread years ago, and honestly, it was a bit dry and crumbly — definitely not the soft, hearty bread I hoped for. After a few tweaks, including adding a touch of honey and a crunchy mix of seeds on top, I finally nailed a version that’s moist, flavorful, and has that perfect crust. The honey adds a gentle sweetness that balances the tang of the buttermilk, while the seeds bring a lovely texture contrast. What I love most is how quick it comes together — no yeast, no rising time, just mix and bake. It’s become a go-to for St. Paddy’s Day breakfast or alongside a bowl of soup on chilly evenings. This bread has a rustic look with a golden crust and a tender, slightly dense crumb. It’s the kind of bread that feels like a warm hug from an Irish grandmother, and I’m excited to share it with you!

Why This Works

Irish soda bread is unique because it uses baking soda as a leavening agent instead of yeast. The key chemical reaction happens when the alkaline baking soda meets the acidic buttermilk, producing carbon dioxide bubbles that lift the dough. This reaction is immediate, which is why the dough must be baked right away to capture the rise. Timing is crucial to avoid a dense loaf.

Flour choice affects texture significantly. All-purpose flour provides enough gluten for structure but keeps the bread tender. Sifting the flour aerates it, improving the crumb. The honey adds a subtle sweetness and also helps retain moisture, making the bread less dry than traditional recipes.

Scoring the dough is more than decorative; it controls how the bread expands in the oven and prevents random cracks. Meanwhile, the seeds on top contribute both flavor and a pleasant crunch, contrasting the soft crumb inside. Baking at a high temperature quickly forms a crust that locks in moisture, while the inside cooks through evenly.

Letting the bread cool completely before slicing is essential. The residual heat finishes the cooking process and allows the crumb to set properly. Cutting too soon leads to a gummy texture, as the starches haven’t stabilized yet. This recipe balances all these elements for a reliably delicious loaf every time.

  • Look for fresh, high-quality all-purpose flour with a moderate protein content (around 10-11%). This balance provides enough gluten for structure without making the bread tough. Avoid self-rising flour here, as it contains added leavening and salt that would throw off the recipe. Store flour in a cool, dry place to maintain freshness.
  • Use fresh baking soda to ensure the bread rises properly. Old or expired baking soda loses potency, resulting in a flat loaf. It’s the alkaline agent that reacts with buttermilk’s acid to produce carbon dioxide bubbles. Measure carefully — too much can cause a bitter taste, too little means insufficient rise.
  • Traditional buttermilk is acidic and helps activate the baking soda. Using cold buttermilk slows the reaction slightly, giving you time to mix and shape the dough. If you don’t have buttermilk, you can make a substitute by adding 1 tablespoon of lemon juice or vinegar to 1 3/4 cups milk and letting it sit for 5 minutes.
  • Adds a mild sweetness and helps retain moisture, improving the bread’s tenderness. Use a mild-flavored, runny honey to mix easily. Honey also aids browning, contributing to a golden crust. If you prefer, you can substitute with maple syrup or agave nectar, but the flavor will shift slightly.
  • A combination of sunflower, sesame, and pumpkin seeds adds nuttiness and crunch. Toast seeds lightly before sprinkling for extra aroma and flavor. Seeds also create visual appeal and texture contrast on top of the bread. If you’re allergic or prefer no seeds, simply omit or replace with oats.

Ingredients

  • 4 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 2 tablespoons honey, room temperature
  • 1 3/4 cups buttermilk, cold
  • 1/4 cup mixed seeds (sunflower, sesame, pumpkin), plus extra for topping

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preheating your oven to 425°F (220°C) and line a baking sheet with parchment paper. In a large bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking soda, and salt. This step ensures even distribution of the leavening agent and salt, which is crucial for the bread to rise properly and have balanced flavor. Sifting also helps avoid lumps and aerates the flour for a lighter crumb. Avoid skipping this or mixing unevenly, as it can cause dense spots or uneven rising.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Dry, evenly mixed flour with no clumps of baking soda or salt visible
  2. Step 2

    Make a well in the center of the dry ingredients and add the honey and cold buttermilk. Use a wooden spoon to gently stir until just combined. The dough will be sticky and shaggy — that’s exactly what you want. Overmixing develops gluten and leads to a tough bread, so mix just enough to bring everything together. The cold buttermilk reacts with the baking soda to create bubbles that help the bread rise, so keep it chilled until mixing.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Sticky dough that holds together but isn’t smooth or overly elastic
  3. Step 3

    Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface and gently knead it just 5-6 times to form a rough ball. Handle the dough gently to maintain the air bubbles created by the leavening reaction. Too much kneading will make the bread dense. Shape it into a round loaf about 7 inches wide.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Soft ball of dough that holds shape but feels light and airy
  4. Step 4

    Transfer the dough to the prepared baking sheet. Using a sharp knife, score a deep cross on top of the loaf. This traditional step helps the bread bake evenly and allows steam to escape, preventing cracks. It also gives the bread its classic rustic look. Don’t be afraid to cut deeply — about 1/2 inch deep works well.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Visible deep cross scored on dough surface
  5. Step 5

    Brush the top of the loaf lightly with a little extra buttermilk or water, then sprinkle the mixed seeds generously over the top. This adds a nutty flavor and a crunchy texture that contrasts beautifully with the soft crumb inside. It also makes the bread look inviting and rustic. If you skip this, the bread will still be tasty but less visually interesting.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Even seed coverage sticking to the moistened dough surface
  6. Step 6

    Place the loaf in the preheated oven and bake for 30-35 minutes. The high temperature helps form a golden crust quickly while the inside cooks through. Halfway through baking, check the bread and rotate the pan if your oven heats unevenly. Avoid opening the oven too often, or the bread might collapse.

    ⏱ 30-35 minutes✓ Golden brown crust with a firm feel when tapped
  7. Step 7

    To check if the bread is done, tap the bottom of the loaf — it should sound hollow. If it doesn’t, bake for another 5 minutes and check again. This hollow sound means the interior is fully cooked and not doughy. Using a thermometer, an internal temp of around 200°F (93°C) is ideal. Underbaking results in a gummy center.

    ⏱ 5 minutes (checking time)✓ Hollow sound when tapping bottom of loaf
  8. Step 8

    Remove the bread from the oven and transfer to a wire rack to cool completely before slicing. Cooling lets the crumb set and prevents it from becoming gummy when cut. Resist the urge to slice it warm — it’s tempting but the texture won’t be right. Letting it cool also enhances the flavors.

    ⏱ 1 hour✓ Bread cool to touch and crumb feels set, not doughy
  9. Step 9

    Slice and serve with butter, jam, or alongside a hearty stew. This bread is best enjoyed fresh but also toasts beautifully the next day. Store leftovers wrapped in a clean kitchen towel or paper bag to keep the crust crisp. Avoid plastic wrap which traps moisture and softens the crust.

    ⏱ Immediate serving or storage✓ Slices hold shape and have a tender crumb with crunchy crust

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your flour and baking soda together before mixing. — Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking soda and salt, which ensures consistent rise and texture. If you skip this, you risk uneven pockets of baking soda that can cause bitter spots or uneven crumb.
  • Use cold buttermilk straight from the fridge. — Cold buttermilk slows the chemical reaction with baking soda, giving you a bit more time to mix and shape the dough before baking. Warm liquids speed up the reaction and can make the bread dense if you don’t bake immediately.
  • Score a deep cross on top of the dough before baking. — This traditional scoring helps the bread expand in a controlled way during baking and prevents random cracks. It also creates the classic rustic look. A shallow score won’t release steam properly, leading to uneven cracks.
  • Don’t overmix or overknead the dough. — Overworking the dough develops gluten, which makes soda bread tough and chewy rather than tender and crumbly. Mixing just enough to bring ingredients together keeps the crumb soft and light.
  • Brush the dough with buttermilk or water before adding seeds. — This moistens the surface so seeds stick well and brown nicely in the oven. Without this step, seeds may fall off or burn unevenly, affecting both texture and appearance.
  • Bake at a high temperature for a crispy crust. — The 425°F oven quickly forms a golden crust that traps moisture inside, giving you a tender crumb. Lower temperatures produce pale, soft crusts and can dry out the bread.
  • Let the bread cool completely before slicing. — Cooling allows the crumb to set and residual heat to finish cooking. Cutting too soon releases steam and results in a gummy texture. Patience here rewards you with perfect slices.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using expired or insufficient baking soda
    Why: Many people overlook the freshness of baking soda, assuming it lasts forever. Old baking soda loses its leavening power, leading to dense, flat bread.
    Fix: Test baking soda by adding a teaspoon to vinegar — it should fizz vigorously. Replace if it doesn’t. Measure precisely and store in a cool, dry place to keep it fresh.
  • Overmixing the dough
    Why: Beginners often think more mixing equals better dough, but for soda bread, overmixing develops gluten and toughens the bread.
    Fix: Mix just until the flour is moistened and the dough holds together. Stop as soon as you see no dry flour. Handle gently when kneading.
  • Not baking immediately after mixing
    Why: Because the leavening reaction starts as soon as wet meets dry, waiting too long causes loss of carbon dioxide bubbles.
    Fix: Preheat your oven first so you can bake right away. Mix dough quickly and place in oven within 10 minutes.
  • Cutting into the bread too soon
    Why: Impatience leads to slicing warm bread, which hasn’t set properly and results in a gummy texture.
    Fix: Allow the loaf to cool fully on a wire rack before cutting. This sets the crumb and improves flavor.
  • Skipping the scoring step
    Why: Some people think scoring is just decorative and omit it, but it controls how the bread expands.
    Fix: Use a sharp knife to cut a deep cross on top before baking. This helps the bread rise evenly and prevents cracks.

Variations

  • Whole Wheat Irish Soda Bread: Replace half the all-purpose flour with whole wheat flour for a nuttier flavor and denser texture. You may need to add an extra tablespoon of buttermilk to keep the dough moist. This version is heartier and perfect for those who love whole grains.
    Whole wheat flour absorbs more liquid, so watch dough consistency and adjust buttermilk as needed. Knead gently to avoid toughness.
  • Cheddar and Chive Soda Bread: Fold 1 cup of shredded sharp cheddar and 2 tablespoons of chopped fresh chives into the dough before shaping. This savory twist adds richness and a fresh herbal note, making it great for serving with soups or stews.
    Add cheese and herbs last and mix gently to distribute evenly. Baking times remain the same.
  • Sweet Raisin and Cinnamon Soda Bread: Add 1 cup of raisins and 1 teaspoon of ground cinnamon to the dry ingredients for a sweeter, breakfast-style bread. It pairs wonderfully with butter and jam for a cozy morning treat.
    Toss raisins in a little flour before adding to prevent sinking. Cinnamon should be mixed evenly with flour.
  • Gluten-Free Irish Soda Bread: Use a gluten-free flour blend designed for baking in place of all-purpose flour. Add 1 teaspoon xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t contain it. The texture will be slightly different but still satisfying and tender.
    Gluten-free dough will be more fragile; handle gently and bake immediately. Expect a denser crumb.
  • Seed-Free Classic Soda Bread: Simply omit the seeds and brush the top with buttermilk for a traditional look. This version is perfect for those with seed allergies or who prefer a simpler bread. The flavor remains rich and comforting.
    No changes to baking time or temperature needed. The crust will be smooth and golden.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Soda bread keeps well at room temperature for up to 2 days if wrapped loosely in a clean kitchen towel or stored in a paper bag. Avoid plastic wrap at room temp as it traps moisture and softens the crust.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store in an airtight container or wrapped tightly in plastic wrap for up to 5 days. The bread may lose some crust crispness but can be refreshed by toasting before serving.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cooled bread tightly in plastic wrap and then foil to prevent freezer burn. Freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight at room temperature wrapped in a towel to retain moisture.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat slices in a toaster or oven at 350°F for 5-7 minutes to restore crustiness. Avoid microwaving as it makes bread gummy. For whole loaf, warm in oven wrapped in foil for 10-15 minutes.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can mix and shape the dough up to 1 hour before baking, keeping it covered at room temperature. For longer storage, bake fully and freeze. On serving day, thaw and reheat as described.

FAQ

Can I substitute regular milk for buttermilk?
You can make a quick buttermilk substitute by adding 1 tablespoon of lemon juice or white vinegar to 1 3/4 cups of milk and letting it sit for 5 minutes. This acidity is necessary to react with the baking soda and create the rise. Using plain milk without acid will result in a dense, flat bread.
Why is my soda bread dense and heavy?
This usually happens if the baking soda is old or if you overmix the dough. Old baking soda won’t produce enough gas to leaven the bread, and overmixing develops too much gluten, making it tough. Also, waiting too long to bake after mixing can cause the reaction to fizzle out.
Can I use whole wheat flour instead of all-purpose?
Yes, but whole wheat flour absorbs more liquid and can make the bread denser. I recommend substituting only half the flour with whole wheat and adding a little extra buttermilk to keep the dough moist. Handle the dough gently to avoid toughness.
What’s the purpose of scoring the dough?
Scoring creates controlled steam vents that allow the bread to expand evenly and prevents random cracks. It also gives the bread its classic look. Without scoring, the loaf may crack unpredictably and not rise as well.
How do I know when the bread is fully baked?
Tap the bottom of the loaf — it should sound hollow, indicating the inside is cooked through. Alternatively, use an instant-read thermometer; the internal temperature should be around 200°F (93°C). If it sounds dull or feels heavy, bake a few minutes longer.
Can I add seeds or nuts inside the dough?
Absolutely! Adding seeds, nuts, or dried fruit inside the dough is a great way to customize flavor and texture. Just fold them in gently after mixing the wet and dry ingredients. Keep in mind additions can affect moisture, so adjust buttermilk slightly if needed.
Is it okay to freeze soda bread?
Yes, soda bread freezes well. Wrap it tightly in plastic wrap and foil to prevent freezer burn. Thaw overnight at room temperature wrapped in a towel to keep moisture balanced. Reheat in the oven or toaster for best texture.
Why does my soda bread have a bitter taste?
A bitter taste often means too much baking soda was used or it wasn’t mixed evenly. Make sure to measure precisely and sift the dry ingredients well. Using expired baking soda can also cause off flavors.
