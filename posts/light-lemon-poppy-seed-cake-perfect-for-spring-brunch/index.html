Light Lemon Poppy Seed Cake Perfect for Spring Brunch

This airy lemon poppy seed cake is just the right balance of bright citrus and subtle crunch from the seeds. I love how it’s light enough for a spring brunch but still feels like a treat. Perfect with a cup of tea or coffee!

Introduction

I first made this lemon poppy seed cake a few springs ago when I was craving something bright but not too heavy for a weekend brunch. The classic combo of lemon and poppy seeds is always a winner, but what makes this version special is how light and tender it turns out — almost fluffy but with a bit of crunch from the seeds. I learned that folding in the sour cream and milk gently keeps the cake moist without weighing it down, and using fresh lemon zest and juice gives it a vibrant, natural flavor that’s not too sweet. Plus, it’s super simple to whip up, which is perfect for a relaxed morning when you want something homemade but fuss-free. The finished cake has a lovely golden crumb and a fresh citrus aroma that fills the kitchen — it’s the kind of thing you want to slice up and share with friends over coffee. Honestly, I’ve tried a few lemon poppy seed cake recipes before, but this one always comes out just right and disappears fast. If you’re looking for a spring brunch dessert that’s light, fresh, and easy, this cake is definitely worth trying!

What You'll Need

One 9-inch cake, 8 servings

  • 1 1/2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 3 tablespoons poppy seeds
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 2 large eggs, room temperature
  • 1/2 cup sour cream
  • 1/4 cup whole milk
  • Zest of 2 lemons
  • 1/4 cup fresh lemon juice
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and grease a 9-inch round cake pan. This step is crucial because a properly greased pan ensures the cake releases easily after baking, preventing any frustrating sticking. You can use butter or a non-stick spray, and I like to lightly flour the pan afterward to give the cake a smooth edge. Avoid skipping this or using too little grease, as it can cause the cake to tear when removed.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Pan is evenly greased and floured, no bare spots
Step 2
In a medium bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, baking soda, salt, and poppy seeds. Sifting helps to aerate the flour and remove lumps, which contributes to a lighter crumb. Mixing the dry ingredients thoroughly ensures even distribution of the leavening agents and seeds, so your cake rises uniformly and has consistent texture.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are well combined and free of clumps
Step 3
In a large bowl, beat the softened butter and sugar together until pale and fluffy using a hand mixer or stand mixer. This creaming process incorporates air into the batter, which is essential for a light texture. It usually takes about 3-5 minutes. Don’t rush this step or use cold butter, as it won’t cream properly and your cake could turn out dense.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter and sugar mixture is light in color and airy in texture
Step 4
Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition. This gradual incorporation helps maintain the batter’s emulsion and prevents it from curdling. If eggs are added too quickly or all at once, the batter might separate, leading to a less tender crumb.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter looks smooth and glossy without streaks
Step 5
Mix in the lemon zest, lemon juice, and vanilla extract until just combined. Fresh lemon zest is key here because it adds aromatic oils that boost the cake’s flavor without adding extra moisture. Stir gently to keep the batter light.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Evenly mixed batter with visible flecks of lemon zest
Step 6
Alternate adding the dry ingredients and the sour cream with milk to the butter mixture, beginning and ending with the dry ingredients. Mix each addition just until combined. This alternating method helps the batter stay light and prevents overmixing, which can develop gluten and make the cake tough.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Smooth batter with no visible flour streaks and a creamy texture
Step 7
Pour the batter into the prepared cake pan and smooth the top with a spatula. Tap the pan gently on the counter to release any large air bubbles. This ensures an even bake and a smooth surface for your cake.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter evenly spread and surface is level
Step 8
Bake in the preheated oven for 30-35 minutes, or until a toothpick inserted into the center comes out clean. The baking time can vary depending on your oven, so start checking at 30 minutes. Overbaking will dry the cake, while underbaking leaves it gummy inside.
⏱ Time: 30-35 minutes
✓ Doneness: Toothpick inserted in center comes out clean or with a few moist crumbs
Step 9
Let the cake cool in the pan for 10 minutes, then run a knife around the edges and transfer to a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling in the pan too long can cause sogginess, while moving it too soon can cause it to break. Cooling on a wire rack prevents condensation and keeps the bottom from getting wet.
⏱ Time: 20-30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake is cool to touch and holds shape when lifted out of pan

Pro Tips

Always use room temperature eggs and butter for the best creaming results.
Room temperature ingredients blend more smoothly and trap air better when creamed, which leads to a lighter, fluffier cake. Cold butter won’t incorporate air properly, and cold eggs can cause the batter to curdle.
Don’t overmix once you add the dry ingredients to avoid a tough cake.
Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which makes the cake chewy and dense instead of tender. Mix just until the flour disappears to keep the crumb soft and delicate.
Use fresh lemon zest and juice for the brightest flavor.
Bottled lemon juice lacks the fresh aroma and complexity of fresh lemons. The zest contains essential oils that provide a fragrant citrus note that can’t be replicated with extracts or bottled juice.
Sift your dry ingredients to remove lumps and aerate the flour.
Sifting helps create a lighter batter by adding air and breaking up clumps, which improves the cake’s texture and rise.
Let the cake cool completely before slicing to avoid crumbling.
Cutting warm cake can cause it to fall apart because the structure hasn’t set fully. Cooling allows the crumb to firm up, making cleaner slices.
Tap the cake pan gently before baking to release air bubbles.
This prevents large holes or tunnels in the cake by allowing trapped air to escape, resulting in an even crumb.
Use a toothpick to check doneness starting at 30 minutes.
Ovens vary, so checking early prevents overbaking, which can dry out the cake. A clean toothpick means it’s perfectly baked.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using cold butter or eggs

Why it happens Many people forget to take ingredients out ahead of time, assuming they can mix right away. Cold ingredients don’t cream well and can cause the batter to curdle.

Fix Plan ahead and bring butter and eggs to room temperature before starting. This ensures smooth mixing and proper air incorporation for a light cake.

Overmixing the batter after adding flour

Why it happens It’s tempting to keep mixing to get a smooth batter, but overworking the flour develops gluten.

Fix Mix flour in gently and just until combined. Stop as soon as you don’t see dry spots to keep the cake tender.

Not measuring flour correctly

Why it happens Scooping flour directly with a measuring cup can pack it down, adding too much flour.

Fix Spoon flour lightly into your measuring cup and level it off with a knife for accurate measurement and a lighter cake.

Skipping the pan greasing and flouring

Why it happens Sometimes people rely only on parchment or forget this step entirely.

Fix Always grease and flour your pan or use parchment to prevent sticking and ensure the cake comes out cleanly.

Overbaking the cake

Why it happens Leaving the cake in too long dries it out, often because of unfamiliarity with oven temperatures.

Fix Check the cake starting at 30 minutes with a toothpick. Remove it as soon as it comes out clean or with moist crumbs.

Variations

Glazed Lemon Poppy Seed Cake

Add a simple lemon glaze made from powdered sugar and lemon juice poured over the cooled cake for extra sweetness and zing. This adds a shiny finish and enhances the citrus flavor without making the cake heavy.

Note: Make sure the cake is completely cooled before glazing to prevent the glaze from melting and running off.

Lemon Poppy Seed Bundt Cake

Use a bundt pan instead of a round pan for a decorative shape. The baking time will be slightly longer, and the cake will have a crispier crust, offering a nice contrast to the moist crumb inside.

Note: Increase baking time by about 10 minutes and check doneness carefully to avoid overbaking.

Vegan Lemon Poppy Seed Cake

Replace eggs with flax eggs and use plant-based yogurt instead of sour cream. Use vegan butter or coconut oil for richness. This version will be slightly denser but still moist and flavorful.

Note: Flax eggs require 5 minutes to gel before mixing. Adjust baking time as needed since vegan cakes sometimes bake faster.

Almond Lemon Poppy Seed Cake

Add 1/2 cup almond flour to the dry ingredients for a nutty flavor and tender crumb. Top with slivered almonds before baking for crunch. This variation adds depth and complexity to the cake’s texture.

Note: Reduce all-purpose flour slightly to accommodate almond flour and prevent a dry cake.

Lemon Poppy Seed Muffins

Portion the batter into muffin tins for individual servings perfect for on-the-go breakfasts or snacks. Baking time is shorter, about 18-22 minutes, and they’re easy to pack and share.

Note: Fill muffin cups about 2/3 full and check doneness with a toothpick early to avoid overbaking.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the cake covered loosely with a cake dome or plastic wrap at room temperature for up to 2 days. Avoid airtight containers at room temp as they can trap moisture and make the crust soggy.

Refrigerator

Keep the cake in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 5 days. The cake may dry out slightly when cold, so bring to room temperature before serving or warm gently in the microwave for 10-15 seconds.

Freezer

Wrap the cooled cake tightly in plastic wrap and then foil, freezing for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature. Reheat slices gently in the microwave to refresh texture.

Reheating

Warm slices in the microwave for 10-15 seconds or in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes. Adding a small splash of lemon juice or brushing with syrup before reheating can help keep the cake moist.

Make Ahead

You can bake the cake a day ahead and store it wrapped at room temperature or in the fridge. If glazing, do it just before serving to keep it fresh and shiny.

FAQ

Can I substitute yogurt for the sour cream?
Yes, plain full-fat yogurt works well as a substitute for sour cream and will keep the cake moist and tender. Use the same amount and make sure it’s not too watery. Greek yogurt is best for thickness.
What if I don’t have fresh lemons?
Fresh lemon zest and juice provide the best flavor, but if you don’t have fresh lemons, bottled lemon juice can be used in a pinch. Use about half the amount of bottled juice and add a teaspoon of lemon extract to boost flavor.
Can I make this cake gluten-free?
You can substitute a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend cup-for-cup, but results may vary slightly. Look for blends with xanthan gum included for structure. The cake might be a bit more crumbly but still delicious.
How do I store leftover cake?
Wrap leftover cake tightly in plastic wrap or store in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days or in the fridge for up to 5 days. For longer storage, freeze as described in the storage section.
Can I double this recipe?
Yes, doubling works fine. Use two pans or a larger pan and adjust the baking time accordingly. Start checking for doneness at the original time and add more as needed.
Why is my cake dense?
Dense cake usually means overmixing after adding flour, cold ingredients, or not enough leavening. Make sure to cream butter and sugar well, use room temperature eggs, and mix flour in gently just until combined.
Can I add a glaze or frosting?
Absolutely! A simple lemon glaze made from powdered sugar and lemon juice complements this cake perfectly. You can also try cream cheese frosting for a richer finish.
What’s the best way to zest lemons?
Use a microplane or fine grater and zest only the yellow part, avoiding the bitter white pith underneath. Fresh zest adds bright oils and flavor that really elevate the cake.
