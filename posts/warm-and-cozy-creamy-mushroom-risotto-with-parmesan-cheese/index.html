Warm and Cozy Creamy Mushroom Risotto with Parmesan Cheese

Molly Dupont
Published on March 28, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
4 Servings
Medium Difficulty

This creamy mushroom risotto is pure comfort in a bowl. The secret is slowly toasting the rice and stirring in warm broth gradually for that perfect creamy texture. Ideal for chilly evenings or a cozy dinner.

Pro Tips

Always keep your broth warm on the stove while making risotto.
Adding cold broth cools down the rice and interrupts the cooking process, leading to uneven texture. Warm broth keeps the temperature consistent, allowing the rice to cook evenly and release starch properly for that creamy finish.
Stir frequently but not constantly; gentle stirring is enough.
Stirring helps release starch from the rice but over-stirring can break grains and make the risotto gluey. Gentle, frequent stirring encourages creaminess while preserving the rice’s texture.
Toast the rice for a couple of minutes before adding liquid.
Toasting coats the grains in oil, enhancing their nutty flavor and helping them maintain a firmer texture during cooking. Skipping this step can result in mushier risotto.
Use dry white wine to deglaze the pan after toasting the rice.
Wine adds acidity that balances the richness of the dish and enhances flavor complexity. It also helps lift browned bits from the pan, incorporating more flavor into the risotto.
Don’t rush the broth additions; add one ladleful at a time.
Slow liquid absorption allows the rice to release starch gradually, creating the signature creamy texture. Pouring too much liquid at once results in uneven cooking and a soupy risotto.
Finish with cold butter and freshly grated Parmesan off the heat.
This technique, called 'mantecatura,' enriches the risotto and gives it a silky, smooth texture. Adding them while still on heat can cause the butter to separate or cheese to clump.
Serve risotto immediately after cooking.
Risotto thickens and loses its creamy texture as it cools. Eating it right away ensures the best flavor and mouthfeel.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Adding cold broth directly to the rice

Why it happens Many cooks forget to warm the broth beforehand, assuming it won’t affect cooking much.

Fix Always heat your broth to a gentle simmer before adding it. This maintains the rice’s cooking temperature and prevents the dish from cooling down and cooking unevenly.

Not stirring enough during cooking

Why it happens Some believe risotto can be left unattended like regular rice and underestimate the importance of stirring.

Fix Stir frequently to release starch from the rice, which creates the creamy texture. Gentle, frequent stirring is best; avoid vigorous stirring that breaks grains.

Using the wrong type of rice

Why it happens People sometimes use long-grain or instant rice, which doesn’t have the right starch content.

Fix Use Arborio or other risotto-specific short-grain rice varieties to achieve the proper creamy texture and bite.

Overcooking mushrooms or overcrowding the pan

Why it happens Trying to cook too many mushrooms at once causes steaming instead of browning.

Fix Cook mushrooms in batches if needed, allowing them to brown properly for better flavor and texture.

Adding cheese too early or overheating it

Why it happens Adding Parmesan while the risotto is still on high heat can cause it to clump or separate.

Fix Remove the pan from heat before stirring in cheese and butter to ensure a smooth, creamy finish.

Variations

Spinach and Lemon Risotto

Add fresh spinach leaves during the last 5 minutes of cooking and finish with a squeeze of lemon juice and zest. This variation brightens the dish and adds a fresh contrast to the creamy mushrooms.

Note: Add spinach late to avoid overcooking and losing its vibrant color; lemon juice added at the end preserves brightness.

Butternut Squash Risotto

Incorporate roasted butternut squash cubes into the risotto for a sweet, nutty flavor. This adds a lovely texture contrast and seasonal twist perfect for fall evenings.

Note: Roast squash separately until tender and add during the last few minutes of cooking to keep pieces intact.

Seafood Risotto

Swap mushrooms for a mix of shrimp, scallops, and mussels. Use seafood broth instead of vegetable broth for a briny depth. This makes a luxurious dinner perfect for special occasions.

Note: Cook seafood separately and fold in at the end to avoid overcooking delicate proteins.

Vegan Mushroom Risotto

Replace butter with olive oil and omit Parmesan or use a vegan cheese alternative. Nutritional yeast adds a cheesy flavor without dairy, perfect for plant-based diets.

Note: Use a good-quality vegetable broth and stir in nutritional yeast at the end for best flavor.

Truffle Oil Risotto

Finish the mushroom risotto with a drizzle of truffle oil for an earthy, aromatic boost. This elevates the dish with minimal effort and pairs beautifully with the mushrooms.

Note: Add truffle oil off heat to preserve its delicate aroma.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Risotto should not be left at room temperature for more than 2 hours to avoid bacterial growth. Cover with plastic wrap or store in a shallow container if serving later.

Refrigerator

Store leftover risotto in an airtight container for up to 3 days. It will thicken and lose creaminess when cold. Reheat gently with a splash of broth or water to restore texture.

Freezer

Risotto can be frozen for up to 1 month. Freeze in airtight containers or heavy-duty freezer bags. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat slowly, adding liquid to loosen as needed.

Reheating

Reheat risotto on the stovetop over low heat, stirring frequently. Add a little broth or water to loosen the texture. Avoid microwave reheating without stirring as it can cause uneven heating and drying.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the risotto up to the point of adding cheese and butter 1 day ahead. Cool quickly and refrigerate. Finish with butter and Parmesan just before serving to maintain fresh flavor and creamy texture.

FAQ

Can I use regular long-grain rice instead of Arborio?
Long-grain rice doesn’t have the high starch content that Arborio has, so it won’t produce the creamy texture risotto is known for. The grains will remain separate and the dish won’t have that luscious mouthfeel. It’s best to stick with Arborio or other short-grain risotto rice varieties for authentic results.
What if I don’t have white wine? Can I skip it?
You can omit the wine if you prefer or don’t have it on hand. Just replace it with a bit more broth or a splash of lemon juice for acidity. The wine adds complexity and brightness, but the risotto will still be delicious without it.
How do I know when the risotto is done cooking?
Risotto is done when the rice is tender but still slightly firm to the bite — what Italians call 'al dente.' The texture should be creamy and not dry or soupy. Taste the rice grains to check doneness during the last few minutes of cooking.
Can I make risotto in advance?
Risotto is best served fresh because it thickens and loses its creamy texture as it cools. However, you can prepare it up to the final step and refrigerate it, then finish with butter and cheese just before serving. Reheat gently with broth to restore creaminess.
Why is my risotto too thick or sticky?
This usually happens if you add broth too quickly or don’t stir enough. Slow addition of warm broth and frequent stirring are key to releasing starch gradually and creating the right creamy texture. Adding too much liquid at once can make it soupy, while not enough stirring can make it sticky.
Can I use frozen mushrooms?
Frozen mushrooms release more water and can make the risotto watery if not cooked properly. If using frozen, thaw and drain them well, then sauté over high heat to evaporate excess moisture before adding rice.
What’s the best way to store leftover risotto?
Cool leftovers quickly and store in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. Reheat gently with a splash of broth to loosen the texture. Avoid leaving risotto at room temperature for long periods to prevent spoilage.
Can I add other vegetables to this risotto?
Absolutely! Vegetables like peas, asparagus, or spinach can be added during the last few minutes of cooking. Just be mindful of their cooking times to avoid overcooking or sogginess.

What You'll Need

Approximately 4 servings

  • 1 1/2 cups Arborio rice, uncooked
  • 5 cups low-sodium vegetable broth, kept warm
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 small onion, finely chopped
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 10 ounces cremini mushrooms, sliced
  • 1/2 cup dry white wine
  • 1/2 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese
  • 2 tablespoons unsalted butter
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
  • Fresh parsley, chopped, for garnish

How To Do It

Step 1
Heat the vegetable broth in a saucepan and keep it at a gentle simmer on low heat. This step is crucial because adding cold broth to the rice interrupts the cooking process and can make the risotto unevenly cooked. Keeping the broth warm ensures the rice absorbs liquid gradually and cooks consistently, resulting in a creamy texture.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Broth should be steaming gently but not boiling.
Step 2
In a large skillet or heavy-bottomed pan, heat olive oil over medium heat. Add the chopped onion and sauté until translucent and soft, about 4-5 minutes. This step builds the flavor base; cooking the onion slowly releases its sweetness without browning, which keeps the risotto delicate and balanced.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Onions should be soft and slightly glossy, not browned.
Step 3
Add the minced garlic and sliced mushrooms to the pan. Cook until the mushrooms release their moisture and start to brown, about 7-8 minutes. This step intensifies the mushroom flavor and adds a nice umami depth to the dish. Avoid overcrowding the pan to ensure mushrooms brown instead of steam.
⏱ Time: 8 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mushrooms should be tender and golden brown.
Step 4
Add the Arborio rice to the pan with the mushrooms and stir well to coat every grain with oil. Toast the rice for about 2 minutes, stirring constantly. This toasting step is important as it helps the rice maintain its texture during cooking and enhances its nutty flavor.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Rice grains look glossy and slightly translucent at the edges.
Step 5
Pour in the white wine and stir until it’s mostly absorbed by the rice. The acidity of the wine balances the richness of the dish and adds a subtle complexity. Don’t rush this step; let the rice soak up the wine fully before moving on.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Wine should be mostly evaporated, rice looks wet but not soupy.
Step 6
Begin adding the warm broth one ladle at a time, stirring frequently. Wait until the liquid is almost fully absorbed before adding the next ladle. This slow addition and constant stirring coax the starch out of the rice, creating that signature creamy risotto texture. Rushing this step can lead to uneven cooking and a less creamy result.
⏱ Time: 20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Rice is tender but still slightly firm to the bite, and the mixture is creamy.
Step 7
Once the rice is cooked to your liking, remove the pan from heat. Stir in the butter and grated Parmesan cheese until melted and fully incorporated. This finishing step enriches the risotto, adding a silky mouthfeel and a savory depth that rounds out the flavors beautifully.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Risotto looks glossy and creamy with no dry bits.
Step 8
Season with salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste. Be careful with salt since Parmesan is already salty. Taste as you go to avoid over-seasoning. The pepper adds a subtle warmth that balances the richness.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Balanced seasoning without overpowering saltiness.
Step 9
Serve immediately garnished with chopped fresh parsley for a pop of color and freshness. Risotto waits for no one — it’s best enjoyed right away while creamy and luscious. Leftover risotto tends to thicken and lose its texture, so plan to serve it hot.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Risotto is steaming hot with vibrant garnish.
