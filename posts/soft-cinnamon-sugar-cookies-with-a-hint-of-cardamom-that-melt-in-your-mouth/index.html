Soft Cinnamon Sugar Cookies with a Hint of Cardamom That Melt in Your Mouth

Molly Dupont Published on May 30, 2026
15 min prep 12 min cook 24 servings

These soft cinnamon sugar cookies are a cozy twist on a classic treat, with just a whisper of cardamom adding a warm, exotic note. They're perfect for afternoon tea or a comforting snack any day.

Introduction

I first stumbled on this soft cinnamon sugar cookie recipe during a chilly fall afternoon when I was craving something sweet but comforting. What makes this version stand out is the subtle hint of cardamom, which adds a fragrant warmth that pairs beautifully with cinnamon. I’ve tried countless cinnamon sugar cookies before, but this one’s softness and spice balance always bring me back. The trick I learned is to cream the butter and sugar just right—too little, and the cookies turn dense; too much, and they spread too thin. Also, chilling the dough before baking helps keep them thick and pillowy. When fresh out of the oven, they’re soft, lightly crisp on the edges, and melt in your mouth with the perfect cinnamon-cardamom blend. My family loves these with a cup of tea or hot cocoa, especially on lazy weekends. Honestly, I’ve had my share of failed batches where the cookies came out too dry or flat, but this recipe nails the texture every time. I think you’ll find it just as comforting and easy to make as I do!

Why This Works

These soft cinnamon sugar cookies owe their tender texture to the balance of ingredients and techniques used. The creaming of butter and sugar incorporates air, which creates tiny pockets that expand during baking, resulting in a light, soft crumb. The use of baking powder provides the necessary leavening to help the cookies rise gently without becoming cakey.

The inclusion of cardamom alongside cinnamon adds complexity without overpowering the palate. Cardamom’s volatile oils release flavor when gently heated, giving a warm, aromatic note that pairs beautifully with the cinnamon’s sweetness. Sifting the spices ensures even distribution, preventing any bitter clumps.

Chilling the dough is another key step. It solidifies the butter, which slows spreading and helps maintain the cookie’s shape and softness. This resting period also allows the flavors to meld, enhancing the overall taste. Finally, baking at a moderate temperature ensures the cookies cook through evenly while retaining moisture, with just a slight crisp on the edges.

The sugar coating not only adds a pleasant crunch but also caramelizes slightly during baking, creating a delicate contrast to the soft interior. Altogether, these elements work in harmony to produce cookies that are tender, flavorful, and irresistibly soft.

  • Choose a good-quality all-purpose flour with moderate protein content (around 10-12%) for the ideal balance of structure and tenderness. Too high protein flours (like bread flour) can make cookies tough, while low protein flours (cake flour) might cause them to spread too much. Sifting the flour aerates it and helps avoid lumps.
  • Use unsalted butter at room temperature for precise control over salt levels and to ensure even creaming with sugar. Butter’s fat content contributes richness and tenderness. Over-softened butter can cause excessive spreading, so aim for soft but still cool butter.
  • Granulated sugar not only sweetens but also helps create the cookie’s crisp edges due to caramelization. Rolling dough balls in sugar before baking adds a delightful crunch. Avoid substituting with powdered sugar here, as it alters texture and spreading.
  • Cinnamon provides the classic warm, sweet spice flavor. Use fresh ground cinnamon for best aroma and taste. Old or stale cinnamon can taste flat or bitter, affecting the cookie’s flavor profile.
  • Cardamom is a potent spice with floral and citrus notes. A small amount (1/4 teaspoon) adds complexity without overpowering. Ground cardamom should be fresh; stale cardamom loses its distinctive aroma. If unavailable, use a pinch of nutmeg as a substitute but expect a different flavor.
  • Baking powder acts as a leavening agent, producing carbon dioxide bubbles that help cookies rise and become tender. Use fresh baking powder; expired leaveners can cause flat, dense cookies.
  • Egg provides moisture, binding, and structure. It also helps with leavening and browning. Using a large egg at room temperature ensures even mixing and better texture.
  • Pure vanilla extract enhances the overall flavor, adding warmth and depth. Avoid artificial vanilla for best taste.

Ingredients

  • 2 1/2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground cardamom
  • 3/4 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1/4 cup granulated sugar (for rolling cookies)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by whisking together the flour, baking powder, salt, cinnamon, and cardamom in a medium bowl. This dry mix is important because it evenly distributes the spices and leavening agent, ensuring every cookie has that perfect flavor and rise. Be sure to sift or whisk thoroughly to avoid clumps, especially with the cardamom, which can be potent if concentrated in one spot.

    ⏱ 5✓ The mixture looks uniform with no lumps or streaks of flour or spices.
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, cream the softened butter and sugar together using a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed. This step is crucial because creaming incorporates air into the butter, which helps make the cookies tender and soft. Beat until the mixture is light in color and fluffy, about 3-5 minutes. Under-creaming will lead to denser cookies, while over-creaming can cause them to spread too much when baked.

    ⏱ 5✓ The butter and sugar mixture is pale, fluffy, and holds soft peaks.
  3. Step 3

    Add the egg and vanilla extract to the creamed butter and sugar. Beat on medium speed until fully combined. The egg adds moisture and structure, while the vanilla enhances the cookie’s aroma. Mixing thoroughly here ensures a smooth batter without streaks of egg white or yolk.

    ⏱ 2✓ The mixture looks smooth and glossy with no visible egg streaks.
  4. Step 4

    Gradually add the dry flour mixture to the wet ingredients in two additions, mixing on low speed. This gentle mixing prevents overworking the dough, which can make cookies tough. Scrape down the bowl between additions to ensure even incorporation. Stop mixing as soon as the flour disappears.

    ⏱ 3✓ The dough is soft, slightly sticky, and homogenous without flour patches.
  5. Step 5

    Cover the dough with plastic wrap and chill it in the refrigerator for at least 30 minutes. Chilling firms up the butter, preventing the cookies from spreading too much during baking and helping the flavors meld. If you skip this step, expect thinner, crispier cookies.

    ⏱ 30✓ The dough is firm and cold to the touch, easy to handle but not hard.
  6. Step 6

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line baking sheets with parchment paper. This temperature is ideal to bake the cookies evenly without browning them too fast, preserving their soft texture.

    ⏱ 10✓ Oven reaches steady 350°F and baking sheets are prepared.
  7. Step 7

    Scoop tablespoon-sized balls of chilled dough and roll each in the extra granulated sugar to coat. This sugar coating gives the cookies a slightly crisp, sweet crust that contrasts nicely with the soft interior. Place the balls about 2 inches apart on the baking sheets to allow for gentle spreading.

    ⏱ 10✓ Dough balls are evenly coated with sugar and spaced properly on sheets.
  8. Step 8

    Bake the cookies for 10-12 minutes, rotating the baking sheet halfway through for even baking. Watch for the edges to set and just start to turn golden, while the centers remain soft. Overbaking will dry them out, so err on the side of underbaking slightly.

    ⏱ 12✓ Edges are lightly golden, centers look set but soft and slightly puffy.
  9. Step 9

    Remove the cookies from the oven and let them cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. This resting time lets the cookies firm up just enough to move without breaking, while still staying soft and chewy inside.

    ⏱ 5✓ Cookies are firm enough to lift but still soft and tender when touched.

Pro Tips

  • Chill your dough for at least 30 minutes before baking. — Chilling solidifies the butter, which prevents the cookies from spreading too much and helps maintain a soft, thick texture. It also allows the flavors to meld, resulting in a more flavorful cookie.
  • Don’t overmix once you add the flour. — Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which can make cookies tough and chewy instead of soft and tender. Mix just until the flour disappears for the best texture.
  • Use room temperature butter for creaming. — Butter that’s too cold won’t cream properly with sugar, and butter that’s too soft won’t trap air well. Room temperature butter creates a fluffy mixture that leads to lighter cookies.
  • Roll dough balls generously in sugar before baking. — This sugar coating caramelizes during baking, creating a slight crisp crust that contrasts beautifully with the soft interior.
  • Bake at 350°F and watch the cookies closely. — This temperature ensures even baking without overbrowning. The cookies should be pulled when edges are set but centers still look soft to keep them tender.
  • Use fresh spices for maximum flavor. — Old cinnamon or cardamom lose their potency and can taste dull or bitter. Fresh spices provide the bright, warm flavor that makes these cookies special.
  • Let cookies cool on the baking sheet for a few minutes before moving. — This resting time lets cookies firm up just enough to transfer without breaking while staying soft inside.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the chilling step
    Why: Sometimes impatience or rushing leads cooks to skip chilling dough, thinking it’s unnecessary.
    Fix: Always chill the dough for at least 30 minutes to prevent excessive spreading and achieve a soft, thick cookie. If you’re short on time, pop it in the freezer for 15 minutes instead.
  • Overmixing after adding flour
    Why: It’s easy to think thorough mixing is better, but overmixing develops gluten.
    Fix: Mix just until the flour disappears to keep cookies tender. Use a spatula to fold gently if needed.
  • Using butter that’s too soft or melted
    Why: Some think melted butter speeds up baking or makes cookies softer.
    Fix: Use room temperature butter for proper creaming. Melting butter changes the dough’s fat structure, leading to flat, greasy cookies.
  • Baking too long
    Why: Waiting for the cookies to look fully firm can lead to overbaking.
    Fix: Remove cookies when edges are set but centers still look soft. They’ll firm up as they cool, staying soft inside.
  • Not evenly distributing spices
    Why: Adding spices directly without mixing can cause clumps or uneven flavor.
    Fix: Sift or thoroughly whisk spices with flour before adding to wet ingredients for even flavor.

Variations

  • Cardamom and Orange Zest Cookies: Add 1 tablespoon of finely grated orange zest to the dough to brighten the warm spices with citrus notes. The zest complements cardamom beautifully and adds a fresh aroma. Expect a slightly more fragrant and lively cookie.
    Mix zest with the dry ingredients to evenly distribute. Baking time remains the same.
  • Soft Brown Butter Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Swap regular butter for brown butter to add a nutty, caramel-like depth. Brown butter enhances the cookie’s flavor and adds richness. The texture stays soft but with a more complex taste.
    Cool the brown butter completely before creaming with sugar to avoid melting the sugar.
  • Gluten-Free Soft Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Use a 1-to-1 gluten-free baking flour blend instead of all-purpose flour. This makes the cookies accessible for gluten-sensitive eaters without sacrificing softness or flavor. Texture may be slightly different but still delicious.
    Add 1/4 teaspoon xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t contain it for better structure.
  • Vegan Soft Cinnamon Sugar Cookies: Replace butter with vegan margarine and use a flax egg (1 tablespoon ground flaxseed + 3 tablespoons water) instead of a chicken egg. These cookies remain soft and flavorful with a subtle plant-based richness.
    Chill the vegan dough longer if it feels too soft before baking.
  • Spiced Soft Cinnamon Sugar Cookies with Nutmeg and Cloves: Add 1/4 teaspoon ground nutmeg and a pinch of ground cloves to the dry spices for a deeper spice profile. These extra warm spices make the cookies perfect for holiday baking.
    Mix spices well with flour to avoid overpowering spots.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to preserve softness. If they start to harden, a quick zap in the microwave softens them nicely.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Cookies can be stored in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 7 days. They may firm up and lose some softness when cold, so warm slightly before serving to restore their tender texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet until solid, then transfer to a freezer-safe bag or container for up to 3 months. Thaw at room temperature or warm gently in the oven to refresh softness.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat cookies in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes or microwave for 10-15 seconds with a damp paper towel to keep moisture. Avoid overheating, which can dry them out.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare the dough up to 2 days ahead and keep it chilled in the fridge. When ready, roll, sugar-coat, and bake fresh for best texture and flavor. Dough can also be frozen for longer storage.

FAQ

Can I use salted butter instead of unsalted?
Yes, but reduce added salt in the recipe slightly or omit it to avoid over-salting. Unsalted butter gives you more control over the salt level, but salted butter works fine if adjusted.
Why are my cookies spreading too much?
This usually happens if the dough wasn’t chilled or the butter was too soft or melted. Chilling the dough firms the fat and prevents excessive spreading during baking.
Can I substitute cardamom with another spice?
If you don’t have cardamom, a pinch of nutmeg or allspice can add warm spice notes, though the flavor won’t be quite the same. Cardamom’s unique floral aroma is hard to replicate exactly.
How do I keep cookies soft longer?
Store them in an airtight container with a slice of bread or apple to maintain moisture. Avoid refrigerating unless necessary, as cold can dry cookies out.
Can I make these cookies gluten-free?
Yes, using a 1-to-1 gluten-free flour blend works well. Adding xanthan gum helps with structure and texture to mimic all-purpose flour.
Is it necessary to roll cookies in sugar before baking?
While optional, rolling in sugar creates a lovely crisp crust that contrasts the soft center. It also adds a bit of sparkle and sweetness on the outside.
Why are my cookies dense instead of soft?
Overmixing the dough after adding flour develops gluten, causing density. Also, not creaming butter and sugar enough or skipping leavening can make cookies heavy.
Can I freeze the cookie dough?
Absolutely! Freeze dough balls on a tray first, then store in a bag for up to 3 months. Bake from frozen, adding a couple of extra minutes to baking time.
