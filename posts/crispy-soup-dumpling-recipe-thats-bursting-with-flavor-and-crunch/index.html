Crispy Soup Dumpling Recipe That’s Bursting With Flavor and Crunch

Molly Dupont
Published on April 30, 2026
40 min Prep Time
20 min Time
4 Servings
Medium Difficulty

These crispy soup dumplings are a fun twist on the classic steamed version, offering a satisfying crunch outside with juicy, savory broth inside. The secret? Pan-frying right after steaming to get that golden crust. Perfect for sharing at dinner or as a special appetizer!

Pro Tips

Rest the dough for at least 30 minutes before rolling.
Resting relaxes the gluten strands formed during kneading, making the dough easier to stretch and roll thin without snapping back. Skipping this step often leads to tough, elastic dough that’s hard to shape and pleat properly.
Chill the broth until fully gelled before dicing and mixing into filling.
Gelled broth holds its shape inside the filling, preventing leaks during folding and steaming. If the broth is too liquid, it will dissolve into the meat mixture and leak out, ruining the soup effect and causing soggy wrappers.
Roll wrappers thinner at the edges than the center.
Thinner edges make pleating easier and result in a tender bite. The thicker center supports the filling and broth. Uniform thickness prevents tearing and ensures the dumplings cook evenly.
Use a non-stick skillet for pan-frying to prevent sticking and tearing.
Soup dumplings are delicate after steaming, and sticking to the pan can cause them to break apart. A non-stick surface allows the bottoms to crisp up nicely while keeping the dumplings intact.
Add water and cover the pan immediately after starting to fry.
This creates steam that finishes cooking the filling and keeps the dumplings moist inside. It also prevents the bottoms from burning while crisping them perfectly.
Don’t overfill the dumplings to avoid bursting during cooking.
Too much filling stretches the wrapper thin and increases the chance of leaks or breaks. Filling should be generous but manageable to fold securely.
Serve dumplings immediately after cooking.
The soup inside cools and thickens quickly, and the crispy bottom loses its crunch. Eating them fresh ensures you get the full textural and flavor experience.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using broth that isn’t gelatinized

Why it happens Many cooks try to use liquid broth directly, not realizing it needs to be chilled until it gels to hold inside the dumpling.

Fix Simmer pork bones to make a rich broth, then refrigerate it overnight until it solidifies. Dice the solid gel and fold into the filling. This ensures the soup stays inside until steamed.

Wrappers too thick or too thin

Why it happens Without practice, it’s easy to roll uneven dough, resulting in tough or breaking wrappers.

Fix Roll dough thinner at edges and slightly thicker in the center. Rest dough well and use a light dusting of flour to prevent sticking.

Sealing dumplings poorly causing leaks

Why it happens Rushing the pleating or not pinching edges tightly lets broth escape during cooking.

Fix Take time to pleat carefully, pressing edges firmly. Use a little water on the wrapper edge to help seal if needed.

Skipping the pan-fry step

Why it happens Some assume steaming alone is enough, missing the crispy texture contrast.

Fix After steaming, pan-fry with oil and water to get that golden crisp bottom that makes these dumplings special.

Overcrowding the steamer

Why it happens Trying to steam too many dumplings at once causes them to stick together and cook unevenly.

Fix Arrange dumplings with space between them and steam in batches if needed to keep shape and texture perfect.

Variations

Shrimp and Pork Crispy Soup Dumplings

Add finely chopped shrimp to the pork filling for a sweet seafood twist. The shrimp adds a nice texture contrast and delicate flavor that pairs beautifully with the crispy bottom and savory broth.

Note: Shrimp cooks quickly, so chop small and mix gently to avoid breaking up the pieces. Adjust steaming time slightly shorter if needed.

Vegetarian Crispy Soup Dumplings

Replace pork with finely chopped mushrooms and tofu mixed with vegetable broth gelatin. Season with soy sauce and sesame oil for umami flavor. These dumplings offer a satisfying vegetarian alternative with the same juicy soup inside.

Note: Use agar-agar or vegetarian gelatin substitute to create the broth gel. Steaming time remains the same.

Spicy Crispy Soup Dumplings

Add chili oil and minced garlic to the filling for a spicy kick. Serve with a dipping sauce made from soy sauce, vinegar, and chili flakes. This variation adds heat and complexity to the classic recipe.

Note: Be cautious with chili quantities to balance spice without overpowering the broth flavor.

Chicken and Ginger Crispy Soup Dumplings

Use ground chicken instead of pork and increase the ginger for a fresh, zesty flavor. This lighter version is equally juicy and pairs well with a soy-ginger dipping sauce.

Note: Chicken tends to be leaner; consider adding a bit of chicken fat or broth gel for moisture.

Cheese and Pork Crispy Soup Dumplings

Mix shredded mozzarella or cream cheese into the pork filling for a creamy surprise inside. The cheese melts with the broth, creating a rich, indulgent bite that’s fun and unexpected.

Note: Use cheese sparingly to prevent overpowering the broth flavor and maintain the dumpling’s structure.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Dumplings should not be left at room temperature for more than 2 hours to avoid bacterial growth. Cover with a damp cloth if waiting briefly before cooking.

Refrigerator

Store cooked dumplings in an airtight container for up to 2 days. They may lose some crispiness when cold but reheat gently in a pan to restore texture.

Freezer

Freeze uncooked dumplings on a tray until solid, then transfer to a sealed bag for up to 3 months. Steam directly from frozen, adding extra cooking time. Avoid thawing before cooking to prevent sogginess.

Reheating

Reheat in a non-stick skillet with a splash of oil and a few tablespoons of water. Cover to steam and crisp the bottoms again. Avoid microwaving alone as it makes wrappers chewy and soggy.

Make Ahead

Prepare filling and broth gel 1-2 days ahead. Make dough and assemble dumplings a day before, then freeze or refrigerate. Cook fresh or steam and pan-fry just before serving for best results.

FAQ

Can I use store-bought broth instead of homemade?
Store-bought broth often lacks the gelatin needed to create the soup inside the dumplings. To fix this, you can add powdered gelatin to the broth and chill it until it gels. This helps achieve that classic juicy burst.
How thin should I roll the dumpling wrappers?
Aim for about 1-2 millimeters thickness, thinner at the edges and slightly thicker in the center. This balance allows the wrapper to be delicate but sturdy enough to hold the filling and soup without breaking.
What if my dumplings leak during steaming?
Leaks usually happen if the broth isn’t fully gelled or the wrappers aren’t sealed tightly. Make sure your broth is solid before mixing and pinch the edges firmly when pleating to prevent leaks.
Can I freeze the dumplings before cooking?
Yes! Freeze assembled dumplings on a tray until firm, then transfer to a freezer bag. Steam them from frozen, adding a few extra minutes to the cooking time. This method keeps the shape and texture intact.
How do I prevent dumplings from sticking to the steamer?
Line your steamer basket with parchment paper punched with holes or use cabbage leaves. Lightly oil the surface as well to keep dumplings from sticking without affecting steaming.
What’s the best oil for pan-frying the dumplings?
Use a neutral oil with a high smoke point like canola, vegetable, or sunflower oil. These oils crisp the bottoms nicely without burning or adding unwanted flavors.
Can I make the filling vegetarian?
Absolutely! Use finely chopped mushrooms, tofu, and a vegetable broth gel instead of pork. Season well with soy sauce and sesame oil to keep the umami depth.
How do I eat soup dumplings without spilling the broth?
Use chopsticks to pick up the dumpling gently and place it on a spoon. Bite a small hole to sip the broth first, then enjoy the rest with dipping sauce.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 dumplings, 4 servings

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 3/4 cup boiling water
  • 1/4 cup cold water
  • 1/2 lb ground pork
  • 1 cup pork broth or gelatinized pork stock, chilled
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil
  • 1 teaspoon grated ginger
  • 2 green onions, finely chopped
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • 1/2 teaspoon white pepper
  • Vegetable oil for frying

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by making the dough: pour the boiling water over the sifted flour and stir quickly with chopsticks or a fork until combined. Then add the cold water and knead the dough on a clean surface until smooth and elastic, about 8-10 minutes. Cover it with a damp cloth and let it rest for at least 30 minutes. This resting period is crucial because it relaxes the gluten, making the dough easier to roll out thinly without tearing.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: The dough should feel smooth, soft, and not sticky to the touch.
Step 2
Prepare the filling by mixing ground pork with soy sauce, sesame oil, grated ginger, sugar, white pepper, and chopped green onions in a bowl. Then fold in the chilled diced pork broth gel cubes gently so they stay intact. This gelatin melts during steaming, creating the soup inside the dumpling. Be careful not to mix too vigorously or the broth cubes will dissolve prematurely.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: The filling should be well combined and slightly sticky but with visible broth cubes.
Step 3
Divide the dough into small equal portions and roll each into thin circles about 3 inches in diameter. Try to make the edges thinner than the center to allow for pleating and a tender bite. If the dough springs back while rolling, let it rest a few more minutes. Proper wrapper thickness is key to holding the soup without breaking.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Wrappers should be thin, flexible, and slightly translucent at the edges.
Step 4
Place a tablespoon of filling in the center of each wrapper. Carefully pleat the edges around the filling, pinching to seal at the top. The pleats not only create the classic dumpling shape but also help trap the soup inside. Make sure the seal is tight to avoid leaks during cooking.
⏱ Time: 20 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dumplings should stand upright with a neat pleated top and no visible gaps.
Step 5
Prepare a steamer lined with parchment paper or cabbage leaves to prevent sticking. Arrange the dumplings with space between them and steam over boiling water for about 8-10 minutes. Steaming cooks the dough and melts the broth cubes inside. Avoid opening the lid too often to keep the steam steady.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dumpling wrappers turn slightly translucent and the filling is cooked through.
Step 6
Heat a non-stick skillet over medium heat and add a tablespoon of vegetable oil. Once hot, carefully transfer steamed dumplings to the pan, flat side down. Fry for 2-3 minutes until the bottoms turn golden brown and crispy. This pan-frying step adds that irresistible crunch and contrasts wonderfully with the soft tops.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Bottoms are golden brown and crisp without burning.
Step 7
Add about 1/4 cup water to the pan and immediately cover with a lid to steam the dumplings further. This ensures the filling stays juicy while crisping the bottoms. Cook until the water evaporates completely, about 3-4 minutes. Watch closely to avoid drying out the dumplings.
⏱ Time: 4 minutes
✓ Doneness: Water is fully evaporated and dumplings are tender but not soggy.
Step 8
Remove the lid and let the dumplings cook uncovered for another minute to re-crisp the bottoms if needed. This final step ensures the perfect balance of textures: crispy base, soft wrapper, and juicy broth inside. Serve immediately with dipping sauce like black vinegar and ginger.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dumplings are hot, crispy on bottom, and juicy inside.
Step 9
Enjoy your crispy soup dumplings fresh! They’re best eaten right away while the broth is still hot and the crust is crunchy. Use chopsticks carefully to pick them up without piercing the skin. Dip lightly and savor each burst of flavor.
⏱ Time: Immediate
✓ Doneness: Dumplings hold together when picked up and release hot broth when bitten.
