Spring Lemon Drizzle Cake with Fresh Blueberries That Bursts with Flavor

This lemon drizzle cake is light, zesty, and studded with juicy fresh blueberries. The drizzle soaks in perfectly, making it moist and tangy. Ideal for a sunny afternoon tea or springtime dessert!

Introduction

I first made this spring lemon drizzle cake with fresh blueberries a few years ago when I wanted a bright, fresh dessert to celebrate the season. What I love about this version is how the lemon zest and juice really come through without being overpowering. The blueberries add a lovely burst of juiciness that pairs perfectly with the tangy drizzle. I’ve tried other lemon cakes before, but the trick I learned here is to fold the blueberries gently into the batter so they don’t break and turn everything purple. Also, making sure the drizzle soaks in while the cake is still warm keeps it moist and flavorful. The finished cake is light, tender, and just sweet enough with a lovely citrus zing. It’s become my go-to for springtime get-togethers or just a weekend treat with a cup of tea. If you’re a fan of lemon desserts, this one’s definitely worth trying!

What You'll Need

One 8-inch round cake, 8 servings

  • 225g unsalted butter, softened
  • 225g caster sugar
  • 4 large eggs
  • 225g self-raising flour
  • 2 tsp baking powder
  • 2 lemons, zest and juice separated
  • 150g fresh blueberries
  • 100g icing sugar

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 180°C (350°F) and grease an 8-inch round cake tin. This step ensures your cake doesn’t stick and bakes evenly. Greasing the tin thoroughly and lining it with parchment paper will make removing the cake a breeze. If you skip this, your cake might tear or crumble when you try to take it out, so don’t rush it!
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cake tin is fully greased and lined with parchment paper, ready for batter
Step 2
Cream together the softened butter and caster sugar until pale and fluffy. This usually takes about 4-5 minutes with an electric mixer on medium speed. Creaming incorporates air, which helps the cake rise and gives it a light texture. If you don’t cream long enough, the cake might turn out dense and heavy.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter and sugar mixture is pale, light, and fluffy
Step 3
Beat in the eggs one at a time, mixing well after each addition. Adding the eggs gradually helps prevent the batter from curdling and ensures a smooth texture. If the batter looks separated or curdled, add a tablespoon of the flour to bring it back together.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is smooth and well combined, no lumps or separation
Step 4
Sift together the self-raising flour and baking powder, then gently fold this into the batter. Folding rather than stirring preserves the air bubbles you created when creaming, which keeps the cake light. Overmixing at this stage can make the cake tough.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flour is fully incorporated with no streaks, batter is smooth
Step 5
Add the lemon zest and fold in the fresh blueberries carefully. The zest adds vibrant citrus flavor, while folding the blueberries gently prevents them from bursting and coloring the batter purple. If you mix too hard, the batter will turn blue and the texture will be uneven.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Blueberries are evenly distributed without breaking apart
Step 6
Pour the batter into the prepared tin and smooth the top with a spatula. This helps the cake bake evenly and gives a nice flat surface for the drizzle later. Avoid tapping the tin too hard, or you’ll lose some air bubbles.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is level and smooth in the tin
Step 7
Bake in the preheated oven for 35-40 minutes, or until a skewer inserted into the center comes out clean. Ovens vary, so start checking at 35 minutes to avoid overbaking, which dries out the cake. The cake should be golden and spring back slightly when touched.
⏱ Time: 35-40 minutes
✓ Doneness: Golden top, skewer comes out clean, cake springs back to touch
Step 8
While the cake is baking, mix the lemon juice with the icing sugar to make the drizzle. This simple syrup adds moisture and a tangy sweetness that makes the cake irresistible. Adjust the consistency by adding more juice or sugar if needed.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Drizzle is smooth and pourable but not too runny
Step 9
Once the cake is out of the oven, prick the top all over with a skewer and pour the lemon drizzle evenly over it while it’s still warm. This allows the syrup to soak in deeply, keeping the cake moist and flavorful. If you wait until it’s cool, the drizzle will just sit on top.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Drizzle absorbed into cake, surface slightly glossy and moist

Pro Tips

Use room temperature ingredients for better mixing.
Butter and eggs at room temperature blend more easily, creating a smooth, homogenous batter that traps air for a lighter cake. Cold ingredients can cause the batter to curdle or mix unevenly, resulting in a dense texture.
Fold blueberries gently to keep them intact.
Blueberries release juice if overmixed, turning the batter purple and making the cake soggy. Folding carefully preserves their shape and prevents bleeding, ensuring pretty speckles and bursts of flavor in each slice.
Prick the cake while warm before adding drizzle.
Warm cake absorbs the lemon syrup deeply, making it moist and flavorful. If you wait for the cake to cool, the drizzle will just sit on top, leaving the cake dry underneath.
Line your cake tin with parchment paper.
Lining prevents sticking and makes removing the cake easier, preserving its shape and surface. Greasing alone might not be enough, especially with sticky drizzles.
Sift flour and baking powder together before folding.
Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking powder, which promotes an even rise and a tender crumb. Skipping this can cause lumps or uneven texture.
Don’t overbake to keep the cake moist.
Overbaking dries out the cake, making it crumbly and less enjoyable. Check with a skewer early and remove as soon as it comes out clean to retain moisture.
Use fresh lemons for zest and juice.
Fresh lemons provide vibrant oils and acidity that bottled juice lacks. This freshness is key to the cake’s bright, tangy flavor.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overmixing the batter after adding flour

Why it happens Many assume mixing longer makes the batter smoother, but overmixing develops gluten, leading to a tough cake.

Fix Fold the flour in gently just until combined using a spatula. Stop mixing as soon as the flour disappears to keep the crumb tender.

Using cold butter or eggs

Why it happens Some skip letting ingredients warm up, causing uneven mixing and curdled batter.

Fix Bring butter and eggs to room temperature before starting. This helps create a smooth, aerated batter for a light cake.

Adding blueberries too early or mixing too hard

Why it happens Rushing to add berries or stirring vigorously can break them, turning the batter purple and soggy.

Fix Fold blueberries in gently at the end to keep them intact and evenly distributed.

Not pricking cake before drizzle

Why it happens Skipping this step means the drizzle just pools on top, not soaking in for moistness.

Fix Use a skewer to poke holes all over the warm cake so the syrup seeps in beautifully.

Overbaking the cake

Why it happens Leaving the cake in too long dries it out and dulls the lemon flavor.

Fix Check doneness early with a skewer and remove as soon as it comes out clean to keep the cake moist.

Variations

Gluten-Free Lemon Blueberry Cake

Swap regular self-raising flour for a gluten-free blend that includes xanthan gum. The texture will be slightly denser but still moist and flavorful. Use fresh blueberries as usual for bursts of sweetness.

Note: Gluten-free flours absorb liquid differently; you may need to add a tablespoon more lemon juice or a splash of milk to keep batter moist.

Lemon Drizzle Cake with Blueberry Compote

Instead of folding fresh blueberries into the batter, make a quick compote to serve alongside or on top. This adds a syrupy, fruity layer without risking color bleed in the cake.

Note: Cook blueberries with a little sugar and lemon juice until thickened; cool before serving to keep freshness.

Vegan Lemon Blueberry Cake

Replace butter with vegan margarine and eggs with flax eggs (1 tbsp ground flax + 3 tbsp water per egg). The cake will be slightly denser but still deliciously moist with bright lemon flavor.

Note: Flax eggs need time to gel before mixing; be mindful of slightly longer baking time and check doneness carefully.

Lemon Poppy Seed Blueberry Cake

Add 2 tablespoons of poppy seeds to the batter for a delightful crunch and nutty flavor that complements the lemon and blueberries beautifully.

Note: Fold poppy seeds in with the flour to distribute evenly without affecting batter consistency.

Lemon Drizzle Bundt Cake with Blueberries

Bake the batter in a bundt pan for a stunning presentation. The shape allows more surface area for the drizzle to soak in, intensifying the lemon flavor.

Note: Increase baking time by 5-10 minutes and check with a skewer for doneness due to thicker cake shape.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the cake covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Keep it wrapped tightly with plastic wrap or in an airtight container to prevent drying out. The drizzle helps keep it moist but avoid leaving uncovered.

Refrigerator

Keep the cake in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. The texture firms up slightly and flavors meld, but bring to room temperature before serving for best taste.

Freezer

Wrap the cooled cake tightly in plastic wrap and aluminum foil, then freeze for up to 2 months. To thaw, leave in the fridge overnight then bring to room temperature before serving to restore moisture.

Reheating

Warm slices gently in the microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a low oven (150°C/300°F) for 10 minutes. Avoid overheating to prevent drying. A light brush of lemon juice or a pat of butter before warming can refresh moisture.

Make Ahead

Bake the cake a day ahead and store covered at room temperature. Prepare the lemon drizzle syrup separately and pour over just before serving to keep the topping fresh and vibrant.

FAQ

Can I use frozen blueberries instead of fresh?
Yes, but thaw and drain them well before folding into the batter to avoid excess moisture that can make the cake soggy. Also, fold gently to prevent the berries from breaking and discoloring the batter.
What’s the best way to zest lemons?
Use a microplane or fine grater to zest only the yellow outer peel, avoiding the bitter white pith underneath. Fresh zest adds bright citrus oils that are essential for the cake’s flavor.
Can I make this cake dairy-free?
Absolutely! Substitute butter with a dairy-free margarine or coconut oil. The texture might be slightly different but still moist and delicious. Just make sure your substitute is solid at room temperature for proper creaming.
How do I know when the cake is done?
Insert a skewer or toothpick into the center; it should come out clean or with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter. The cake should also spring back lightly when pressed gently on top.
What if my cake sinks in the middle?
This often happens if the oven temperature is too low or the cake is underbaked. Make sure your oven is properly preheated and test doneness with a skewer before removing the cake.
Can I use lemon extract instead of fresh lemons?
You can, but fresh lemon zest and juice provide a much brighter, fresher flavor. Extracts tend to be more artificial and less vibrant, so fresh lemons are recommended for best results.
How do I prevent blueberries from sinking to the bottom?
Toss blueberries lightly in a bit of flour before folding into the batter. This helps suspend them evenly throughout the cake instead of sinking.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, but bake in a larger pan and increase baking time accordingly. Check doneness early and often to avoid over or underbaking.
