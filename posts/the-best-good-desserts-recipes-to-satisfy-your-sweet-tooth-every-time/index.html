What You'll Need

Approximately 4 servings

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1/2 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1/4 cup milk

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and grease an 8-inch square baking pan. This step is crucial because a properly preheated oven ensures even baking and that perfect rise. Greasing the pan prevents sticking, so your dessert comes out clean and intact. Avoid skipping this or using too little grease, which can cause your dessert to stick and break when removing.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Oven reaches stable 350°F and pan is evenly coated with butter or non-stick spray
Step 2
In a large bowl, cream together the softened butter and sugar until light and fluffy. This usually takes about 3-4 minutes with an electric mixer. Creaming incorporates air, which helps create a tender texture. If you rush this or don’t cream well, your dessert might turn out dense or heavy.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture is pale, fluffy, and has increased in volume
Step 3
Beat in the eggs one at a time, mixing well after each addition. This helps emulsify the batter and ensures an even texture. Adding eggs too quickly can cause the mixture to curdle, so patience here pays off.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Smooth, glossy batter with no streaks of egg whites
Step 4
Stir in the vanilla extract for flavor depth. Vanilla enhances sweetness and rounds out the taste, making the dessert more aromatic and inviting.
⏱ Time: 1 minute
✓ Doneness: Vanilla evenly distributed in batter
Step 5
In a separate bowl, whisk together the flour, baking powder, and salt. This ensures the leavening agent and salt are evenly mixed, which is key for consistent rise and balanced flavor.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are combined with no lumps
Step 6
Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, alternating with the milk. Start and end with the dry ingredients. This gentle folding prevents overmixing, which can make the dessert tough.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter is smooth and just combined, no flour streaks
Step 7
Pour the batter into the prepared pan and smooth the top with a spatula. Even spreading ensures uniform baking and a pretty final look.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Batter surface is level and smooth
Step 8
Bake in the preheated oven for 25-30 minutes until a toothpick inserted in the center comes out clean or with a few moist crumbs. Avoid opening the oven door too often, which can cause the dessert to collapse.
⏱ Time: 25-30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Golden brown top and toothpick test passes
Step 9
Let the dessert cool in the pan for at least 15 minutes before slicing. Cooling allows the structure to set, so it cuts cleanly without crumbling.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dessert is warm but firm to the touch

Pro Tips

Always measure your flour properly by spooning it into the measuring cup and leveling off with a knife.
Scooping flour directly can compact it, leading to too much flour and a dry, dense dessert. Accurate measurement ensures the right texture and moisture balance.
Use room temperature eggs and butter for better mixing.
Cold ingredients don’t combine well, which can cause uneven texture and poor rise. Room temperature ingredients blend smoothly, creating a uniform batter.
Don’t overmix the batter once you add the flour.
Overmixing develops gluten, making the dessert tough instead of tender. Mix just until combined to keep it soft and fluffy.
Preheat your oven fully before baking.
Starting in a hot oven ensures proper rise and crust formation. Baking in a cold oven can lead to uneven cooking and dense texture.
Let your dessert cool completely before slicing.
Cooling allows the structure to set, preventing crumbling and making clean slices easier.
Use fresh baking powder for best results.
Old baking powder loses potency, causing flat and dense desserts. Check the date and replace if necessary.
Grease your pan well or line with parchment paper.
This prevents sticking, so your dessert comes out in perfect shape without breaking apart.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Overmixing the batter after adding flour

Why it happens Many home bakers think mixing longer ensures better blending, but it actually develops gluten, making desserts tough.

Fix Mix just until the flour disappears. Stop as soon as the batter looks uniform to keep the texture light and tender.

Using cold butter or eggs

Why it happens Sometimes ingredients are used straight from the fridge out of convenience or forgetfulness.

Fix Let butter and eggs sit at room temperature for 30 minutes before starting. This helps them incorporate smoothly for a better batter.

Not preheating the oven

Why it happens Rushing or multitasking can lead to baking in an oven that hasn’t reached the right temperature.

Fix Always preheat fully before baking. Use an oven thermometer if your oven’s gauge is unreliable.

Incorrect flour measurement

Why it happens Scooping flour directly with a measuring cup packs it down, leading to too much flour.

Fix Spoon flour into your measuring cup and level it off with a knife for accuracy.

Opening the oven door too often

Why it happens Curiosity or impatience causes frequent checks, which drops oven temperature suddenly.

Fix Trust the timer and only check near the end of baking to avoid collapsing.

Variations

Chocolate Chip Good Desserts

Add 1 cup of chocolate chips to the batter for a classic twist. The chips melt during baking, adding pockets of gooey chocolate that everyone loves.

Note: Fold chips in gently at the end to avoid breaking them up and distribute evenly.

Lemon Zest Good Desserts

Mix in the zest of one lemon and a tablespoon of lemon juice for a fresh, tangy flavor. It brightens the dessert and pairs well with a dusting of powdered sugar.

Note: Avoid adding too much liquid lemon juice to maintain batter consistency.

Nutty Good Desserts

Stir in 1/2 cup chopped walnuts or pecans for a crunchy texture contrast. Toast the nuts lightly beforehand to enhance their flavor.

Note: Fold nuts in carefully to avoid overmixing the batter.

Berry Swirl Good Desserts

Drop spoonfuls of your favorite berry jam into the batter and swirl with a knife before baking. This adds a fruity burst and pretty marbled effect.

Note: Don’t over-swirl or the jam will blend completely, losing the swirl pattern.

Gluten-Free Good Desserts

Substitute the all-purpose flour with a gluten-free blend. The texture will be slightly different but still moist and delicious.

Note: Add 1/4 teaspoon xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t contain it for better structure.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store the dessert covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Make sure it’s in an airtight container or wrapped tightly to prevent drying out.

Refrigerator

Keep leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. The texture may firm up, so let it come to room temperature before serving or warm briefly in the microwave.

Freezer

Wrap the dessert tightly in plastic wrap and foil, then freeze for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge and warm gently before serving to restore softness.

Reheating

Reheat slices in the microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes. Add a tiny splash of milk if needed to keep moist. Avoid overheating which can dry it out.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the batter a day ahead and keep it covered in the fridge. Stir gently before baking. This saves time and often improves flavor as ingredients meld.

FAQ

Can I substitute butter with oil in this dessert?
Yes, you can use a neutral oil like vegetable or canola oil, but the texture will be a bit different—oil makes baked goods moister and denser, while butter adds flavor and a tender crumb. Use about 3/4 the amount of butter called for.
What can I do if my dessert turns out too dry?
Dryness often results from overbaking or too much flour. Next time, check the dessert a few minutes before the timer and remove it as soon as a toothpick comes out mostly clean. You can also brush a little simple syrup on slices to add moisture.
Can I double this recipe?
Absolutely! Just double all ingredients and use a larger baking pan or two pans. Keep an eye on baking time—it may need a few extra minutes. Test doneness with a toothpick as usual.
Is it okay to use brown sugar instead of granulated sugar?
Brown sugar adds moisture and a deeper flavor thanks to its molasses content. You can substitute it 1:1, but expect a slightly denser texture and a caramel note in the dessert.
Why does my dessert sometimes sink in the middle?
This usually happens if the batter is underbaked or the oven temperature is too low. Make sure your oven is fully preheated and bake until the center is set. Avoid opening the oven door early, which can cause collapse.
Can I add mix-ins like nuts or chocolate chips?
Yes! Fold in about 1 cup of mix-ins gently at the end of mixing. This adds texture and flavor but be careful not to overmix the batter afterward.
How do I store leftover dessert to keep it fresh?
Store leftovers in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days or in the fridge for up to 4 days. For longer storage, freeze tightly wrapped for up to 2 months.
What’s the best way to reheat this dessert?
Microwave slices for 15-20 seconds or warm in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes. Adding a splash of milk before reheating helps keep it moist.
Can I make this dessert vegan?
You can substitute eggs with flax eggs (1 tbsp flaxseed meal + 3 tbsp water per egg) and use vegan butter or coconut oil. The texture may be slightly different but still tasty.
Why is creaming butter and sugar important?
Creaming traps air bubbles that help the dessert rise and create a tender crumb. Skipping this step or not creaming well can result in dense, heavy baked goods.
