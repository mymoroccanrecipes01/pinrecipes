Moist Strawberry Shortcake Layer Cake with Whipped Cream Frosting You'll Love

Molly Dupont Published on May 30, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 8 servings

This strawberry shortcake layer cake is super moist and bursting with fresh berries. The whipped cream frosting is light and fluffy, making it perfect for spring gatherings or a sweet weekend treat.

Introduction

I first made this strawberry shortcake layer cake a few summers ago when I wanted to elevate the classic shortcake into something a bit more special for a family birthday. What makes this version stand out is the cake’s incredible moisture and tender crumb, which I achieve by using whole milk at room temperature and beating the butter and sugar until super fluffy. The whipped cream frosting is not your typical heavy buttercream; it’s light, airy, and just sweet enough to complement the fresh strawberries without overpowering them. I've tried other whipped cream frostings before, but adding a touch of vanilla and powdered sugar really balances the flavors and keeps it stable enough to hold the layers together. The strawberry slices, macerated with a bit of sugar, add a juicy brightness that cuts through the richness of the cake and cream. When you slice into it, you get this beautiful contrast of textures and flavors that feels like a perfect summer day in dessert form. Honestly, it’s become a favorite for birthdays and weekend treats alike. I love how it looks so impressive but is pretty straightforward to make. Just be sure to whip the cream just right and don’t rush the cake baking to get that perfect rise and softness. It’s a recipe I’m excited to share because it’s truly a crowd-pleaser that feels homemade and special all at once.

Why This Works

What makes this strawberry shortcake layer cake truly stand out is the balance between moisture, texture, and flavor. The creaming of butter and sugar is fundamental because it traps air bubbles that expand during baking, giving the cake a light crumb. Using whole milk instead of water or skim milk adds fat and richness, which tenderizes the gluten network and keeps the cake moist longer. The alternation of dry and wet ingredients during mixing minimizes gluten formation, preventing toughness.

Macerating the strawberries with sugar is more than just flavor enhancement. This process draws out the fruit's natural juices, creating a syrup that moistens the cake layers from within and adds a fresh, tangy contrast to the sweet cream. The whipped cream frosting is stabilized with powdered sugar and chilled cream, which helps it hold shape without heaviness, making it complementary rather than overpowering.

The baking temperature and time are carefully chosen to ensure the cake cooks evenly. Too hot, and the outside will brown too fast while the inside remains undercooked; too low, and the crumb becomes dense. Cooling the cake fully before frosting prevents the cream from melting and sliding off, which is a common pitfall. Altogether, these techniques work harmoniously to produce a cake that's moist, tender, and bursting with fresh strawberry flavor.

Finally, the layering and assembly create a beautiful presentation and a balanced bite of cake, cream, and fruit. The whipped cream’s lightness offsets the cake’s richness, while the fresh strawberries add texture and brightness. This recipe is a perfect example of how attention to detail at each step elevates a classic dessert into something memorable.

  • Choose a good quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for the best texture. Bleached flour can sometimes give a slightly off flavor or affect the crumb. Sifting the flour aerates it and removes lumps, which helps the cake rise evenly and stay tender.
  • Use unsalted butter at room temperature so you can control the saltiness of the cake. Softened butter creams better with sugar, trapping air that helps the cake rise. Avoid melted butter here, as it won’t trap air and will lead to a denser cake.
  • Pick ripe, fragrant strawberries for the best flavor. Freshness is key since they provide both flavor and moisture. Hull and slice them evenly to macerate properly and ensure uniform distribution in the cake layers.
  • Use cold, high-fat heavy cream for the frosting. The fat content is essential for whipping and stability. Lower-fat creams won’t whip well or hold shape, resulting in a runny frosting that won’t support the cake layers.
  • Regular granulated sugar is used for creaming and macerating. It dissolves well during creaming, helping to aerate the batter. When macerating strawberries, sugar draws out juices and enhances natural sweetness without overpowering.

Ingredients

  • 2 1/2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 2 1/2 tsp baking powder
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 3/4 cups granulated sugar
  • 4 large eggs, room temperature
  • 1 tbsp vanilla extract
  • 1 cup whole milk, room temperature
  • 4 cups fresh strawberries, hulled and sliced
  • 2 tbsp granulated sugar (for strawberries)
  • 2 cups heavy whipping cream, chilled
  • 1/4 cup powdered sugar
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract (for whipped cream)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease and flour two 8-inch round cake pans or line them with parchment paper. This step is crucial because it ensures the cakes release easily after baking and maintain their shape without sticking or breaking apart. If you skip greasing or flouring, the cake layers might stick and tear, ruining the presentation.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Pans feel evenly coated with no bare spots, parchment paper fits snugly inside pans
  2. Step 2

    In a medium bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, and salt. Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the leavening agents, which helps the cake rise uniformly and stay tender. Mixing dry ingredients first prevents overmixing later when combined with wet ingredients.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are fully combined with no visible clumps
  3. Step 3

    In a large bowl, beat the softened butter and granulated sugar together on medium-high speed until pale, fluffy, and light in texture—about 4-5 minutes. This creaming step is key because it incorporates air, leading to a lighter cake crumb. If you rush or underbeat, the cake can turn dense.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Butter-sugar mixture looks pale yellow and fluffy
  4. Step 4

    Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition to fully incorporate. Then mix in the vanilla extract. Adding eggs slowly helps emulsify the batter, ensuring a smooth texture and preventing curdling. Skipping this can cause a grainy batter or uneven crumb.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter looks smooth and glossy without streaks
  5. Step 5

    Alternate adding the dry ingredients and whole milk to the butter mixture, beginning and ending with the dry ingredients. Mix gently after each addition just until combined. Overmixing at this stage develops gluten and makes the cake tough. The whole milk adds moisture and richness, so don’t substitute with skim milk.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Batter is smooth, thick but pourable, no lumps
  6. Step 6

    Divide the batter evenly between the prepared pans and smooth the tops with a spatula. Bake for 28-32 minutes, or until a toothpick inserted in the center comes out clean. Oven times vary, so start checking at 28 minutes to avoid overbaking, which dries the cake out.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Golden brown edges, toothpick comes out clean or with a few moist crumbs
  7. Step 7

    While the cakes bake, toss the sliced strawberries with 2 tablespoons of sugar and let them sit at room temperature to macerate. This draws out their natural juices, creating a sweet syrup that keeps the cake moist and adds flavor contrast. Don’t skip this step, or the strawberries will taste flat.

    ⏱ 15-20 minutes✓ Strawberries look glossy and have released some juice
  8. Step 8

    For the frosting, whip the chilled heavy cream with powdered sugar and vanilla extract until soft peaks form. Be careful not to overwhip or it will turn grainy and eventually into butter. The goal is a smooth, airy cream that holds its shape but is still soft enough to spread easily.

    ⏱ 5-7 minutes✓ Cream holds soft peaks and looks fluffy and smooth
  9. Step 9

    Once the cakes are completely cool, level the tops if needed. Place one cake layer on your serving plate, spread a generous layer of whipped cream, then add a layer of macerated strawberries. Top with the second cake layer and frost the top and sides with remaining whipped cream. Decorate with extra strawberries if you like. Chill for at least an hour before serving to let flavors meld and frosting set.

    ⏱ 20 minutes + chilling✓ Cake layers are even, frosting is smooth, strawberries peek through beautifully

Pro Tips

  • Make sure your butter is softened but not melted before creaming. — Softened butter traps air bubbles when beaten with sugar, creating a fluffy batter. If the butter is too warm or melted, it won’t hold air, resulting in a dense cake.
  • Use room temperature eggs to help the batter emulsify smoothly. — Cold eggs can cause the batter to curdle or mix unevenly, affecting texture. Room temperature eggs blend better, leading to a uniform crumb and proper rise.
  • Don’t overmix the batter after adding flour to avoid toughness. — Overmixing develops gluten, which makes cake chewy or dense instead of tender. Mix just until ingredients are combined for a soft cake.
  • Chill your mixing bowl and beaters before whipping the cream. — Cold equipment helps the cream whip faster and hold its structure. Warm bowls or beaters can cause the cream to turn buttery or separate.
  • Macerate strawberries at least 15 minutes before assembling the cake. — This releases their juices, making the fruit sweeter and juicier, which moistens the cake layers beautifully.
  • Level cake layers with a serrated knife for even stacking. — Uneven layers can cause the cake to lean or collapse. Leveling ensures stability and a professional look.
  • Chill the assembled cake for at least an hour before serving. — This helps the whipped cream set and the flavors meld, making slicing easier and the cake more cohesive.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using cold milk instead of room temperature milk
    Why: Many assume cold milk is fine, but it can cause the batter to seize or mix unevenly.
    Fix: Let milk sit out for 30 minutes before using. This helps the batter emulsify and ensures even texture.
  • Overwhipping the cream until it turns grainy
    Why: It’s easy to get distracted and whip past soft peaks into butter.
    Fix: Stop whipping as soon as soft peaks form. If you overwhip, start over with fresh cream.
  • Skipping the maceration of strawberries
    Why: Some think fresh fruit is fine as-is, but maceration brings out flavor and juiciness.
    Fix: Always toss strawberries with sugar and let sit for at least 15 minutes before layering.
  • Not cooling the cake completely before frosting
    Why: Impatience leads to frosting melting and sliding off.
    Fix: Allow cakes to cool fully on racks before assembling to keep frosting stable.
  • Overbaking the cake, causing dryness
    Why: Ovens vary and timers get ignored, leading to a dry crumb.
    Fix: Start checking cake doneness 3-5 minutes before timer ends using toothpick test.

Variations

  • Lemon-Infused Strawberry Shortcake Cake: Add 1 tbsp of lemon zest to the cake batter and a teaspoon of lemon juice to the whipped cream for a bright citrus twist that pairs beautifully with strawberries. The lemon adds freshness and balances the sweetness.
    Be careful not to add too much lemon juice to the cream or it may curdle; zest is safer for flavor.
  • Gluten-Free Strawberry Shortcake Cake: Swap all-purpose flour for a gluten-free blend suitable for baking. The texture will be slightly denser but still moist. Add 1/4 tsp xanthan gum if your blend doesn’t contain it to help with structure.
    Gluten-free batters require gentle mixing and extra moisture to avoid dryness.
  • Vegan Strawberry Shortcake Cake: Use plant-based butter and substitute eggs with flax eggs (1 tbsp ground flax + 3 tbsp water per egg). Replace milk with almond or oat milk and whip coconut cream for frosting. Expect a slightly different texture but still delicious.
    Chill coconut cream overnight and whip cold for best results.
  • Chocolate Strawberry Shortcake Cake: Add 1/3 cup cocoa powder to the dry ingredients for a chocolate version. The rich cocoa complements the strawberries and whipped cream, creating a decadent treat.
    Reduce flour by 1/3 cup to keep batter balance when adding cocoa powder.
  • Strawberry Shortcake Cupcakes: Divide the batter into cupcake liners and bake for 18-22 minutes. Top each cupcake with whipped cream and macerated strawberries for individual servings perfect for parties.
    Check doneness earlier as cupcakes bake faster than cakes.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: This cake can be kept at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered loosely with a cake dome or foil to prevent drying. Avoid leaving it out longer as the whipped cream can soften and spoil.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store the cake in an airtight container or covered with plastic wrap in the fridge for up to 3 days. The whipped cream will firm up, and the cake may become slightly denser but still delicious. Serve cold or bring to room temperature before eating.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze the cake wrapped tightly in plastic wrap and then foil for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge to maintain texture. Whipped cream may separate slightly after freezing, so consider whipping fresh cream to refresh the topping.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Because of the whipped cream, reheating is not recommended. Instead, serve chilled or at room temperature for best flavor and texture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Bake the cake layers up to 2 days ahead and wrap tightly. Prepare and macerate strawberries the day of serving. Whip the cream just before assembling to ensure freshness and stability.

FAQ

Can I use frozen strawberries instead of fresh?
Frozen strawberries release a lot of water when thawed, which can make the cake soggy. If you must use frozen, thaw and drain them well, then toss with sugar to macerate. Fresh strawberries are best for texture and flavor.
How do I prevent the whipped cream from melting on the cake?
Make sure the cake is completely cooled before frosting and keep the assembled cake refrigerated until serving. Also, whip the cream to soft peaks—not too stiff—to maintain a stable but soft texture that won’t melt quickly.
Can I substitute the heavy cream with something lighter?
Heavy cream is essential for whipping and stability. Lighter creams or milk won’t whip properly and will result in a runny frosting that can’t hold the layers together.
What’s the best way to store leftover cake?
Store leftovers in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 3 days. The whipped cream may firm up, so let the cake sit at room temperature for 15-20 minutes before serving for softer texture.
Can I make this cake gluten-free?
Yes! Use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend with xanthan gum included. The texture will be slightly different but still tasty. Be gentle when mixing to avoid dryness.
How do I know when the cake is done baking?
Insert a toothpick into the center of the cake. If it comes out clean or with a few moist crumbs, it’s done. The cake should also pull slightly away from the pan edges and be golden brown.
Can I add other fruits besides strawberries?
Absolutely! Blueberries, raspberries, or sliced peaches work well. Just macerate them similarly with sugar to bring out their juices and sweetness.
Is there a way to make the whipped cream frosting more stable for longer display?
Yes, adding a small amount of cream cheese or gelatin can stabilize whipped cream for longer periods. However, this changes the texture slightly. For best results, assemble close to serving time.
