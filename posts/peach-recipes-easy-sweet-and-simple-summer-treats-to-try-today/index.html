Introduction

I've always loved peaches during the summer, but sometimes I just want a quick and easy way to enjoy their natural sweetness without spending hours in the kitchen. This peach recipe is my go-to when I want a simple dessert or snack that still feels a little special. What sets this apart is the crumbly oat topping that adds a nice texture contrast to the soft peaches. I first made a version of this years ago when I wanted a no-fuss dessert for a family get-together, and it was a hit! I learned that using cold butter and mixing it quickly keeps the topping light and flaky, which really makes the difference. The finished dish is warm, fragrant with cinnamon, and bursting with juicy peach flavor—perfect for a cozy summer evening or even a casual brunch. Plus, it’s forgiving enough that even if you’re new to baking, it’ll turn out great. I hope you enjoy making and eating it as much as my family does!

What You'll Need

Approximately 4 servings

  • 4 ripe peaches, peeled and sliced
  • 2 tablespoons brown sugar
  • 1 teaspoon cinnamon
  • 1 tablespoon lemon juice
  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 cup rolled oats
  • 1/3 cup unsalted butter, cold and diced
  • 1/4 cup granulated sugar
  • Pinch of salt

How To Do It

Step 1
Begin by peeling and slicing your ripe peaches. Peeling helps the texture be smoother and prevents any bitterness from the skin. Toss the sliced peaches with brown sugar, cinnamon, and lemon juice in a large bowl. The sugar draws out the natural juices while the lemon juice adds a bright, fresh contrast and prevents browning. Mix gently but thoroughly so all slices are evenly coated. Be careful not to mash the peaches; you want them intact to keep that juicy bite.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Peaches should look glossy and slightly softened but still hold their shape.
Step 2
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). While it warms up, prepare the oat topping by combining flour, rolled oats, granulated sugar, and a pinch of salt in a bowl. Cut in the cold, diced butter using your fingers or a pastry cutter until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs. This step is crucial because the cold butter creates a crumbly, flaky texture when baked. If the butter melts too much, the topping will be greasy and dense instead of light and crisp.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: The topping should be crumbly and hold together loosely when pinched.
Step 3
Transfer the peach mixture into a greased baking dish, spreading it evenly. Sprinkle the oat topping evenly over the peaches, covering the fruit completely. This ensures every bite has a bit of that sweet, crunchy texture. Avoid pressing the topping down; letting it stay loose helps it crisp up nicely in the oven.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: The peaches are evenly spread and the topping looks like a loose layer of crumbs.
Step 4
Place the baking dish in the preheated oven and bake for about 25-30 minutes. The goal here is to cook the peaches until bubbly and tender, and the topping golden brown and crisp. Keep an eye on the topping to prevent burning, especially towards the end. If it browns too quickly, loosely cover with foil for the remaining time.
⏱ Time: 25-30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Bubbling peach juices around edges and golden brown, crisp topping.
Step 5
Once baked, remove from the oven and let it cool slightly before serving. This resting time helps the juices thicken a bit so the dessert isn’t too runny. Serve warm, optionally with a scoop of vanilla ice cream or a dollop of whipped cream for an extra treat. The contrast of warm peaches and cold cream is just heavenly.
⏱ Time: 5-10 minutes
✓ Doneness: The dessert is warm but not hot, and juices have thickened slightly.
Step 6
If you want to make it ahead, you can prepare the peach mixture and topping separately and store them in the fridge. Assemble and bake just before serving. This keeps the topping from getting soggy and the peaches fresh. Just remember to add a few extra minutes to the baking time if your ingredients are cold from the fridge.
⏱ Time: Preparation ahead varies
✓ Doneness: Topping remains crumbly and peaches are fresh when baked.
Step 7
For a little extra flavor, try stirring in a teaspoon of vanilla extract or a splash of bourbon to the peach mixture before baking. It adds a lovely depth without overpowering the natural peach taste. Just mix it in with the sugar and lemon juice for an even distribution.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture smells fragrant and rich before baking.
Step 8
To make clean slicing easier, let the dish cool completely if you’re serving it later or want neat portions. Warm peach desserts can be a bit soft and may fall apart if cut too soon. Cooling firms it up and makes serving more manageable.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes or overnight
✓ Doneness: Dessert feels firm to the touch and slices hold shape.
Step 9
Finally, when serving leftovers, reheat gently in the microwave or oven. Add a splash of water or juice if it seems dry to refresh the fruit. Avoid overheating or it can dry out or toughen the topping. A gentle warm-up keeps the texture pleasant and juicy.
⏱ Time: 3-5 minutes reheating
✓ Doneness: Warm throughout but still moist and tender.

Pro Tips

Use ripe but firm peaches for best texture.
Ripe peaches provide sweetness and juiciness, but if they’re too soft, the dessert can become mushy. Firm peaches hold their shape better during baking, giving a pleasant bite.
Keep butter cold when making the topping.
Cold butter creates small pockets of fat that melt during baking, resulting in a flaky, crumbly texture. Warm butter will blend too much and produce a dense, greasy topping.
Don’t overmix the peach filling.
Overmixing breaks down the fruit, releasing too much juice and making the filling watery. Gentle tossing keeps slices intact and juicy.
Add a splash of lemon juice to prevent browning.
Lemon juice’s acidity slows oxidation, which keeps the peaches looking fresh and bright instead of dull or brown.
Bake until juices bubble around edges.
Bubbling juices indicate the fruit is cooked through and sugars have caramelized slightly, enhancing flavor and texture.
Let the dessert rest before serving.
Resting allows juices to thicken, making it easier to serve and preventing a soggy mess.
Use a shallow baking dish for even cooking.
A shallow dish spreads the fruit and topping evenly, promoting uniform baking and crisp topping.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using overripe peaches that are too soft

Why it happens Many assume the softer the peach, the sweeter and better the dessert. But too soft peaches break down during baking, making the filling watery and mushy.

Fix Choose peaches that give slightly when pressed but aren’t squishy. If you only have very ripe peaches, reduce added liquid and bake for a shorter time to avoid excess softness.

Melting butter before mixing the topping

Why it happens Some mix butter too long or warm it up to combine faster, not realizing it affects texture.

Fix Keep butter chilled and use a pastry cutter or fingers to work it into dry ingredients quickly. This creates the crumbly texture essential for a good topping.

Skipping lemon juice in the peach mixture

Why it happens People often overlook lemon juice, thinking it’s optional.

Fix Add lemon juice to brighten flavor and prevent peaches from browning. It’s a small step that makes a big difference in taste and appearance.

Pressing down the topping before baking

Why it happens Some think pressing the topping will help it stick or bake evenly.

Fix Leave the topping loose so it crisps up nicely. Pressing it down compacts the butter and flour, resulting in a dense, less crunchy crust.

Not letting the dessert rest after baking

Why it happens Impatience to serve warm can lead to a runny, messy dessert.

Fix Allow the peach crisp to cool for 5-10 minutes so juices thicken. This makes serving cleaner and improves texture.

Variations

Peach and Blueberry Crisp

Add a cup of fresh or frozen blueberries to the peach mixture for a colorful twist. Blueberries add a tart contrast and deepen the flavor profile without changing the baking time much.

Note: If using frozen berries, thaw and drain excess liquid to avoid sogginess.

Peach Cobbler with Biscuit Topping

Swap the oat topping for a biscuit dough for a heartier, cake-like texture. Drop spoonfuls of biscuit dough over the peach filling and bake until golden and fluffy.

Note: Baking time may increase by 5-10 minutes to cook the biscuit topping through.

Spiced Peach Crisp with Nut Topping

Mix chopped pecans or walnuts into the oat topping along with a pinch of nutmeg for added crunch and warm spice notes. This adds texture and a richer flavor.

Note: Toast nuts lightly before mixing to enhance flavor.

Vegan Peach Crisp

Use coconut oil or vegan butter instead of dairy butter for a plant-based version. The texture will be slightly different but still delicious and crisp.

Note: Make sure the vegan butter is firm and cold for best topping texture.

Peach and Ginger Crisp

Add 1 teaspoon of freshly grated ginger to the peach mixture for a zesty kick. Ginger pairs beautifully with peach and adds a subtle warmth to the dessert.

Note: Fresh ginger is preferred; powdered ginger can be used but start with less to avoid overpowering.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

This peach crisp can safely sit out covered for up to 2 hours. Cover loosely with foil or a clean kitchen towel to keep dust away but allow some air circulation so the topping stays crisp.

Refrigerator

Store leftovers in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The topping may soften when chilled, so reheat before serving to restore crispness. It can be enjoyed cold but the texture changes noticeably.

Freezer

Freeze the baked peach crisp in a freezer-safe container for up to 2 months. Wrap tightly to prevent freezer burn. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat in the oven to refresh the topping’s texture.

Reheating

Reheat in a 350°F oven for 10-15 minutes until warmed through and topping is crisp again. You can microwave for convenience but the topping will lose crispness. Add a splash of juice if the filling looks dry.

Make Ahead

Prepare the peach filling and topping separately up to 2 days ahead. Store filling in the fridge and topping in an airtight container at room temperature. Assemble and bake just before serving for best texture.

FAQ

Can I use frozen peaches for this recipe?
Yes, you can use frozen peaches but be sure to thaw them completely and drain any excess liquid before mixing. Frozen peaches release more water, so draining helps prevent a soggy dessert. You might also want to reduce added liquids slightly.
What can I substitute for rolled oats in the topping?
You can substitute quick oats or even chopped nuts if you prefer. Quick oats will soften more and produce a less crunchy topping, while nuts add extra texture and flavor but change the character of the topping.
How do I know when the peach crisp is done baking?
Look for bubbly juices around the edges and a golden brown topping. The fruit should be tender when pierced with a fork, and the topping crisp to touch. If the topping browns too fast, cover with foil to finish baking.
Can I make this recipe gluten-free?
Absolutely! Use gluten-free all-purpose flour and certified gluten-free oats to make the topping gluten-free. The texture will be very similar, and the peaches remain naturally gluten-free.
Can I add other fruits to this peach recipe?
Yes, fruits like blueberries, raspberries, or sliced nectarines work well. Just be mindful that some fruits release more juice, so adjust sugar and lemon juice accordingly to balance sweetness and acidity.
What’s the best way to peel peaches easily?
Blanch peaches in boiling water for 30 seconds, then transfer to ice water. The skins will loosen and peel off easily with your fingers or a paring knife. This method is quick and keeps the fruit intact.
Can I prepare this dessert ahead of time?
Yes, you can prepare the peach filling and topping separately and refrigerate them for up to 2 days. Assemble and bake just before serving to keep the topping crisp and peaches fresh.
Is it okay to skip the cinnamon?
You can skip cinnamon if you prefer, but it adds warmth and depth to the flavor. If you don’t like cinnamon, try substituting with a pinch of nutmeg or ginger for a different spice note.
