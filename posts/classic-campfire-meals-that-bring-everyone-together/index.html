Classic Campfire Meals That Bring Everyone Together

Molly Dupont
Published on June 12, 2026
15 min Prep Time
30 min Time
4 Servings
Easy Difficulty

Nothing beats gathering around a campfire with hearty, simple meals that warm you up and fill you up. These recipes use easy ingredients and classic techniques, perfect for a cozy night outdoors or backyard fire pit.

Introduction

I’ve always loved campfire meals because they bring back memories of family trips and evenings spent under the stars. The smell of smoky chicken and roasting potatoes just feels like home. This recipe is my go-to because it’s simple but packed with flavor. I’ve tweaked it over time to include smoked paprika for that perfect campfire taste and use thinly sliced potatoes to ensure they cook through evenly. The trick I learned is to wrap everything tightly in foil packets so the juices stay locked in, making the chicken tender and the potatoes soft but not mushy. When it’s done, you get that wonderful smoky aroma and a hearty, comforting meal that’s perfect for sharing with loved ones. It’s easy enough for beginners but impressive enough to bring smiles around the fire. Plus, cleanup is a breeze since everything cooks in one packet. I think you’ll find this recipe becomes a staple for your outdoor dinners just like it did for mine.

What You'll Need

4 servings

  • 4 boneless chicken thighs, skin on
  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 teaspoon smoked paprika
  • 1 teaspoon garlic powder
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 4 medium potatoes, sliced thin
  • 1 medium onion, sliced
  • 1 cup shredded cheddar cheese
  • Fresh parsley for garnish

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by prepping your chicken thighs. Pat them dry with paper towels, then rub each piece with olive oil, smoked paprika, garlic powder, salt, and pepper. This seasoning mix gives the chicken that classic campfire smoky flavor and helps keep the meat juicy. Make sure to coat evenly so every bite is flavorful. Avoid skipping the oil because it helps the spices stick and prevents the chicken from drying out during cooking.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chicken skin looks evenly coated and slightly glossy
Step 2
Slice your potatoes and onions thinly—this helps them cook through faster and absorb the chicken’s juices. Toss the slices in a bowl with a pinch of salt and a drizzle of olive oil to keep them from sticking together. Thin slices also ensure a tender texture without needing a long cook time, which is perfect for campfire cooking where heat can be uneven.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Potatoes and onions are lightly coated and separate easily
Step 3
Lay out large pieces of heavy-duty aluminum foil—enough to fold into a sealed packet. Place a layer of potatoes and onions in the center, then top with one seasoned chicken thigh. Sprinkle shredded cheddar cheese over the top for a melty, savory finish. The foil traps steam and smoke, cooking everything evenly while keeping flavors locked in.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Packets are neatly wrapped with no gaps or tears
Step 4
Seal the foil packets tightly, folding edges carefully to avoid leaks. This is key to steaming the ingredients inside and preventing flare-ups on the fire. If packets aren’t sealed well, juices will drip out and cause uneven cooking or flare-ups. Double wrap if you’re worried about holes.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Packets are fully sealed with no openings
Step 5
Place the foil packets on hot coals or a grill over the campfire. Cook for about 25-30 minutes, turning once halfway through to ensure even heat distribution. Keep an eye on the fire so it stays steady but not too hot, which could burn the food. If the fire is too strong, move packets to cooler spots.
⏱ Time: 30 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chicken juices run clear and potatoes are tender when pierced
Step 6
Carefully remove packets from the fire using tongs or heat-resistant gloves. Let them rest for a few minutes before opening to allow the steam to settle and finish cooking the chicken gently. This resting step helps the meat stay moist and juicy.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Packets are cool enough to handle safely
Step 7
Open the packets carefully to avoid steam burns. The melted cheese should be bubbly and golden, and the potatoes soft but not mushy. Garnish with fresh parsley for a pop of color and fresh flavor. Serve directly from the foil for that authentic campfire feel.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cheese is melted and potatoes are fork-tender
Step 8
Pair your meal with crusty bread or a simple green salad to balance the hearty flavors. I like to bring some pickles or a tangy slaw to add brightness. This contrast really elevates the meal and keeps it from feeling too heavy.
⏱ Time: Optional
✓ Doneness: Sides are fresh and ready to serve
Step 9
Enjoy your meal around the fire with friends or family. The smoky aroma and tender textures make this meal feel special and comforting. Don’t forget to clean up foil scraps and keep your campsite tidy!
⏱ Time: Variable
✓ Doneness: Everyone is happily eating and satisfied

Pro Tips

Use heavy-duty aluminum foil to prevent tearing during cooking.
Campfire heat is uneven and can create hot spots that puncture thin foil. Heavy-duty foil holds up better, keeping juices sealed inside and preventing flare-ups that can burn your food.
Let the foil packets rest for 5 minutes after cooking before opening.
Resting allows the juices to redistribute through the chicken and potatoes, making the texture more tender and flavorful. Opening immediately can cause steam burns and dry out the meat.
Turn your foil packets halfway through cooking.
Campfire heat can be inconsistent, so flipping packets ensures even cooking on all sides and prevents one side from burning while the other remains undercooked.
Slice potatoes thinly and evenly for consistent cooking.
Thin slices cook faster and absorb flavors better. Uneven pieces can lead to some slices being undercooked while others are mushy, ruining the texture of the dish.
Avoid opening the foil packets while cooking.
Opening releases steam and heat, which slows down cooking and can dry out the food. Keep packets sealed until the end for best results.
Season the chicken well before cooking.
Proper seasoning enhances flavor and helps create a tasty crust through the Maillard reaction. Undersalted chicken can taste bland, especially when cooked outdoors.
Use gloves or tongs to handle hot foil packets safely.
Foil conducts heat quickly, and packets can be very hot. Protect your hands to avoid burns and safely remove packets from the fire.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using thin or regular foil that tears easily

Why it happens Many cooks assume any foil will do, but thin foil can rip from campfire heat or handling, causing juices to leak and food to burn.

Fix Always use heavy-duty foil and double wrap if needed. Check for tears before cooking and reseal tightly to keep moisture in.

Cutting potatoes too thick

Why it happens Thicker slices take longer to cook and may remain hard while chicken finishes cooking.

Fix Slice potatoes thinly and evenly, about 1/8 inch thick, so they cook fully and absorb flavors.

Opening packets too early

Why it happens Curiosity or impatience leads to opening packets before food is done, releasing steam and heat.

Fix Trust the cooking time and resist opening until resting time to keep food moist and tender.

Not turning packets during cooking

Why it happens Some assume one side of the fire is enough, but campfire heat is uneven and unpredictable.

Fix Turn packets halfway through cooking to ensure even heat exposure and prevent burning.

Skipping seasoning or using too little

Why it happens Underseasoning is common when cooking outdoors, leading to bland results.

Fix Generously season chicken and vegetables before cooking for full flavor development.

Variations

Spicy Campfire Chicken Packets

Add 1 teaspoon chili powder and a pinch of cayenne to the seasoning mix for a spicy kick. You can also toss in sliced jalapeños with the potatoes for extra heat. Expect a bolder flavor that pairs well with a cooling side like sour cream or ranch.

Note: Spices may intensify during cooking, so adjust amounts to taste and watch for flare-ups from added oils.

Vegetarian Campfire Packets

Replace chicken with hearty vegetables like sliced mushrooms, zucchini, and bell peppers. Use smoked paprika and garlic powder for seasoning, and add a sprinkle of feta or mozzarella cheese. This version offers a lighter, veggie-packed meal with plenty of smoky flavor.

Note: Vegetables cook faster than chicken, so reduce cook time to 20-25 minutes and check for tenderness.

Herb-Infused Campfire Chicken

Mix fresh herbs like rosemary, thyme, and parsley into the seasoning for a fragrant twist. Adding lemon slices inside the packet brightens the flavors and adds moisture. This variation feels fresh and aromatic, perfect for spring or summer camping.

Note: Herbs release oils during cooking, enhancing aroma but watch for burning if fire is too hot.

Cheesy Bacon Campfire Packets

Add cooked, crumbled bacon to the cheese layer for smoky richness. The bacon fat adds flavor and moisture, making this a decadent treat. Great for weekend camping trips when you want to indulge a bit.

Note: Pre-cook bacon to avoid undercooked meat and reduce grease inside the packet.

Sweet Potato and Apple Packets

Swap regular potatoes for thinly sliced sweet potatoes and add apple slices with a sprinkle of cinnamon. This sweet and savory combo offers a comforting fall flavor that pairs nicely with pork or chicken.

Note: Sweet potatoes cook similarly to regular potatoes but may caramelize slightly—watch for burning edges.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Campfire meals should not be left at room temperature for more than 2 hours due to food safety concerns. Keep packets covered if serving outdoors and refrigerate leftovers promptly.

Refrigerator

Store leftover foil packets in an airtight container or resealed foil for up to 3 days. Reheat gently to avoid drying out the chicken. Flavor and texture may be slightly less smoky when served cold.

Freezer

Freeze leftover cooked packets for up to 2 months. Wrap tightly in foil and place in freezer bags to prevent freezer burn. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating.

Reheating

Reheat foil packets in a 350°F oven for 15-20 minutes or until heated through. Alternatively, remove food from foil and microwave with a splash of water to keep moist. Avoid high microwave power to prevent drying.

Make Ahead

You can prep and season the chicken and slice potatoes a day ahead. Assemble packets and store wrapped in the refrigerator. On the day of cooking, bring packets to room temperature before placing on the fire for even cooking.

FAQ

Can I use chicken breasts instead of thighs?
Yes, but chicken breasts cook faster and can dry out more easily over a campfire. To avoid this, slice breasts thinner or reduce cook time, and consider adding a bit more oil or a marinade to keep them moist.
What if I don’t have smoked paprika?
You can substitute regular paprika and add a small amount of liquid smoke if you have it. Otherwise, a mix of paprika and a pinch of chili powder can provide some smoky depth, though it won’t be quite the same.
How do I know when the chicken is fully cooked?
The safest way is to check that the juices run clear when pierced and the meat is no longer pink inside. If you have a meat thermometer, aim for an internal temperature of 165°F.
Can I cook these packets on a grill instead of a campfire?
Absolutely! A grill works great and allows more temperature control. Cook over medium heat for about 25-30 minutes, turning packets halfway through.
How can I prevent the foil from sticking to the food?
Lightly brush the foil with oil before adding ingredients, or use parchment paper inside the foil packets to create a barrier. This helps prevent sticking and makes cleanup easier.
What sides go well with campfire meals?
Simple sides like a green salad, crusty bread, or pickled vegetables complement the smoky, hearty main dish nicely without overpowering it.
Can I add other vegetables to the packets?
Yes, sturdy vegetables like carrots, bell peppers, or mushrooms work well. Just slice them thinly to ensure they cook fully alongside the potatoes and chicken.
Is it safe to cook food directly on campfire coals?
Yes, but be sure to use heavy-duty foil and turn packets regularly to avoid burning. Also, place packets on hot coals rather than open flames for more even heat.

Final Thoughts

I hope you give these campfire meals a try on your next outdoor adventure. They’re simple, satisfying, and sure to become a family favorite. Enjoy the warmth of the fire and the company around it!
