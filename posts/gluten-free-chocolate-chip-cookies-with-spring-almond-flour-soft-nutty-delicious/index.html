Gluten-Free Chocolate Chip Cookies with Spring Almond Flour: Soft, Nutty & Delicious

Molly Dupont
Published on May 29, 2026
15 min Prep Time
12 min Time
24 Servings
Easy Difficulty

These gluten-free chocolate chip cookies are soft and buttery, thanks to almond flour’s natural nuttiness. I love how they stay moist and tender without any gluten. Perfect for afternoon snacks or sharing with friends!

Introduction

I first tried making gluten-free chocolate chip cookies years ago when a close friend asked me to bake something safe for her gluten intolerance. After a few failed attempts with various flours, I stumbled upon almond flour during springtime when fresh almonds were abundant. The nutty flavor and natural moisture it adds completely changed the game. This recipe is my favorite because the cookies come out soft and chewy, not crumbly or dry like some gluten-free versions. The trick I learned is to use a mix of coconut and brown sugar for depth and to beat the butter and sugars well for extra fluffiness. Plus, a little baking soda helps them rise just enough without becoming cakey. When you bite into one, you get a tender crumb with bursts of melty chocolate and a subtle almond aroma that’s perfect for an afternoon treat or sharing at a casual get-together. I love how simple it is to whip up a batch, and it’s always a crowd-pleaser, gluten-free or not.

What You'll Need

Approximately 24 cookies

  • 2 1/2 cups almond flour, finely ground
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • 1/4 cup coconut sugar
  • 1/4 cup brown sugar, packed
  • 1/4 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon pure vanilla extract
  • 1 cup semi-sweet chocolate chips

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line two baking sheets with parchment paper. This ensures even baking and prevents sticking. Proper preheating is key here because almond flour cookies can spread quickly if the oven isn’t hot enough right from the start. Make sure your oven is fully heated before placing the cookies inside to get that perfect golden edge and soft center.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Oven is fully preheated to 350°F
Step 2
In a medium bowl, whisk together the almond flour, baking soda, and salt. Mixing these dry ingredients well helps distribute the leavening agent evenly, which is crucial for consistent texture. Almond flour can clump, so breaking up lumps now avoids uneven spots in your cookies later.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dry ingredients are fully combined and smooth
Step 3
In a large bowl, beat the softened butter, coconut sugar, and brown sugar until creamy and fluffy. This aeration traps air, giving your cookies a lighter texture. Don’t rush this step; it usually takes about 3-5 minutes with a hand mixer. If you skip it, your cookies might turn out dense and less tender.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Butter and sugars are pale and fluffy
Step 4
Add the egg and vanilla extract to the butter-sugar mixture and beat until fully incorporated. The egg acts as a binder and adds moisture, while vanilla enhances the cookie’s aroma. Make sure the mixture is smooth and uniform before moving on to avoid streaks or dry spots.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Mixture is smooth, glossy, and uniform in color
Step 5
Gradually add the dry ingredients into the wet mixture, folding gently with a spatula. Overmixing can break down the almond flour’s texture and make cookies tough, so fold just until combined. The dough should be slightly sticky but hold together well.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough is uniform with no visible flour streaks
Step 6
Fold in the chocolate chips evenly throughout the dough. This ensures every cookie gets a good amount of chocolate. If you add them too early or mix too vigorously, they can melt or break down, affecting the texture and appearance.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chocolate chips are evenly distributed without melting
Step 7
Scoop tablespoon-sized balls of dough onto the prepared baking sheets, spacing them about 2 inches apart. Almond flour cookies spread less than wheat flour ones, but giving them space prevents them from merging. Using a cookie scoop helps keep sizes consistent for even baking.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Dough balls are uniform and spaced properly
Step 8
Bake for 10-12 minutes until the edges are lightly golden and the centers look set but still soft. Almond flour browns faster than wheat flour, so watch closely to avoid burning. The cookies will firm up more as they cool, so don’t overbake.
⏱ Time: 12 minutes
✓ Doneness: Edges are golden and centers appear just set
Step 9
Allow the cookies to cool on the baking sheet for 10 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. This resting time lets them firm up without breaking apart. Moving them too soon can cause them to crumble, as almond flour cookies are more delicate than traditional ones.
⏱ Time: 10 minutes
✓ Doneness: Cookies hold their shape when lifted

Pro Tips

Always measure almond flour by weight for accuracy.
Almond flour can vary in density depending on brand and how it's packed. Measuring by weight ensures consistent texture and prevents dry or overly moist dough, which affects cookie structure.
Use room temperature butter for proper creaming.
Cold butter won’t mix well with sugars, and melted butter changes dough consistency. Creaming room temperature butter traps air, creating a lighter texture and helping cookies rise evenly.
Don’t overmix once dry ingredients are added.
Overmixing develops structure in wheat flour cookies, but here it can break down almond flour’s delicate texture, leading to dense or tough cookies instead of tender ones.
Chill the dough briefly if it feels too sticky.
Chilling firms the dough, making it easier to scoop and preventing excessive spreading during baking, which keeps cookies thick and chewy.
Use parchment paper or silicone mats on baking sheets.
These surfaces prevent sticking and promote even heat distribution, which helps cookies bake uniformly without burning on the bottom.
Watch baking time carefully; almonds brown quickly.
Almond flour’s natural oils cause faster browning. Baking too long results in dry, bitter cookies, so remove them when edges are lightly golden and centers still look soft.
Let cookies cool on the baking sheet before moving.
This resting period allows cookies to set and firm up, preventing breakage. Almond flour cookies are more fragile when hot compared to wheat-based ones.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using coarse almond meal instead of fine almond flour.

Why it happens Some people assume any almond-based flour works the same, but coarse meal doesn’t blend smoothly.

Fix Make sure to buy or grind finely blanched almond flour. This gives a tender texture and prevents gritty or crumbly cookies.

Overbaking the cookies.

Why it happens It’s tempting to bake until fully browned, but almond flour browns quickly and dries out.

Fix Remove cookies when edges are just golden and centers look set but soft. They’ll firm up as they cool.

Skipping the creaming step with butter and sugars.

Why it happens In a rush, some mix all ingredients together at once.

Fix Take time to cream butter and sugars until fluffy. This traps air and improves texture dramatically.

Adding chocolate chips too early.

Why it happens Some mix chips with dry ingredients, causing them to melt or break down.

Fix Fold chips in last to keep their shape and texture intact.

Not spacing cookies properly on the baking sheet.

Why it happens Crowding leads to merged cookies or uneven baking.

Fix Space dough balls 2 inches apart to allow for spreading and even heat circulation.

Variations

Add chopped toasted pecans

Mix in 1/2 cup toasted pecans for an extra crunch and deeper nutty flavor. This adds texture contrast and complements the almond flour beautifully.

Note: Add nuts along with chocolate chips to distribute evenly without affecting dough consistency.

Use dark chocolate chunks instead of chips

Swap semi-sweet chips for chopped dark chocolate for a richer, less sweet cookie. The chunks melt in pockets, creating gooey bites.

Note: Chop chocolate into uniform pieces to ensure even melting and distribution.

Make vegan by replacing egg and butter

Use flax egg (1 tbsp flaxseed meal + 3 tbsp water) and coconut oil instead of butter. The texture will be slightly more crumbly but still delicious.

Note: Chill dough longer before baking to help with structure since flax eggs don’t bind as strongly.

Add a pinch of cinnamon and sea salt

Sprinkle cinnamon into the dough and finish cookies with a tiny sea salt pinch on top before baking. This enhances flavor complexity and balances sweetness.

Note: Adjust cinnamon to taste; too much can overpower the almond flavor.

Swap half almond flour for oat flour

Replace 1 1/4 cups almond flour with oat flour for a lighter texture and mild oat flavor. This variation is great if you want a less dense cookie.

Note: Oat flour absorbs more moisture, so you may need to add a splash of milk or reduce baking time slightly.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain softness and prevent melting chocolate.

Refrigerator

Cookies can be refrigerated for up to 7 days in an airtight container. They may firm up and lose some softness; warm slightly before eating to restore texture.

Freezer

Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe bag for up to 3 months. Thaw at room temperature and warm briefly in the oven to refresh their softness.

Reheating

Reheat cookies in a 300°F oven for 5-7 minutes or microwave for 10-15 seconds with a damp paper towel nearby to keep moisture. Avoid overheating to prevent drying out.

Make Ahead

Prepare dough up to 2 days ahead and keep refrigerated. Scoop and bake fresh when ready to serve for best texture and flavor.

FAQ

Can I substitute almond flour with another gluten-free flour?
Almond flour’s unique moisture and texture are hard to replicate. You can try oat or hazelnut flour, but expect changes in texture and flavor. If using other flours, you may need to adjust liquid amounts and baking time.
Why are my cookies spreading too much?
If your dough is too warm or butter too soft, cookies will spread excessively. Chill dough before baking and ensure butter is just softened, not melted. Also, don’t overcrowd the baking sheet.
How do I keep cookies soft longer?
Store in an airtight container with a slice of bread or apple to maintain moisture. Avoid refrigeration as it can dry them out. Reheat gently before serving if they firm up.
What’s the best way to measure almond flour?
Weighing almond flour with a kitchen scale is best for accuracy. If using cups, lightly spoon the flour into the cup without packing, then level with a knife to avoid dense dough.
Can I add nuts or dried fruit?
Absolutely! Toasted nuts add crunch, and dried fruit adds chewiness. Add about 1/2 cup along with chocolate chips, but be mindful of moisture content from dried fruit.
Why do my cookies sometimes come out dry?
Overbaking is the main culprit. Almond flour cookies dry out quickly if baked too long. Remove when edges turn golden but centers remain soft, then cool on the sheet.
Can I double the recipe?
Yes, doubling works well. Just mix in a larger bowl and bake in batches. Make sure to space cookies properly on baking sheets to avoid merging.
Is it okay to use salted butter?
You can, but reduce added salt in the recipe to avoid oversalting. Unsalted butter gives you more control over seasoning.
