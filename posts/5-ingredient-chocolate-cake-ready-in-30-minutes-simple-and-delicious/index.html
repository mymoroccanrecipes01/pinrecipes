5 Ingredient Chocolate Cake Ready in 30 Minutes: Simple and Delicious

Molly Dupont Published on May 22, 2026
10 min prep 20 min cook 8 servings

This easy chocolate cake uses just five ingredients and comes together in half an hour. The secret? Mixing in sour cream for extra moistness. Great for a quick dessert fix or last-minute celebrations!

Why This Works

This recipe’s success hinges on the balance of ingredients and technique that produce a moist, tender crumb without complexity. The use of baking soda as a leavening agent reacts with the acidic sour cream to create carbon dioxide bubbles, which give the cake its rise and light texture. Without the acid, the baking soda wouldn’t activate properly, resulting in a dense cake.

The sour cream also plays a dual role: it adds moisture and richness while tenderizing the crumb through its fat content and acidity. This ingredient prevents the cake from drying out, which is a common pitfall in simple chocolate cakes.

Mixing technique is crucial here. Sifting the dry ingredients aerates the flour and cocoa powder, preventing lumps and ensuring even leavening. Folding the wet and dry mixtures gently avoids overdeveloping gluten, which would make the cake chewy instead of soft. The warm water helps dissolve cocoa and sugar, intensifying the chocolate flavor and creating a smooth batter.

Finally, baking at a moderate 350°F ensures the cake cooks evenly without drying out. The relatively short baking time, combined with the moist batter, yields a cake that’s tender, rich, and satisfying every time.

  • Choose a good-quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for best results. It provides the right balance of protein to develop just enough gluten for structure without making the cake tough. Avoid using cake flour here, as it might create a crumb that’s too delicate for this simple recipe.
  • Use natural unsweetened cocoa powder for a bright chocolate flavor that reacts well with baking soda. Dutch-processed cocoa is less acidic and won’t activate the baking soda as effectively, potentially affecting rise and texture.
  • Baking soda is a powerful leavening agent that requires an acid to activate. Here, sour cream provides that acid, producing carbon dioxide bubbles that help the cake rise and become fluffy. Using baking powder instead would require recipe adjustments.
  • Sour cream adds moisture, tenderness, and a subtle tang that enhances chocolate flavor. It also activates the baking soda. Make sure it’s at room temperature to blend smoothly into the batter, preventing lumps.
  • Vegetable oil keeps the cake moist and tender because it remains liquid at room temperature, unlike butter which solidifies. This helps the cake stay soft even after cooling. Neutral oils like canola or sunflower work well here.

Ingredients

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsweetened cocoa powder
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/2 cup sour cream, at room temperature
  • 1/2 cup water, warm
  • 1/3 cup vegetable oil
  • 1 large egg
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and grease an 8-inch round cake pan. This step ensures your cake doesn’t stick and bakes evenly. Greasing the pan well and lightly dusting it with flour or cocoa powder will help the cake release cleanly after baking. If you skip this, you might end up with a crumbly mess when removing the cake, so don’t rush this part.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Pan is evenly greased and dusted, no shiny spots
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, sift together the flour, sugar, cocoa powder, and baking soda. Sifting helps remove lumps and aerates the dry ingredients, which leads to a lighter cake texture. Mixing dry ingredients thoroughly at this stage also ensures even distribution of the leavening agent, so your cake rises uniformly. Avoid skipping sifting; otherwise, you might get uneven pockets of cocoa or baking soda.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are well combined with no visible lumps
  3. Step 3

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sour cream, warm water, vegetable oil, egg, and vanilla extract until smooth. The warmth of the water helps dissolve the cocoa and sugar better, enhancing the chocolate flavor. Mixing these wet ingredients well ensures a smooth batter and helps incorporate air, contributing to the cake’s tenderness. Be careful not to overbeat, which can make the cake dense.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Wet mixture is smooth, homogeneous, and slightly thick
  4. Step 4

    Pour the wet ingredients into the dry ingredients and gently fold together using a spatula. Folding rather than stirring vigorously prevents overdeveloping gluten in the flour, which can make the cake tough. Mix just until no streaks of flour remain. Overmixing is a common mistake here and will affect the cake’s texture negatively.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter is smooth with no dry spots but not overly mixed
  5. Step 5

    Pour the batter into the prepared pan, smoothing the top with a spatula. This helps the cake bake evenly and look nice once done. Tap the pan gently on the counter to release any large air bubbles trapped in the batter, which can cause holes or uneven crumb.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Batter is evenly spread and surface is smooth
  6. Step 6

    Bake in the preheated oven for 18 to 22 minutes. Start checking at 18 minutes by inserting a toothpick in the center; it should come out with a few moist crumbs but not wet batter. Baking too long dries out the cake; too short leaves it undercooked in the middle.

    ⏱ 18-22 minutes✓ Toothpick inserted comes out with moist crumbs, cake springs back lightly
  7. Step 7

    Remove the cake from the oven and let it cool in the pan for 10 minutes. Cooling slightly helps the cake firm up, making it easier to remove from the pan without breaking. If you try to remove it while hot, it can crumble or stick.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Cake feels set but still warm to the touch
  8. Step 8

    Run a knife around the edge of the cake to loosen it, then invert onto a wire rack to cool completely. Cooling on a rack prevents sogginess from steam and keeps the bottom crust firm. Letting it cool fully before frosting or slicing ensures clean cuts and better texture.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Cake is completely cool, no condensation underneath
  9. Step 9

    Once cooled, frost as desired or dust with powdered sugar. This cake pairs beautifully with whipped cream, chocolate ganache, or fresh berries. If you want to keep it simple, a light dusting of sugar highlights the chocolate flavor without overpowering it.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Frosting smoothly spread or sugar evenly dusted

Pro Tips

  • Always use room temperature ingredients for the wet mix. — Room temperature ingredients blend more easily, creating a smooth batter without lumps. Cold sour cream or eggs can cause uneven mixing and affect the cake’s texture.
  • Don’t overmix the batter when combining wet and dry ingredients. — Overmixing develops gluten in the flour, which makes the cake dense and chewy instead of tender and soft. Fold gently until just combined for the best crumb.
  • Sift your dry ingredients before mixing. — Sifting aerates the flour and cocoa powder, removes lumps, and ensures even distribution of baking soda. This helps the cake rise properly and have a uniform texture.
  • Use warm water to dissolve the cocoa and sugar. — Warm water enhances the chocolate flavor by dissolving cocoa solids and sugar better, resulting in a richer, more intense taste.
  • Grease and dust your pan with cocoa powder instead of flour. — Using cocoa powder prevents a white flour residue on your cake crust and enhances the chocolate flavor while helping the cake release cleanly.
  • Check for doneness early to avoid overbaking. — Overbaking dries out the cake. Start checking with a toothpick at 18 minutes to catch it at perfect moistness.
  • Cool the cake completely before frosting. — Frosting a warm cake causes it to melt and slide off. Cooling ensures the frosting sets nicely and the cake slices cleanly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overmixing the batter
    Why: It’s tempting to mix thoroughly to avoid lumps, but this develops gluten and toughens the cake.
    Fix: Use a gentle folding motion just until the ingredients are combined. Stop mixing as soon as you don’t see dry flour.
  • Skipping the sour cream
    Why: Some might think it’s optional, but it’s key for moisture and activating baking soda.
    Fix: Don’t omit sour cream; if unavailable, substitute with plain yogurt but expect slightly different texture.
  • Using cold wet ingredients
    Why: Cold eggs or sour cream don’t blend well, causing lumps or uneven texture.
    Fix: Bring eggs and sour cream to room temperature before mixing for smooth batter.
  • Not greasing the pan properly
    Why: Rushing this step leads to sticking and cake breakage.
    Fix: Grease thoroughly and dust with cocoa powder to prevent sticking and enhance flavor.
  • Overbaking the cake
    Why: Leaving the cake in too long dries it out.
    Fix: Check doneness early with a toothpick and remove as soon as it comes out with moist crumbs.

Variations

  • Add Espresso Powder for a Mocha Twist: Mix 1 teaspoon of instant espresso powder into the dry ingredients to deepen the chocolate flavor with a subtle coffee note. It’s a great way to make the cake taste more complex without adding bitterness.
    Espresso powder dissolves easily and won’t affect batter texture; just ensure even mixing.
  • Use Greek Yogurt Instead of Sour Cream: Swap sour cream with the same amount of full-fat Greek yogurt for a slightly tangier cake with a bit more protein. The texture remains moist but with a fresher flavor.
    Greek yogurt is thicker, so whisk it well before mixing to avoid lumps.
  • Make It Gluten-Free: Replace all-purpose flour with a gluten-free flour blend that includes xanthan gum. The cake will still rise and be tender but may have a slightly different crumb texture.
    Check that your blend is one-to-one substitution and avoid overmixing to keep it light.
  • Add Chocolate Chips for Extra Gooeyness: Fold in 1/2 cup of semi-sweet chocolate chips into the batter before baking. This adds pockets of melted chocolate for a gooey surprise in every bite.
    Toss chips in a little flour before adding to batter to prevent sinking.
  • Top with Fresh Berries and Whipped Cream: Serve slices topped with fresh raspberries or strawberries and a dollop of whipped cream for a fresh, elegant dessert that balances richness with brightness.
    Add toppings just before serving to keep berries fresh.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the cake covered loosely with a cake dome or plastic wrap at room temperature for up to 2 days. This keeps the crumb soft but avoid direct sunlight or heat which can dry it out.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Keep the cake in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 5 days. The cake may firm up and lose some moisture, so bring it to room temperature before serving or warm slightly in the microwave.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cooled cake tightly in plastic wrap and then foil. Freeze for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature. Reheat gently to restore softness.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm slices in the microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes. Adding a small splash of water or covering loosely helps retain moisture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the batter a few hours ahead and refrigerate it tightly covered. Bring to room temperature and stir gently before baking. Alternatively, bake the cake a day ahead and store cooled, then frost or dust sugar just before serving.

FAQ

Can I use baking powder instead of baking soda?
Baking soda requires an acid like sour cream to activate, so if you substitute baking powder, the cake may not rise properly or could taste different. You’d need to adjust quantities and possibly remove the sour cream. For best results, stick with baking soda here.
What if I don’t have sour cream?
You can substitute sour cream with full-fat Greek yogurt or buttermilk in the same amount. These also provide acidity to activate the baking soda and add moisture. Avoid using low-fat versions as they may dry out the cake.
How do I know when the cake is done?
Insert a toothpick in the center after 18 minutes. It’s done if the toothpick comes out with a few moist crumbs but no wet batter. The cake should also spring back lightly when pressed gently.
Can I make this cake vegan?
This recipe uses egg and dairy, so to make it vegan, you’d need to substitute the egg with a flax or chia egg and use dairy-free sour cream or yogurt alternatives. Keep in mind texture and rise may vary.
Why is my cake dense or tough?
This usually happens from overmixing the batter or using too much flour. Mix just until combined and measure flour carefully by spooning it into the cup instead of scooping.
Can I add frosting or glaze?
Absolutely! This cake pairs well with chocolate ganache, cream cheese frosting, or a simple powdered sugar glaze. Just make sure the cake is fully cooled before applying.
How should I store leftover cake?
Store leftovers in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 2 days or in the fridge for up to 5 days. For longer storage, freeze well wrapped for up to 3 months.
Can I double the recipe?
Yes, you can double the ingredients and bake in a larger pan or two pans. Adjust baking time accordingly—start checking doneness a few minutes earlier than expected.
