Quick Homemade Rhubarb and Strawberry Crisp Dessert That’s Perfect for Spring

Molly Dupont Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 6 servings

This rhubarb and strawberry crisp is a sweet-tart delight that comes together fast. The crumbly oat topping adds a cozy crunch, making it ideal for a last-minute dessert or weekend treat.

Introduction

I first made this rhubarb and strawberry crisp a few springs ago when my local farmer's market was bursting with fresh fruit. I wanted a dessert that wasn’t too sweet but still felt comforting, and this crisp hit the spot perfectly. What I love about this version is the balance between the tartness of rhubarb and the natural sweetness of strawberries, all wrapped in a buttery, crunchy oat topping. It’s quick to throw together, which is great because I often discover rhubarb and strawberries on a whim and want to whip something up fast. One trick I learned is to toss the fruit with cornstarch and sugar before baking; it thickens the juices beautifully without making the filling mushy. The topping is a simple mix of oats, flour, cinnamon, and butter that bakes into a golden, crisp layer that contrasts so well with the soft fruit underneath. The finished dessert is bubbly, fragrant, and perfect served warm with a scoop of vanilla ice cream or just on its own. Honestly, it’s become one of my go-to treats for spring and early summer when rhubarb and strawberries are at their peak. If you’ve never tried rhubarb before, this recipe is a gentle introduction that’s hard to resist.

Why This Works

This crisp recipe succeeds because of the careful balance between tart and sweet fruit, a thickening agent that controls moisture, and a buttery oat topping that provides texture contrast. Rhubarb is naturally quite tart and fibrous, so pairing it with sweet strawberries softens the overall flavor, creating a harmonious sweet-tart profile that’s very appealing. The cornstarch acts as a thickener, absorbing the fruit juices released during baking. Without it, the crisp would become watery and soggy, ruining the texture.

The oat topping is a classic choice for crisps because oats add chewiness and a rustic feel, while the cold butter incorporated into the dry ingredients creates pockets of flakiness as it melts in the oven. This results in a topping that’s crunchy on the outside but tender inside, complementing the soft fruit beneath. Cinnamon adds a subtle warmth that enhances the fruit’s natural flavors without overpowering them.

Temperature control is also key. Baking at 350°F allows the fruit to cook through and the sugars to caramelize slowly, promoting Maillard reactions in the topping for a golden-brown color and rich flavor. Letting the crisp cool briefly before serving helps the filling set so it’s not runny, making it easier to plate and enjoy.

Overall, this recipe balances flavor, texture, and chemistry to deliver a satisfying dessert that’s both quick and impressive.

  • Choose firm, bright red stalks without blemishes or discoloration. Rhubarb’s tartness is stronger in greener stalks, so redder ones tend to be milder and sweeter. Trim off leaves completely as they’re toxic. Fresh rhubarb adds a tangy punch and fibrous texture that holds up well in baking.
  • Select ripe but firm strawberries to avoid mushy filling. Fresh berries provide natural sweetness and juiciness that balances rhubarb’s tartness. If out of season, frozen strawberries can be used but drain excess liquid to prevent sogginess.
  • A neutral thickening agent that swells when heated, cornstarch traps fruit juices released during baking, preventing a watery filling. It’s important to use the right amount—too much can create a gummy texture, too little leads to a runny crisp.
  • These oats hold their shape during baking, providing a hearty and chewy texture. Instant oats are too fine and can create a dense topping, while steel-cut oats don’t soften enough. Rolled oats also help absorb butter and sugar evenly for a balanced crisp.
  • Cold, unsalted butter is essential for creating a flaky and crumbly topping. Butter’s fat coats the flour and oats, inhibiting gluten formation and producing a tender texture. Using salted butter can throw off the flavor balance, so it’s best to add salt separately.

Ingredients

  • 4 cups rhubarb, chopped into 1-inch pieces
  • 3 cups fresh strawberries, hulled and halved
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar, packed
  • 2 tablespoons cornstarch
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 1 cup old-fashioned rolled oats
  • 1/2 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 cup cold unsalted butter, cubed
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • Pinch of salt

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). While it heats, prepare your fruit filling by combining the chopped rhubarb and halved strawberries in a large bowl. This step is important because it ensures your fruit is evenly mixed and ready for the thickening process. Mixing the fruit first also helps you spot any pieces that might be too large or uneven, so you can adjust for a consistent bake.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Fruit is evenly coated and mixed with no dry patches
  2. Step 2

    Add the granulated sugar, brown sugar, and cornstarch to the fruit mixture. Stir gently but thoroughly to coat every piece. The cornstarch is key here—it will absorb excess juices released during baking and prevent your crisp from becoming soggy. Don’t skip this or add too much sugar, or the texture and sweetness will be off balance.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Fruit looks glossy and slightly thickened with sugar and cornstarch
  3. Step 3

    Stir in the vanilla extract and mix once more. This adds a subtle warmth and depth to the fruit filling, enhancing the natural flavors without overpowering them. It’s a small addition but makes a big difference in the final taste.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Vanilla is evenly distributed throughout the fruit
  4. Step 4

    Transfer the fruit mixture to a greased 9x13-inch baking dish, spreading it out evenly. The even layer ensures uniform baking and that every bite has a good balance of fruit and topping. Avoid overfilling the dish to prevent bubbling over during baking.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Fruit layer is smooth and level in the dish
  5. Step 5

    In a separate bowl, combine the rolled oats, flour, brown sugar, cinnamon, and a pinch of salt. Mixing dry ingredients first helps distribute flavors evenly in the topping. The oats provide a hearty texture while the cinnamon adds a warm spice note that complements the fruit.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are well combined with no clumps
  6. Step 6

    Add the cold cubed butter to the oat mixture. Using a pastry cutter or your fingers, work the butter into the dry ingredients until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs. The cold butter creates pockets of flakiness and crunch when baked, which is essential for a crisp topping. Avoid letting the butter melt during this step to keep the texture right.

    ⏱ 7 minutes✓ Mixture looks crumbly with pea-sized butter pieces visible
  7. Step 7

    Sprinkle the oat topping evenly over the fruit in the baking dish. Covering the fruit completely ensures a nice crunchy contrast to the soft filling. Don’t press down on the topping; keeping it loose helps it bake into a crisp rather than a dense crust.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Topping is evenly distributed and loosely layered over fruit
  8. Step 8

    Bake the crisp in the preheated oven for 30 minutes. During baking, the fruit will bubble and the topping will turn golden brown. Keep an eye on it toward the end to avoid burning the topping. The bubbling fruit juices indicate the filling is thickening properly.

    ⏱ 30 minutes✓ Topping is golden and crispy; fruit filling is bubbling around edges
  9. Step 9

    Remove the crisp from the oven and let it cool for at least 10 minutes before serving. Cooling allows the filling to set, making it easier to serve and enhancing the flavors. Serve warm with vanilla ice cream or whipped cream for the best experience.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Filling is thickened and crisp is slightly cooled but still warm

Pro Tips

  • Use cold butter for the topping and handle it minimally. — Cold butter creates small pockets of fat in the topping that melt during baking, producing a flaky, crumbly texture. If the butter warms up or is overworked, it blends too much with the flour and oats, resulting in a dense, greasy topping.
  • Toss fruit with cornstarch and sugar before baking. — Cornstarch thickens the juices released by the fruit as it cooks, preventing a watery dessert. Sugar helps macerate the fruit slightly, releasing natural juices that blend with the thickener to create a luscious filling.
  • Don’t press the topping down on the fruit. — Keeping the topping loose allows it to bake into a crunchy, golden crust. Pressing it down compacts the mixture, which can lead to a dense, less crispy texture and uneven baking.
  • Let the crisp cool before serving. — Cooling allows the filling to set and thicken, making it easier to serve and enhancing the flavors. Serving immediately can result in a runny filling that’s hard to plate.
  • Use fresh, ripe strawberries for best flavor. — Ripe strawberries add natural sweetness and juiciness that balance rhubarb’s tartness. Using underripe or frozen berries without draining can lead to bland or overly watery filling.
  • Bake at a moderate temperature (350°F) for even cooking. — This temperature allows the fruit to soften and juices to thicken without burning the topping. Higher heat risks burning the oats before the filling is cooked through.
  • Add a pinch of cinnamon to the topping. — Cinnamon adds warmth and depth without overpowering the fruit, enhancing the overall flavor profile and making the crisp taste more complex.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using too much sugar in the fruit filling
    Why: Many assume more sugar equals better sweetness, but it can overpower the natural tartness of rhubarb and make the dessert cloying.
    Fix: Stick to the recommended sugar amounts and taste your fruit before adding. You can always sprinkle extra sugar on top before baking if needed, but start conservatively to preserve balance.
  • Skipping the thickener like cornstarch
    Why: Some cooks forget or don’t realize the importance of thickening fruit juices, thinking the crisp will set on its own.
    Fix: Always add cornstarch or a similar thickener to trap juices and avoid a soggy filling. Mix it thoroughly with the fruit and sugar before baking.
  • Using warm butter for the topping
    Why: Butter can soften quickly at room temperature, especially if handled too much, leading to greasy, dense topping.
    Fix: Keep butter cold and work quickly. Chill it again if it softens too much before mixing into dry ingredients.
  • Overbaking or underbaking
    Why: Impatience or distractions can cause uneven baking—either burnt topping or undercooked filling.
    Fix: Bake at 350°F for about 30 minutes and watch for bubbling juices and golden topping. Adjust time slightly based on your oven’s quirks.
  • Not letting the crisp cool before serving
    Why: Eagerness to eat can lead to serving right from the oven when the filling is still liquid.
    Fix: Allow at least 10 minutes cooling so the filling thickens and the crisp slices cleanly.

Variations

  • Add a Nutty Crunch: Mix chopped pecans or walnuts into the oat topping for added texture and a toasty flavor. This variation adds richness and a pleasant crunch that pairs well with the fruit’s softness.
    Nuts may brown faster, so check the crisp a few minutes earlier to prevent burning.
  • Gluten-Free Version: Substitute the all-purpose flour with gluten-free flour blend and use certified gluten-free oats. This makes the crisp suitable for gluten-sensitive diets without compromising texture.
    Gluten-free flour can absorb moisture differently; you may need to adjust butter slightly for proper topping consistency.
  • Add Ginger for Warmth: Stir in a teaspoon of freshly grated ginger into the fruit filling for a spicy kick that complements the tart rhubarb and sweet strawberries beautifully.
    Fresh ginger adds moisture; reduce other liquids slightly if needed to keep filling from becoming too runny.
  • Vegan Crisp: Replace butter with coconut oil or vegan butter substitute. This swap keeps the topping crumbly and flavorful while making it suitable for vegan diets.
    Coconut oil melts faster, so chill the topping mixture before baking to maintain texture.
  • Add Citrus Zest: Incorporate lemon or orange zest into the fruit mixture for a bright, fresh aroma that lifts the whole dessert and adds complexity to the flavor profile.
    Zest should be added just before baking to preserve its vibrant flavor.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: The crisp can be left out covered at room temperature safely for up to 2 hours. Beyond that, it’s best to refrigerate to maintain freshness and prevent spoilage.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftovers in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The crisp topping may soften in the fridge but reheating in the oven will restore some crunch. The filling thickens and flavors meld nicely when chilled.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze the crisp in a tightly wrapped container for up to 3 months. To prevent freezer burn, wrap with plastic wrap and then foil. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat in a 350°F oven for 15-20 minutes until warmed through and topping is crisp again. Avoid microwaving as it can make the topping soggy. Adding a small pat of butter on top before reheating can refresh the topping’s texture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the fruit filling and topping separately a day ahead. Store chilled and assemble just before baking. This saves time on the day you want to serve it and keeps the topping fresh and crumbly.

FAQ

Can I use frozen rhubarb or strawberries for this crisp?
Yes, frozen fruit works well, but it’s important to thaw and drain excess liquid thoroughly before mixing with sugar and cornstarch. This prevents the filling from becoming too watery and ensures the crisp bakes properly.
What can I substitute for cornstarch if I don’t have any?
You can use arrowroot powder or tapioca starch as a thickener in equal amounts. Flour can also work but may create a slightly cloudier filling and a different texture. Avoid skipping the thickener altogether.
How do I know when the crisp is done baking?
Look for a golden-brown topping and bubbling fruit juices around the edges of the dish. The bubbling indicates the filling is hot and thickened. The topping should be crisp to the touch but not burnt.
Can I make this crisp vegan?
Absolutely! Substitute the butter with coconut oil or a vegan butter alternative. The texture will remain crumbly and delicious, though coconut oil may add a subtle flavor difference.
Why is my crisp topping sometimes soggy?
This usually happens if the butter melts before baking or if the topping is pressed down too firmly on the fruit. Use cold butter and keep the topping loose to bake into a crisp texture.
Can I add other fruits to this crisp?
Yes, you can mix in fruits like blueberries, raspberries, or blackberries. Just adjust sugar and thickener amounts slightly if you add very juicy fruits to maintain the right consistency.
How long can I store leftovers?
Store leftovers in the fridge for up to 4 days in an airtight container. The crisp topping may soften but reheating in the oven will help restore some crunch.
Is it better to use old-fashioned oats or quick oats?
Old-fashioned rolled oats are best for topping because they hold their texture during baking and provide a pleasing chewiness. Quick oats tend to bake into a denser, less crunchy topping.
