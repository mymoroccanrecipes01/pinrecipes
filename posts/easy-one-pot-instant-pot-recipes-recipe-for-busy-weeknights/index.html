Easy One Pot Instant Pot Recipes Recipe for Busy Weeknights

Molly Dupont Published on May 30, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 4 servings

This one pot Instant Pot recipe is a lifesaver when you want a quick, hearty meal with minimal cleanup. Using the pressure cooker locks in flavor and tenderness. Perfect for busy weeknights or lazy weekends!

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this easy one pot Instant Pot recipe when I was scrambling for a quick dinner after a long day. What hooked me immediately was how little fuss it required—just a handful of ingredients and one pot to clean! Over time, I tweaked it to balance flavors better, adding smoked paprika for a subtle smoky depth and using chicken thighs instead of breasts for juicier meat. The trick I learned is to sauté the onions and garlic right in the pot before adding everything else; it builds a richer base flavor that really makes the dish shine. When it’s done, the rice is perfectly tender, the chicken melts in your mouth, and the peas add a nice pop of color and freshness. It’s the kind of meal that feels like a hug on a plate but comes together in under an hour. I love making this on busy weeknights or lazy weekends when I want comfort food without the hassle. Plus, since it’s all cooked in one pot, cleanup is a breeze, which is a huge bonus in my book!

Why This Works

One pot recipes simplify cooking by combining all ingredients into a single vessel, which in this case is the Instant Pot. The pressure cooking environment cooks food quickly while sealing in moisture and intensifying flavors. This recipe uses chicken thighs because their higher fat content keeps the meat tender and juicy under pressure, unlike leaner cuts that can dry out. The initial sautéing of onions and garlic builds a flavor foundation through caramelization and Maillard reactions, which wouldn’t develop in pressure cooking alone.

The rice cooks perfectly because it absorbs the flavorful broth and tomato juices during the pressure phase, making it tender but not mushy. The timing is crucial—overcooking rice under pressure can lead to a sticky mess, so 10 minutes with natural release is ideal. Adding frozen peas after cooking preserves their texture and color, preventing them from becoming mushy.

Spices like smoked paprika and oregano add depth without overpowering the dish, balancing the savory chicken and acidity from tomatoes. The natural release step is important as it lets the rice finish cooking gently and prevents sudden pressure drops that could affect texture. Finally, the one pot method reduces cleanup and makes weeknight cooking less stressful, which is why this recipe is a favorite for busy cooks.

  • Chicken thighs are preferred here for their rich flavor and moist texture. When buying, look for fresh, skinless, boneless thighs without excess liquid. Their higher fat content compared to breasts helps keep the meat tender during pressure cooking, preventing dryness. If substituting with breasts, reduce cooking time slightly to avoid overcooking.
  • Long grain rice is ideal because it cooks up fluffy and separate, absorbing the broth well without turning too sticky. Rinse the rice before use to remove excess starch, which helps prevent clumping. Avoid instant or quick-cooking rice varieties as they can overcook under pressure.
  • Smoked paprika adds a subtle smoky depth that enhances the chicken and tomato flavors without heat. Choose a good quality Spanish smoked paprika for authentic taste. It’s a simple way to elevate this easy dish with a complex flavor note.
  • Using low-sodium chicken broth allows you to control the saltiness of the dish. It provides the essential liquid for cooking rice while imparting savory notes. Homemade broth or high-quality store-bought options work best. Avoid broth with added flavors that might clash.
  • Frozen peas are convenient and add a fresh pop of color and sweetness. They should be stirred in after cooking to keep their texture intact. Fresh peas can be used in season but require a slightly longer cooking time if added earlier.

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon olive oil
  • 1 medium onion, diced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 pound boneless chicken thighs, cut into chunks
  • 1 cup long grain rice, rinsed
  • 1 1/2 cups chicken broth
  • 1 cup diced tomatoes (canned or fresh)
  • 1 teaspoon smoked paprika
  • 1/2 teaspoon dried oregano
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1 cup frozen peas
  • Fresh parsley, chopped (for garnish)

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Set your Instant Pot to 'Sauté' mode and heat the olive oil. Add the diced onion and cook, stirring occasionally, until translucent and soft—about 4-5 minutes. This step is key because it develops the base flavor of the dish. Avoid browning the onions too much as it can make the flavor bitter. Once softened, add the minced garlic and sauté for another 30 seconds until fragrant. This quick sauté releases the garlic’s aroma without burning it, which would cause bitterness.

    ⏱ 7 minutes✓ Onions should be translucent and tender, garlic fragrant but not browned
  2. Step 2

    Add the chicken thigh chunks to the pot and season with salt, pepper, smoked paprika, and oregano. Stir well to coat the chicken with the spices and cook until the edges start to brown slightly, about 5 minutes. This step locks in flavor and seals the chicken, which helps keep it juicy during pressure cooking. Don’t rush this step—proper browning adds depth to the final dish.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Chicken edges lightly browned, spices evenly coating pieces
  3. Step 3

    Pour in the rinsed rice, chicken broth, and diced tomatoes. Stir everything together, making sure the rice is submerged in liquid. This is important because the rice needs enough moisture to cook properly under pressure. Scrape the bottom of the pot gently to loosen any browned bits, which prevents the burn warning on the Instant Pot and adds extra flavor.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Rice evenly mixed with broth and tomatoes, no dry patches
  4. Step 4

    Secure the lid on the Instant Pot and set the valve to sealing. Select 'Pressure Cook' or 'Manual' mode and set the timer for 10 minutes on high pressure. Pressure cooking quickly cooks the rice and chicken together, locking in moisture and flavor. Avoid opening the lid during this time to maintain pressure and ensure even cooking.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Instant Pot reaches pressure and timer counts down without interruption
  5. Step 5

    Once the cooking time is up, allow the pressure to release naturally for 10 minutes before carefully turning the valve to venting to release any remaining steam. This natural release lets the rice finish cooking gently and keeps the chicken tender. Quick release right away can make the rice undercooked or mushy.

    ⏱ 15 minutes✓ Pressure fully released, lid can be safely opened
  6. Step 6

    Open the lid and gently fluff the rice and chicken mixture with a fork. Stir in the frozen peas, then close the lid again (no pressure needed) and let it sit for 5 minutes. The residual heat will thaw and warm the peas without overcooking them, preserving their bright color and slight crunch.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Peas are bright green and warmed through
  7. Step 7

    Taste and adjust seasoning with additional salt and pepper if needed. Adding seasoning at the end lets you fine-tune the flavor without over-salting. Remember that broth and canned tomatoes already contribute saltiness.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Balanced flavor with no overpowering saltiness
  8. Step 8

    Spoon the dish into bowls and garnish with freshly chopped parsley for a pop of color and fresh herbal note. This simple garnish brightens the rich flavors and makes the dish look inviting.

    ⏱ 1 minute✓ Parsley evenly scattered, vibrant green
  9. Step 9

    Serve immediately while warm, pairing with a simple green salad or crusty bread if desired. This meal is hearty enough on its own but pairs well with fresh sides for a balanced dinner.

    ⏱ 1 minute✓ Dish served hot and fresh

Pro Tips

  • Always sauté onions and garlic first in the Instant Pot. — Sautéing releases sugars and aromas that build a flavor base. Skipping this step results in a flatter taste because pressure cooking alone doesn’t brown ingredients.
  • Rinse rice thoroughly before cooking. — Rinsing removes surface starch that can cause rice to clump or become gummy under pressure, ensuring a fluffier texture.
  • Use natural pressure release instead of quick release. — Natural release allows rice to finish cooking gently and prevents sudden pressure changes that can lead to uneven texture or spillage.
  • Don’t overcrowd the pot with too much liquid. — Too much liquid can make the rice mushy and dilute flavors. Follow the liquid-to-rice ratio carefully for best results.
  • Add delicate ingredients like peas after cooking. — Adding them post-cooking preserves their texture and color, avoiding overcooking and mushiness.
  • Use chicken thighs for juicier meat. — Thighs retain moisture better under pressure, preventing dry, tough chicken common with breasts.
  • Scrape the pot bottom before pressure cooking. — This prevents the dreaded 'burn' error on the Instant Pot and incorporates flavorful browned bits into the dish.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the sauté step
    Why: People often want to speed up the process and jump straight to pressure cooking.
    Fix: Take the extra few minutes to sauté onions and garlic first. This step is crucial for flavor development and prevents a bland dish.
  • Not rinsing the rice
    Why: Some cooks don’t realize rinsing affects texture.
    Fix: Rinse rice until water runs clear to remove starch, which helps keep rice fluffy and prevents clumping.
  • Using too much liquid
    Why: Assuming more liquid is safer for pressure cooking rice.
    Fix: Stick to recommended liquid amounts. Excess water makes rice mushy and dilutes flavors.
  • Quick releasing pressure immediately
    Why: Impatience or misunderstanding pressure release methods.
    Fix: Use natural release for at least 10 minutes to let rice finish cooking gently and avoid texture issues.
  • Adding frozen peas before pressure cooking
    Why: Assuming all ingredients can be cooked together.
    Fix: Add peas after cooking to keep them vibrant and slightly crisp.

Variations

  • Vegetarian Version: Swap chicken thighs for hearty mushrooms or tofu and use vegetable broth. Add extra veggies like bell peppers or zucchini for a colorful, meatless one pot meal. The texture will be lighter but still satisfying.
    Cooking time may be reduced slightly since vegetables cook faster than chicken.
  • Spicy Kick: Add chopped jalapeños or a teaspoon of chili powder with the spices. Top with fresh cilantro and a squeeze of lime for a zesty, spicy twist that livens up the cozy base recipe.
    Adjust spice levels carefully to avoid overpowering the dish.
  • Creamy Version: Stir in half a cup of cream or coconut milk after cooking for a rich, creamy texture. This adds indulgence and smoothness, perfect for cooler nights or special occasions.
    Add cream off heat to prevent curdling and keep the sauce silky.
  • Brown Rice Adaptation: Use brown rice instead of long grain white rice for a nuttier flavor and more fiber. Increase cooking time to 22 minutes and use slightly more broth for proper hydration.
    Brown rice requires longer pressure cooking and natural release to soften fully.
  • Herb-Infused: Add fresh thyme or rosemary sprigs during cooking for an aromatic boost. Remove herbs before serving to keep flavors balanced and fresh.
    Herbs infuse flavor during pressure cooking but can become bitter if left too long.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: This dish can safely sit at room temperature, covered, for up to 2 hours. Beyond that, bacteria growth increases, so refrigerate leftovers promptly.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store leftovers in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The rice may firm up when cold; reheat gently with a splash of broth to restore moisture. Flavor remains rich but texture softens.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze in airtight containers for up to 3 months. To prevent freezer burn, wrap containers tightly or use vacuum-sealed bags. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat on the stovetop over low heat with a bit of broth to prevent drying out, stirring occasionally. Microwave reheating works too—cover loosely and add moisture if needed. Avoid overheating to keep chicken tender.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the sautéed base and cut chicken a day ahead, storing them separately in the fridge. Assemble and cook in the Instant Pot on the day you plan to serve for maximum freshness and flavor.

FAQ

Can I use chicken breasts instead of thighs?
Yes, but chicken breasts cook faster and can dry out under pressure. Reduce pressure cooking time by 2-3 minutes and keep a close eye on texture. Adding a bit more broth can help maintain moisture.
What if I don’t have smoked paprika?
You can substitute with regular paprika or a mix of paprika and a tiny pinch of cumin for some smokiness. The flavor will be milder but still tasty.
Can I double the recipe?
Yes, but make sure your Instant Pot is large enough to handle the increased volume. Cooking time remains similar, but natural pressure release might take longer due to extra liquid.
Is it okay to use fresh peas instead of frozen?
Absolutely! Fresh peas can be added at the same stage as frozen but might require a minute or two longer to warm through.
How do I avoid the Instant Pot burn warning?
Make sure to deglaze the pot well after sautéing by scraping up any browned bits before adding liquid. Also, don’t add thick sauces or too little liquid, which can cause burning.
Can I make this recipe vegan?
Yes, swap chicken for tofu or mushrooms and use vegetable broth. The cooking times stay the same, but flavors will be different—consider adding extra herbs or spices.
What’s the best way to reheat leftovers?
Reheat gently on the stovetop with a splash of broth to keep rice moist. Microwave works too but cover loosely and stir halfway through for even heating.
Can I add other vegetables?
Yes, diced carrots, bell peppers, or zucchini work well. Add them with the rice and broth so they cook through during pressure cooking.
