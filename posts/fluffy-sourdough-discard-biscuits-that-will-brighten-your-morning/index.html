Fluffy Sourdough Discard Biscuits That Will Brighten Your Morning

Molly Dupont Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 15 min cook 8 servings

Introduction

I stumbled upon sourdough discard biscuits a few years ago when I didn’t want to waste my starter discard but also craved fresh biscuits. After a few tries, I nailed a recipe that’s fluffy with a perfect golden crust and a subtle tang from the discard. What sets this version apart is using cold butter cut into the flour and a quick mix that keeps the dough tender without overworking it. I remember my first batch came out tough because I mixed too much — lesson learned! Now, I just gently fold everything together and get them in the oven fast. The result is light, flaky biscuits that are perfect for slathering with butter and honey or sandwiching with eggs. They’re quick enough for a weekend morning but special enough to impress guests. Honestly, I think once you try these, you’ll keep your sourdough discard just for biscuits.

These biscuits are fluffy, buttery, and made using sourdough discard, so nothing goes to waste. They’re perfect for breakfast or a cozy brunch, with a slight tang that’s so satisfying. I love how quick they come together!

Why This Works

The magic behind these biscuits lies in the balance of ingredients and techniques that create a tender, flaky texture with a subtle tang. The cold butter cut into the flour forms tiny pockets of fat that melt during baking, releasing steam and creating layers. This is why handling the dough gently and keeping the butter cold is essential for flaky biscuits rather than dense ones.

The sourdough discard contributes acidity that reacts with the baking soda, enhancing leavening and adding complexity to the flavor. This natural fermentation also tenderizes the dough by breaking down gluten, making the biscuits softer. Using buttermilk alongside the discard further supports this reaction, ensuring a light crumb and slight tang.

Folding the dough gently before cutting helps build layers without overdeveloping gluten, which would toughen the biscuits. Cutting biscuits with a straight press rather than twisting preserves the edges, allowing the dough to rise evenly. Finally, brushing the tops with buttermilk or butter encourages Maillard browning, giving the biscuits their inviting golden crust and rich flavor.

Altogether, these steps create biscuits that are fluffy, tender, and full of flavor, making great use of sourdough discard while delivering a comforting breakfast staple.

  • Choose a good-quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for the best texture. This flour has enough protein to create structure but not so much that the biscuits become tough. Avoid using bread flour, which is higher in gluten and can yield chewier biscuits. Sifting the flour helps aerate it and ensures even mixing with leavening agents.
  • Use fresh, aluminum-free baking powder to ensure proper rise without metallic aftertaste. Baking powder provides most of the lift in this recipe, so expired powder can result in dense, flat biscuits. It reacts with moisture and heat to release carbon dioxide, creating the fluffy texture.
  • Baking soda reacts with acidic ingredients like sourdough discard and buttermilk to produce additional rise and tenderize the dough. Using the right amount is crucial; too much can cause a soapy flavor, while too little results in dense biscuits. Make sure it's fresh for maximum effectiveness.
  • Cold butter is critical for flaky biscuits. As it melts during baking, it creates steam pockets that separate dough layers. Using unsalted butter allows you to control salt levels precisely. Cut the butter into small cubes and keep it chilled until mixing to prevent it from blending fully into the flour.
  • Using unfed sourdough discard adds a subtle tang and acidity that tenderizes the dough and enhances flavor complexity. It also reacts with baking soda to help the biscuits rise. Make sure the discard is at room temperature for easier mixing and consistent results.

Pro Tips

  • Keep your butter and liquids cold until mixing. — Cold ingredients prevent the butter from melting into the flour too soon, which is essential for creating flaky layers. Warm butter blends completely with flour, resulting in dense biscuits. Cold liquids keep the dough cool and slow gluten development, producing a tender crumb.
  • Don’t overmix the dough once wet ingredients are added. — Overmixing develops gluten, making biscuits tough and chewy rather than soft and flaky. Stir just until the dough holds together to preserve tenderness. It’s okay if the dough looks a little shaggy or sticky—that’s actually a good sign.
  • Use a sharp biscuit cutter and press straight down without twisting. — Twisting seals the edges of the biscuits and prevents them from rising properly. Pressing straight down cuts clean edges that allow the dough to expand upward, producing taller, fluffier biscuits with light texture.
  • Rest the dough briefly in the fridge if it feels too warm. — If your kitchen is warm or the dough feels sticky and soft, chilling it for 15-20 minutes firms up the butter and slows gluten development. This helps maintain flakiness and prevents biscuits from spreading too much during baking.
  • Brush the tops with buttermilk or melted butter before baking. — This encourages a golden brown crust through Maillard browning, adding flavor and a lovely appearance. It also adds a slight tang if using buttermilk, complementing the sourdough discard’s flavor.
  • Use parchment paper to line your baking sheet. — Parchment prevents sticking and promotes even baking by insulating the bottoms of the biscuits. It also makes cleanup easier and helps maintain the biscuits’ shape during baking.
  • Serve biscuits warm for best texture and flavor. — Biscuits are at their fluffiest and most tender right out of the oven. As they cool, they can dry out or become crumbly. Reheating gently restores softness but fresh is always best.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overmixing the dough
    Why: Many cooks think mixing more thoroughly will give a better dough, but it actually develops gluten too much.
    Fix: Mix just until the wet and dry ingredients come together. Stop when the dough holds but is still a bit shaggy. Use a gentle folding motion instead of stirring vigorously.
  • Using warm butter
    Why: Butter softens quickly at room temperature, especially if hands are warm or kitchen is hot.
    Fix: Keep butter in the fridge until ready to cut in. Work quickly and chill dough if needed. Use a pastry cutter or two knives to avoid warming butter with your hands.
  • Twisting biscuit cutter
    Why: Twisting is a common instinct to release the dough from the cutter but seals edges.
    Fix: Press straight down firmly and lift straight up without twisting. Use a sharp cutter for clean cuts.
  • Not preheating the oven fully
    Why: Some rush the baking step, putting biscuits in before the oven is hot.
    Fix: Preheat oven to 425°F (220°C) and wait until fully heated before baking. This ensures biscuits rise quickly and brown properly.
  • Using old baking soda or powder
    Why: Leavening agents lose potency over time, leading to flat biscuits.
    Fix: Check expiration dates and test baking powder by mixing a teaspoon in warm water (it should bubble). Replace if expired.

Variations

  • Cheddar and Chive Sourdough Biscuits: Fold 1 cup shredded sharp cheddar cheese and 2 tablespoons chopped fresh chives into the dough before shaping. The cheese adds savory richness, while chives bring a fresh oniony note. These biscuits are perfect alongside soups or salads.
    Add cheese and herbs gently to avoid overworking dough; cheese may slightly soften the dough so handle carefully.
  • Garlic Herb Biscuits: Mix 1 teaspoon garlic powder and 1 tablespoon mixed dried herbs (like thyme and rosemary) into the dry ingredients. This variation has a fragrant, savory twist that pairs well with roasted meats or breakfast eggs.
    Ensure herbs are finely chopped or ground to distribute flavor evenly without tough bits.
  • Sweet Honey Butter Biscuits: Add 2 tablespoons sugar to the dry ingredients and brush tops with honey butter after baking. These are delightful for breakfast or brunch with jam or fruit preserves.
    Sugar slightly tenderizes dough; watch baking time to prevent over-browning.
  • Whole Wheat Sourdough Biscuits: Substitute half the all-purpose flour with whole wheat flour for a nuttier flavor and heartier texture. These biscuits are more rustic but still tender and perfect for sandwiches.
    Whole wheat absorbs more liquid; you may need to add an extra tablespoon of buttermilk to keep dough moist.
  • Spicy Jalapeño Biscuits: Fold in 1-2 finely chopped jalapeños and 1/2 cup shredded pepper jack cheese for a spicy kick. Great for pairing with chili or barbecue dishes.
    Remove seeds from jalapeños for milder heat; handle dough gently to keep texture.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Biscuits keep well at room temperature for up to 4 hours when covered loosely with a clean kitchen towel to prevent drying out. Avoid airtight containers at room temp as they can trap moisture and make biscuits soggy.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store biscuits in an airtight container or wrapped tightly in plastic wrap for up to 3 days. Refrigerated biscuits may become slightly denser and less flaky. Reheat gently in a warm oven or microwave to restore softness.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze biscuits individually wrapped in plastic wrap and placed in a freezer bag for up to 2 months. To thaw, leave at room temperature for 30 minutes or reheat directly from frozen in a 350°F oven for 10-12 minutes to refresh texture.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat biscuits in a 350°F oven for 8-10 minutes, wrapped in foil to prevent drying out. Alternatively, microwave on medium power for 20-30 seconds but avoid overheating which can make them tough. Adding a small pat of butter before reheating helps restore moisture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the biscuit dough up to the cutting stage 1 day ahead; wrap tightly and refrigerate. The next day, cut and bake as usual. This allows flavors to develop and saves time on busy mornings.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup cold unsalted butter, cubed
  • 1 cup sourdough discard (unfed, room temperature)
  • 3/4 cup buttermilk, cold

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preheating your oven to 425°F (220°C). In a large bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, baking soda, and salt. This step is crucial because it evenly distributes the leavening agents and salt through the flour, ensuring your biscuits rise properly and have balanced flavor. Skipping the sifting or mixing lightly can cause uneven rising or salty pockets. Make sure to use fresh baking powder and soda for best results.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are uniformly combined with no clumps or streaks
  2. Step 2

    Add the cold, cubed butter to the flour mixture. Using a pastry cutter or your fingertips, quickly cut the butter into the flour until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs with pea-sized bits of butter. This technique is key to creating flaky layers in your biscuits because the cold butter melts during baking, creating steam pockets. Avoid warming the butter with your hands; if it gets too soft, the biscuits will be dense.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Mixture looks crumbly with visible small chunks of butter
  3. Step 3

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sourdough discard and cold buttermilk until smooth. The sourdough discard adds a subtle tang and helps tenderize the biscuits, while the buttermilk reacts with the baking soda to provide lift and tenderness. Make sure your discard is at room temperature to mix smoothly without clumps.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Liquid mixture is smooth and homogeneous
  4. Step 4

    Pour the wet mixture into the dry ingredients and gently fold together with a spatula or wooden spoon. Stir just until the dough comes together; it will be sticky and shaggy. Overmixing develops gluten, making the biscuits tough, so resist the urge to knead or stir more than necessary. The dough should hold together but still look a bit rough.

    ⏱ 2-3 minutes✓ Dough forms a cohesive mass with no large dry patches
  5. Step 5

    Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Using floured hands, gently pat it into a rectangle about 1-inch thick. Fold the dough over on itself 2-3 times to create layers, then gently pat it back down to 1-inch thickness. This folding technique helps build flaky layers without toughening the dough. Avoid pressing too hard or overhandling.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Dough feels soft but holds shape with visible layers
  6. Step 6

    Using a floured 2.5-inch biscuit cutter, press straight down into the dough without twisting. Twisting seals the edges and prevents the biscuits from rising fully. Place the biscuits on a parchment-lined baking sheet, close but not touching to allow heat circulation.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Clean biscuit edges and uniform thickness
  7. Step 7

    Gather any scraps, gently re-pat the dough, and cut additional biscuits until all dough is used. Don’t overwork the scraps; just fold and pat lightly to keep tenderness.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ All dough portioned into biscuit shapes
  8. Step 8

    Brush the tops of the biscuits with a little buttermilk or melted butter to encourage browning and a shiny crust. This step adds a beautiful golden color and a slight tangy richness on top.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Evenly coated biscuit tops with wet sheen
  9. Step 9

    Bake in the preheated oven for 12-15 minutes until the biscuits are puffed and golden brown on top. Avoid opening the oven early to keep the heat steady. When done, the biscuits should sound hollow when tapped on the bottom and feel light.

    ⏱ 12-15 minutes✓ Golden brown tops and hollow sound when tapped

FAQ

Can I use active sourdough starter instead of discard?
Using active starter instead of discard can work but since active starter is more acidic and bubbly, it may affect the rise and flavor intensity. You might need to adjust the baking soda slightly. Also, active starter has more hydration, so reduce buttermilk a bit to keep dough consistency.
What if I don’t have buttermilk?
You can make a buttermilk substitute by adding 1 tablespoon of lemon juice or white vinegar to 3/4 cup of milk. Let it sit for 5 minutes before using. This adds the acidity needed to react with baking soda and tenderize the biscuits.
How do I store leftover biscuits without them getting dry?
Wrap biscuits tightly in plastic wrap or place in an airtight container to keep moisture in. Avoid leaving them uncovered or loosely covered for long periods. Reheating with a bit of butter or under foil helps restore softness.
Can I freeze the raw biscuit dough?
Freezing raw dough is possible but tricky because the butter can soften and affect texture. If freezing, shape biscuits first and freeze on a tray, then transfer to a bag. Bake from frozen adding a few extra minutes to baking time.
Why did my biscuits turn out dense?
Dense biscuits often result from overmixing the dough, using warm butter, or expired leavening agents. Mix gently, keep butter cold, and check that baking powder and soda are fresh for best rise and fluffiness.
Can I make these biscuits vegan?
You can substitute the butter with a vegan butter alternative and use a plant-based milk mixed with lemon juice as a buttermilk substitute. Note that the sourdough discard needs to be dairy-free as well. Texture might be slightly different but still delicious.
What’s the best way to reheat biscuits without drying them out?
Reheat biscuits wrapped in foil in a 350°F oven for 8-10 minutes. This traps moisture so they stay soft. Microwaving is faster but can dry biscuits if overheated, so use medium power and short bursts.
Can I add herbs or cheese to the dough?
Absolutely! Adding fresh herbs or shredded cheese is a fantastic way to customize flavor. Fold them in gently after mixing wet and dry ingredients to avoid overworking the dough. Expect a slightly different texture depending on additions.
