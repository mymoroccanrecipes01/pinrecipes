Authentic Italian Easter Cookies Your Family Will Adore, A Timeless Tradition

Molly Dupont Published on May 29, 2026
30 min prep 20 min cook 24 servings

Introduction

I've been making these Italian Easter cookies for years, ever since my Nonna shared her recipe with me. They're a staple in our family during springtime, bringing back memories of warm kitchens and laughter around the table. What makes this version special is the perfect balance of almond and anise flavors, which gives the cookies a subtle, fragrant lift that you don't find in typical sugar cookies. I learned early on that chilling the dough before baking is key—it helps the cookies hold their shape and develop that tender crumb everyone loves. When baked just right, these cookies have a delicate crunch on the outside and a soft, melt-in-your-mouth center. They’re simple to make but feel truly special, ideal for Easter celebrations or any time you want to share a bit of Italian heritage with your family. Honestly, my first attempt was a bit too dry because I skipped chilling, so don’t make that mistake! Trust me, the results are worth the wait.

These Italian Easter cookies are soft, lightly sweet, and bursting with almond flavor—a cherished family tradition I love sharing every spring. The secret? A touch of anise and a sprinkle of colored sugar. Ideal for holiday tables or anytime you want a little festive joy!

Why This Works

These cookies owe their tender crumb and delicate flavor to the careful balance of ingredients and technique. Creaming the butter and sugar incorporates tiny air bubbles, which help create a light texture once baked. The addition of eggs provides moisture and structure through protein coagulation, binding the dough together without making it tough.

The use of almond and anise extracts is more than just flavoring—they contribute aromatic compounds that elevate the cookie’s profile, making it uniquely festive. The anise extract, in particular, adds a subtle licorice note that complements the nutty sweetness of the almonds.

Chilling the dough is critical because it solidifies the fat, which slows spreading during baking and promotes a tender, chewy center with slightly crisp edges. Baking at 350°F allows for even cooking, enabling the Maillard reaction to develop golden edges and a crackled top without drying out the cookie’s interior.

Finally, the sprinkling of colored sanding sugar not only adds visual appeal but also a pleasant textural contrast, giving a slight crunch that enhances the overall eating experience. Together, these elements create a cookie that’s both traditional and perfectly balanced.

  • Choose a good quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for the best texture. It provides enough gluten to hold the cookie’s shape while allowing tenderness. Avoid cake flour here, as it can make the cookies too crumbly and fragile.
  • Use unsalted butter at room temperature for easy creaming. Butter contributes flavor and moisture, and its fat content affects spread and texture. Salted butter can throw off the balance, so stick to unsalted and add salt separately.
  • Regular granulated sugar is key for structure and sweetness. It also helps with the creaming process, creating air pockets. Avoid using fine sugars or substitutes as they can alter texture and sweetness levels.
  • This extract is essential for authentic flavor, imparting a mild licorice note that’s traditional in Italian Easter cookies. Pure anise extract is best; artificial versions may taste harsh or chemical.
  • Baking powder leavens the cookies gently, giving them a light rise without making them cakey. Make sure it’s fresh to ensure proper rising. Too much or expired powder can cause off flavors or poor texture.

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your flour and baking powder together before adding to the wet ingredients. — Sifting aerates the flour and ensures the baking powder is evenly distributed. This prevents clumps, which can cause uneven rising and dense spots in your cookies. It also helps the dough mix more smoothly.
  • Chill the dough for at least an hour before shaping and baking. — Chilling firms the butter, which controls spread during baking and helps maintain the cookie’s shape. It also allows the flavors to meld, resulting in a more aromatic and flavorful cookie.
  • Use room temperature butter for creaming but chill the dough afterward. — Room temperature butter creams easily with sugar, incorporating air for lightness. However, the dough benefits from chilling to prevent excessive spreading and to develop a tender texture.
  • Don’t overmix once the flour is added. — Overmixing develops gluten, which can make cookies tough and chewy instead of tender. Mix just until combined to keep the texture soft and delicate.
  • Use colored sanding sugar for decoration just before baking. — Adding sugar before baking lets it adhere and slightly melt, providing a pretty sparkle and a subtle crunch. Adding it after baking won’t stick as well and can fall off.
  • Rotate baking sheets halfway through baking. — Oven heat can be uneven, so rotating ensures cookies bake evenly, preventing some from burning while others remain undercooked.
  • Let cookies cool on the baking sheet for a few minutes before transferring. — They firm up as they cool, making them less fragile and easier to move without breaking or crumbling.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the dough chilling step.
    Why: Many rush through baking and skip chilling to save time, assuming it’s unnecessary.
    Fix: Plan ahead to chill the dough at least an hour. This step is vital to control spread and achieve the right texture. If you’re short on time, pop it in the freezer for 20-30 minutes but watch closely when baking.
  • Overmixing after adding flour.
    Why: It’s tempting to mix thoroughly to combine everything, but this can develop gluten.
    Fix: Mix just until the flour disappears. Use a spatula to fold gently if needed. This keeps the cookies tender and soft rather than tough.
  • Using salted butter and forgetting to adjust salt.
    Why: Sometimes cooks grab whatever butter is on hand and don’t adjust salt accordingly.
    Fix: Always use unsalted butter for baking and add salt separately. If you must use salted butter, reduce or omit added salt to avoid an overly salty cookie.
  • Not watching the cookies closely near the end of baking.
    Why: Ovens vary, and baking times can differ, so blindly following the timer can cause overbaking.
    Fix: Check cookies a few minutes before the timer ends. Look for lightly golden edges and crackled tops for perfect doneness.
  • Pressing the colored sugar too hard onto the dough balls.
    Why: Some think pressing sugar in ensures it sticks better.
    Fix: Gently sprinkle sugar on top without pressing. This keeps the sugar crystals intact, giving a nice sparkle and crunch after baking.

Variations

  • Lemon Zest Italian Easter Cookies: Add the zest of one lemon to the dough for a bright, citrusy twist on the classic. This variation adds freshness and pairs beautifully with the almond and anise flavors, perfect for springtime celebrations.
    Add lemon zest with the extracts in step 2 and reduce anise extract slightly to balance flavors.
  • Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies: Use a gluten-free all-purpose flour blend instead of regular flour. This makes the cookies accessible to those with gluten sensitivities while keeping the texture tender and delicious.
    Ensure your gluten-free blend contains xanthan gum or add 1/2 teaspoon to help with structure.
  • Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies: Once cooled, dip half of each cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. This adds a rich, indulgent layer that contrasts nicely with the almond flavor and adds a festive touch.
    Use good quality chocolate and chill dipped cookies on parchment to set.
  • Anise-Free Version: Simply omit the anise extract and add an extra 1/2 teaspoon of almond extract for those who prefer a milder flavor. The cookies remain flavorful and tender without the licorice note.
    Adjust extract amounts carefully to maintain balance without overpowering.
  • Honey-Sweetened Italian Easter Cookies: Replace half the sugar with mild honey for a natural sweetness and slight chewiness. This variation adds depth and a subtle floral note, great for those wanting less refined sugar.
    Reduce liquid slightly if dough feels too sticky and watch baking time closely.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 4 days. Keep them away from direct sunlight or heat to maintain their tender texture and prevent sugar from melting.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: You can refrigerate cookies for up to 7 days in an airtight container. Refrigeration may slightly harden the cookies, so bring to room temperature before serving for best flavor and texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a sealed container or freezer bag for up to 3 months. Separate layers with parchment paper to prevent sticking. Thaw at room temperature before eating to restore softness.
  • ♨️ Reheating: If desired, warm cookies in a 300°F oven for 5 minutes to refresh their softness. Avoid microwaving as it can make them tough or chewy. Add a splash of water to the oven tray for moisture if needed.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare dough up to 2 days ahead and keep refrigerated. Shape and bake cookies the day you plan to serve for freshest results. Alternatively, bake in advance and freeze fully cooled cookies.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • 1/2 teaspoon almond extract
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt
  • Colored sanding sugar for decoration

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by creaming the softened butter and sugar together in a large bowl until the mixture is light and fluffy. This step is crucial because it incorporates air into the dough, which helps the cookies rise and gives them a tender texture. Use a hand mixer or stand mixer on medium speed for about 3-5 minutes. If you rush this, the cookies can turn out dense and heavy. Watch for the mixture to become pale and smooth, indicating it's ready for the next ingredients.

    ⏱ 5✓ Butter and sugar mixture is pale, creamy, and smooth
  2. Step 2

    Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition to fully combine. Then stir in the anise and almond extracts. These extracts are what give these cookies their signature flavor, so don't skip or substitute them lightly. Mixing thoroughly ensures the flavor is evenly distributed. If you add eggs too quickly or skip mixing well, the dough can curdle or become uneven.

    ⏱ 3✓ Dough is smooth, glossy, and fragrant with almond and anise
  3. Step 3

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the flour, baking powder, and salt. This step ensures the leavening is evenly mixed and the salt is distributed, which balances the sweetness. Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, mixing on low speed or folding gently with a spatula. Overmixing here can develop gluten and make the cookies tough. The dough should come together but remain soft and slightly sticky.

    ⏱ 5✓ Dough forms a cohesive ball, slightly sticky but workable
  4. Step 4

    Cover the dough with plastic wrap and refrigerate for at least 1 hour. Chilling firms the dough, making it easier to shape, and helps the flavors meld. Skipping this step often leads to cookies spreading too much during baking. If you're short on time, 30 minutes is the minimum, but longer is better.

    ⏱ 60✓ Dough is firm to the touch and cold
  5. Step 5

    Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C) and line baking sheets with parchment paper. Scoop out tablespoon-sized portions of dough and roll them into balls. Place them on the baking sheet spaced about 2 inches apart. Press each ball lightly with your fingers or the bottom of a glass to flatten slightly. This helps them bake evenly and develop a delicate texture.

    ⏱ 15✓ Cookies are evenly spaced, slightly flattened balls on the tray
  6. Step 6

    Sprinkle the tops generously with colored sanding sugar before baking. This not only adds a festive touch but also a slight crunch and sweetness contrast. Be sure not to press the sugar in too hard or it will dissolve during baking.

    ⏱ 2✓ Sugar crystals are evenly distributed and visibly sparkling on each cookie
  7. Step 7

    Bake the cookies for 15-20 minutes, rotating the pans halfway through for even cooking. Watch closely starting at 15 minutes—the edges should be lightly golden while the centers remain soft. Overbaking will dry them out. The cookies are done when they have a slight crackle on top and feel set but not hard.

    ⏱ 20✓ Edges are golden, tops crackled, centers soft to touch
  8. Step 8

    Remove the cookies from the oven and let them cool on the baking sheet for 5 minutes before transferring to a wire rack. This resting time allows them to firm up a bit, preventing breakage. Trying to move them too soon can cause them to crumble.

    ⏱ 5✓ Cookies hold their shape when lifted and are cool enough to handle
  9. Step 9

    Once fully cooled, store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature. They stay fresh for several days and even taste better after a day as the flavors deepen. These cookies are perfect alongside coffee or tea, or as a sweet finish to your Easter meal.

    ✓ Cookies are completely cool and firm

FAQ

Can I substitute almond extract if I don’t have it?
Almond extract is key for authentic flavor, but if you don’t have it, vanilla extract can be used as a milder alternative. The cookies won’t have the same nutty aroma but will still be delicious. Use the same amount and consider adding finely chopped almonds for texture.
Why do my cookies spread too much and become flat?
This usually happens if the dough isn’t chilled or the butter is too soft. Chilling solidifies the fat, preventing excessive spread. Also, avoid over-creaming butter and sugar, which can cause cookies to flatten. Make sure your oven temperature is accurate too.
Can I use anise seeds instead of extract?
You can, but anise seeds have a stronger, more bitter flavor. If you use seeds, toast them lightly and grind before adding a small amount to the dough. Start with less than 1/2 teaspoon to avoid overpowering the cookies.
How do I store these cookies to keep them fresh?
Store in an airtight container at room temperature away from heat and moisture. Layer cookies with parchment paper to prevent sticking. They keep well for several days and actually taste better after resting a day or two.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
To make vegan, substitute butter with a plant-based margarine and replace eggs with flax eggs or commercial egg replacer. Texture may be slightly different—softer and less crisp—but still tasty. You might need to adjust baking time slightly.
Why are my cookies dry and crumbly?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or too much flour. Measure flour carefully, spooning into the cup rather than scooping. Also, bake just until edges are golden and centers set. Overbaking removes moisture, making cookies dry.
Can I freeze the dough before baking?
Yes! Freeze dough balls on a tray, then transfer to a bag once solid. Bake from frozen, adding a couple extra minutes to baking time. This is great for making ahead and enjoying fresh cookies anytime.
What’s the best way to achieve the crackled top on these cookies?
The crackled top forms naturally from the baking powder and dough moisture as the cookies bake. Make sure to chill the dough and don’t overmix. Also, sprinkling sanding sugar before baking enhances the crackled appearance.
