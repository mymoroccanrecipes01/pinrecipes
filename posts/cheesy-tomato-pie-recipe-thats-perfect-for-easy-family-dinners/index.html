Cheesy Tomato Pie Recipe That’s Perfect for Easy Family Dinners

This cheesy tomato pie is a cozy, comforting dish that’s bursting with fresh tomato flavor and melty cheese. The buttery crust and simple seasoning make it a perfect weeknight dinner or casual gathering treat.

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this cheesy tomato pie recipe during a summer cookout, and it quickly became a staple in my kitchen. Unlike the traditional tomato pies I’d tried before, this version balances fresh tomatoes with a blend of cheeses that melt into a luscious, savory filling. What really sets it apart is the simple mayo and Parmesan mix that adds a creamy tang without overpowering the tomatoes. I love how the crust crisps up perfectly while the cheese bubbles golden on top. It’s a dish that’s easy enough for a weeknight but special enough to bring to a family gathering. Over time, I’ve tweaked the seasoning and layering to get just the right balance — juicy tomatoes, cheesy richness, and a buttery crust that holds it all together. You’ll notice the difference right away, especially with the fresh basil and garlic powder that give it a subtle aromatic lift. It’s the kind of recipe that feels like a warm hug on a plate, and I hope it becomes as loved in your home as it is in mine.

What You'll Need

One 9-inch pie, serves 4

  • 1 pre-made pie crust (9-inch), chilled
  • 3 large ripe tomatoes, sliced about 1/4 inch thick
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon black pepper
  • 1 tablespoon olive oil
  • 1 cup shredded sharp cheddar cheese
  • 1/2 cup shredded mozzarella cheese
  • 1/4 cup grated Parmesan cheese
  • 2 tablespoons mayonnaise
  • 1 teaspoon dried basil
  • 1/2 teaspoon garlic powder

How To Do It

Step 1
Preheat your oven to 375°F (190°C). Place the chilled pie crust into a 9-inch pie dish, pressing it gently into the edges. To prevent a soggy bottom, prick the crust all over with a fork (this is called docking) so steam can escape while baking. This step helps the crust bake evenly and stay crisp once the juicy tomatoes are added later.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Crust edges start to turn a light golden color and feel firm to the touch
Step 2
Bake the crust blind (without filling) for about 10 minutes. Use pie weights or dried beans on parchment paper to keep the crust from puffing up. This partial baking ensures the crust sets before adding the moist tomatoes, preventing sogginess. Don’t skip this step; it’s key for a flaky, crisp base.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Crust looks dry and lightly golden, no puffing or bubbles
Step 3
While the crust bakes, slice the tomatoes about 1/4 inch thick and lay them on paper towels. Sprinkle salt over the slices and let them sit for 10 minutes to draw out excess moisture. This step is crucial to avoid a watery pie and intensifies the tomato flavor.
⏱ Time: 10
✓ Doneness: Tomato slices feel less wet and have released some liquid onto the paper towels
Step 4
Pat the tomato slices dry with fresh paper towels to remove the drawn-out moisture. This prevents sogginess and helps the cheese and crust stay crisp. Layering wet tomatoes without drying will make the pie watery and less flavorful.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Tomatoes feel dry to the touch but still juicy
Step 5
In a small bowl, mix the mayonnaise, grated Parmesan, dried basil, and garlic powder. This creamy mixture adds richness and a subtle tang that complements the tomatoes and cheese perfectly. Spread this mixture evenly over the partially baked crust to act as a flavorful barrier.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Mixture is smooth and evenly spread on crust
Step 6
Arrange a layer of tomato slices over the mayo mixture in the crust. Sprinkle with black pepper and a little salt if desired. Layering evenly ensures every bite gets a balanced tomato flavor. Avoid piling too thickly to maintain the pie's structure.
⏱ Time: 5
✓ Doneness: Tomatoes cover the crust in a single even layer
Step 7
Sprinkle half of the shredded cheddar and mozzarella cheeses over the tomato layer. Cheese melts and binds the filling, creating that signature gooey texture. Using a blend adds depth of flavor and a nice stretchiness.
⏱ Time: 3
✓ Doneness: Cheese layer evenly covers tomatoes
Step 8
Add a second layer of tomato slices, followed by the remaining cheese blend. This layering technique ensures cheesy pockets throughout the pie rather than just on top. It also helps balance moisture and flavor.
⏱ Time: 3
✓ Doneness: Top layer of tomatoes and cheese looks even and well-distributed
Step 9
Drizzle olive oil over the top and bake the pie in the preheated oven for 20-25 minutes, until the cheese is bubbly and golden and the crust is deep golden brown. The olive oil adds flavor and helps the cheese brown beautifully. Watch closely near the end to avoid burning.
⏱ Time: 25
✓ Doneness: Cheese is bubbly with golden spots and crust is golden brown

Pro Tips

Use paper towels to drain tomatoes thoroughly before layering.
Removing excess moisture prevents the crust from becoming soggy and helps the pie maintain its structure. If you skip this, the filling can become watery and the crust mushy, ruining the texture.
Blind bake the crust before adding filling.
Pre-baking sets the crust and creates a barrier that keeps it crisp despite the juicy tomato filling. Without this step, the crust tends to absorb moisture and turn soggy during baking.
Mix Parmesan cheese with mayonnaise for the base layer.
This combo adds a creamy, tangy layer that complements the tomatoes and cheese. It also helps bind the filling and prevents moisture from soaking into the crust.
Use a blend of sharp cheddar and mozzarella cheeses.
Sharp cheddar gives bold flavor, while mozzarella offers meltiness and creaminess. Together, they create a rich, balanced cheese profile that’s both flavorful and gooey.
Drizzle olive oil over the top before baking.
Olive oil helps cheese brown evenly and adds a subtle fruity aroma. It also prevents the cheese from drying out and forming a hard crust.
Layer tomatoes evenly and avoid piling too thickly.
Even layers ensure consistent cooking and prevent the pie from becoming too watery or collapsing. Thick piles can cause uneven baking and soggy spots.
Let the pie rest for 10 minutes before slicing.
Resting allows the filling to set, making it easier to cut clean slices without the filling spilling out. It also lets the flavors meld together for a better taste.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Skipping the tomato draining step

Why it happens Many cooks want to save time and skip salting and draining tomatoes, not realizing the moisture will ruin the crust.

Fix Always salt the tomato slices and let them sit on paper towels for at least 10 minutes, then pat dry before layering. This removes excess water and prevents sogginess.

Not blind baking the crust

Why it happens Some assume the crust will bake fully with the filling, but the moisture from tomatoes can keep it undercooked.

Fix Blind bake the crust for 10 minutes with weights to set it before adding filling. This ensures a crisp bottom and prevents sogginess.

Using watery or overripe tomatoes

Why it happens Sometimes ripe tomatoes are chosen for flavor but are too juicy, causing excess liquid.

Fix Choose firm slicing tomatoes like Roma and drain well. If tomatoes are very juicy, consider removing seeds to reduce moisture.

Overloading the pie with too much filling

Why it happens Trying to make the pie extra hearty can lead to uneven baking and soggy crust.

Fix Layer ingredients evenly and avoid piling too thickly. This helps the pie bake evenly and maintain structure.

Not letting the pie rest before slicing

Why it happens Impatience to serve leads to cutting the pie too soon, causing filling to spill out.

Fix Allow the pie to cool and set for 10 minutes after baking. This firms up the filling and makes slicing cleaner.

Variations

Add Fresh Basil and Oregano

For a more herbaceous flavor, add chopped fresh basil and oregano to the mayo mixture or sprinkle over the tomatoes before baking. This adds a bright, fresh note that complements the cheese and tomatoes beautifully.

Note: Fresh herbs release moisture, so add them sparingly to avoid sogginess.

Use a Buttery Pâte Brisée Crust

Swap the pre-made crust for a homemade pâte brisée to add a richer, flakier texture. This classic French-style crust enhances the pie’s buttery flavor and crispiness.

Note: Keep dough chilled and handle minimally to maintain flakiness.

Add Caramelized Onions

Layer thinly sliced caramelized onions under the tomatoes for a sweet, savory depth. The onions add complexity and pair wonderfully with the cheese and tomato acidity.

Note: Cook onions slowly until golden brown to develop sweetness without burning.

Make it Spicy with Red Pepper Flakes

Sprinkle red pepper flakes over the tomatoes or mix into the mayo base to add a gentle heat. This variation adds a spicy kick that livens up the classic flavors.

Note: Adjust amount to taste; too much can overpower the delicate tomato flavor.

Use Goat Cheese Instead of Mayonnaise

Replace the mayo and Parmesan mixture with softened goat cheese for a tangy, creamy layer that pairs well with tomatoes and herbs.

Note: Goat cheese is denser, so spread thinly to avoid heaviness.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

The pie can safely sit at room temperature for up to 2 hours if covered loosely with foil or a clean cloth. Beyond this, refrigeration is recommended to maintain freshness and prevent bacterial growth.

Refrigerator

Store leftover pie in an airtight container or cover tightly with plastic wrap in the fridge for up to 3 days. The crust may soften slightly when cold, so reheat before serving to restore crispness.

Freezer

Wrap the pie tightly in plastic wrap and foil to prevent freezer burn. Freeze for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating to maintain texture and flavor.

Reheating

Reheat slices in a 350°F oven for 10-15 minutes until warmed through and crust is crisp again. Avoid microwaving as it can make the crust soggy. Adding a splash of olive oil before reheating helps restore crispness.

Make Ahead

You can prepare the pie crust and mayo mixture 1 day ahead and keep refrigerated. Slice and drain tomatoes just before assembling to keep them fresh. Assemble and bake on the day you plan to serve for best texture.

FAQ

Can I use fresh mozzarella instead of shredded?
Fresh mozzarella has higher moisture content and can make the pie watery if not drained well. If using fresh, slice thinly and pat dry thoroughly before layering to reduce excess moisture.
How do I prevent the crust from getting soggy?
Blind baking the crust before adding filling and draining the tomatoes well are key steps. Also, spreading the mayo-Parmesan mixture on the crust creates a moisture barrier that keeps it crisp.
Can I make this pie vegan?
Yes, substitute the cheeses with vegan cheese alternatives and use vegan mayo. Keep in mind texture and meltability may differ, so choose high-quality vegan cheeses designed for baking.
What’s the best way to slice the pie without it falling apart?
Let the pie rest for at least 10 minutes after baking to let the filling set. Use a sharp knife and cut gently in a rocking motion to keep slices intact.
Can I add other vegetables to the pie?
Yes, thinly sliced zucchini or caramelized onions work well. Be mindful of moisture content and cook or drain them beforehand to avoid sogginess.
Is it okay to make the pie ahead and reheat?
You can assemble the pie a few hours ahead and refrigerate before baking. For leftovers, reheat in the oven to restore crust crispness. Avoid microwaving to maintain texture.
What type of tomatoes are best for this recipe?
Roma or plum tomatoes are ideal because they’re less watery and hold their shape well. Avoid overly ripe or juicy tomatoes to prevent a soggy pie.
Can I freeze leftover pie?
Yes, wrap tightly and freeze for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat in the oven for best texture and flavor.
