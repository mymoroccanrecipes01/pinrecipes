Crispy Wonton Soup Recipe That Brings the Perfect Crunch to Your Bowl

Molly Dupont Published on May 22, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 4 servings

This crispy wonton soup combines crunchy, golden wontons with a flavorful broth for an irresistible bowl. The secret is frying the wontons just right so they stay crisp in the soup. Great for a cozy dinner or when you want a fun twist on classic soup.

Introduction

I first tried crispy wonton soup on a chilly evening at a local Asian eatery, and I was hooked immediately. The crunch of the fried wontons paired with the warm, savory broth felt like the perfect comfort food. I started experimenting at home to recreate that magic, and this recipe was born. What sets this version apart is the balance between crispy and tender — frying the wontons just right so they stay crisp even when added to the broth. I also like to add fresh baby spinach for a pop of color and nutrition. The trick I learned is to fry the wontons in small batches and drain them well to keep them from getting soggy too quickly. When you serve it, the broth is fragrant with ginger and garlic, and each bite has that satisfying crunch. It’s a great dish for sharing with friends or a cozy solo dinner when you want something a little special but still comforting. I hope you enjoy making it as much as I do!

Why This Works

The success of this crispy wonton soup lies in balancing texture and flavor. The wontons are filled with a savory pork mixture that’s well-seasoned and moist, which ensures juicy bites inside a crispy shell. Frying the wontons at the right temperature (around 350°F) creates a golden, crunchy exterior through the Maillard reaction, a chemical process that develops complex flavors and appealing color. Overcrowding the oil lowers the temperature, causing soggy, greasy wontons, so frying in small batches is crucial.

The broth is kept simple but flavorful, using chicken stock enhanced with aromatics like ginger and garlic. Simmering gently preserves clarity and flavor, avoiding cloudiness that comes from boiling too hard. Adding fresh spinach just before serving keeps the greens vibrant and tender, adding nutritional value and a fresh contrast to the rich pork and fried wontons.

Sealing the wontons tightly prevents the filling from leaking during frying, which maintains the texture and presentation. The contrast between the crisp wonton and the warm broth is what makes this dish so enjoyable. Additionally, reheating tips help preserve the texture if you have leftovers, which is often a challenge with fried foods in soups.

Overall, this recipe harmonizes crispy textures with a comforting broth, while paying close attention to cooking temperatures and timing to deliver a satisfying and authentic experience every time.

  • Choose fresh, lean ground pork with a bit of fat content (around 20%) to keep the filling juicy but not greasy. The fat helps bind the mixture and adds flavor. Avoid extra lean pork as it tends to dry out during cooking, resulting in tough wontons.
  • Look for thin, square wonton wrappers made from wheat flour and water. Fresh wrappers are easier to fold and seal, but frozen work well if thawed properly. Avoid thick or overly dry wrappers which won’t crisp up nicely when fried.
  • Use a good quality light soy sauce for seasoning the filling and broth. It adds umami and saltiness without overpowering. Dark soy sauce is thicker and sweeter, better for braising than this recipe.
  • Pick a neutral oil with a high smoke point like canola or peanut oil for frying. This ensures the wontons crisp quickly without absorbing too much oil or burning. Olive oil isn’t recommended as it smokes at lower temperatures.
  • Homemade or low-sodium store-bought chicken broth works best. It provides a clean, savory base that complements the pork wontons. Avoid broth with strong additives or excess salt, which can overshadow the delicate flavors.

Ingredients

  • 20 wonton wrappers
  • 1/2 pound ground pork
  • 2 green onions, finely chopped
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
  • 1 teaspoon grated ginger
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 4 cups chicken broth
  • 1 cup baby spinach
  • Vegetable oil for frying
  • Salt and pepper to taste

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Start by preparing the wonton filling. In a bowl, combine the ground pork, finely chopped green onions, soy sauce, sesame oil, grated ginger, and minced garlic. Mix everything thoroughly with your hands or a spoon until well combined. This mixture is the flavor base for the wontons, so be sure to season with a pinch of salt and pepper. The soy sauce adds umami, while the ginger and garlic bring warmth and aroma. Mixing well ensures the filling is evenly seasoned and moist, which helps the wontons seal properly and taste delicious.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Filling should be well mixed and sticky but not watery.
  2. Step 2

    Place a teaspoon of the pork mixture in the center of each wonton wrapper. Wet the edges of the wrapper with a little water to help seal, then fold the wrapper over to create a triangle or pouch shape, pressing the edges firmly to prevent leaks. It’s important to remove any air pockets inside the wonton to avoid bursting during frying. Take your time sealing them tightly, as a good seal keeps the filling juicy and prevents oil from seeping in. If the edges aren’t sealed well, the wontons can open while frying, which makes a mess and affects texture.

    ⏱ 15 minutes✓ Wontons should be neatly sealed with no gaps or air bubbles.
  3. Step 3

    Heat about 2 inches of vegetable oil in a deep skillet or pot over medium-high heat until it reaches 350°F (175°C). To test, drop a small piece of wonton wrapper into the oil; it should sizzle immediately. Fry the wontons in batches, about 5-6 at a time, for 2-3 minutes until they turn golden brown and crisp. Avoid overcrowding the pan, which drops the oil temperature and leads to greasy, soggy wontons. Use a slotted spoon to transfer the fried wontons to a paper towel-lined plate to drain excess oil. The key here is to get a crunchy exterior without overcooking the filling inside.

    ⏱ 15 minutes✓ Wontons are crisp and golden, not dark brown or oily.
  4. Step 4

    In a separate pot, bring the chicken broth to a gentle boil over medium heat. Add a pinch of salt and pepper to taste. The broth should be flavorful but not overpowering so it complements the wontons. You can add a small piece of ginger or a smashed garlic clove to infuse extra aroma if you like, then strain before serving. Keep the broth hot but not boiling vigorously, which can make it cloudy and flatten the taste.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Broth is clear, aromatic, and simmering gently.
  5. Step 5

    Add the baby spinach to the hot broth just before serving and stir until wilted, about 1 minute. The spinach adds freshness and a mild earthiness that balances the rich pork wontons. Don’t add it too early or it will overcook and lose its vibrant color and texture.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Spinach is bright green and tender but not mushy.
  6. Step 6

    To serve, ladle the hot broth into bowls and carefully add 4-5 crispy wontons on top. The contrast between the crunchy wontons and warm broth is what makes this dish special. Eat the wontons quickly so they retain their crunch, or dip them briefly in the broth to soften slightly if preferred. Garnish with extra chopped green onions or a drizzle of chili oil for a little heat.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Soup is hot, wontons are crisp or slightly softened as desired.
  7. Step 7

    If you want to add more flavor, consider a splash of soy sauce or a sprinkle of toasted sesame seeds on top before serving. These little touches elevate the dish and bring extra depth without overpowering the delicate broth and wontons.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Seasonings are well balanced and enhance the overall flavor.
  8. Step 8

    For leftovers, keep the broth and wontons separate to maintain crispness. Reheat the broth gently on the stove, and crisp the wontons again in a hot pan or oven before serving. This helps preserve the texture and flavor better than microwaving everything together.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Broth steaming hot, wontons reheated to crisp.
  9. Step 9

    Clean up by straining leftover broth through a fine sieve to remove any bits, then store in airtight containers. Wontons that aren’t eaten can be refrigerated in a single layer to avoid sogginess. This way, you can enjoy the soup again the next day with minimal loss of quality.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Kitchen tidy, broth clear and ready for storage.

Pro Tips

  • Don’t overcrowd the frying pan when cooking wontons. — Overcrowding lowers the oil temperature quickly, which causes the wontons to absorb excess oil and become greasy instead of crisp. Maintaining a steady 350°F ensures the wontons cook evenly and develop a crunchy golden crust.
  • Dry wonton wrappers thoroughly before frying. — Any moisture on the wrapper surface causes the oil to splatter and prevents proper crisping. Patting them dry or allowing them to rest on paper towels before frying helps achieve that perfect crunch.
  • Use fresh aromatics like ginger and garlic in the filling. — Fresh ingredients release essential oils and flavors that elevate the pork filling. Powdered or dried versions lack the vibrant aroma and can make the filling taste flat or artificial.
  • Seal wontons tightly to avoid bursting. — Air pockets inside the wontons expand when frying and can cause them to burst open. Pressing out air and sealing edges properly keeps the filling enclosed and maintains the wonton’s shape and texture.
  • Add spinach to the broth just before serving. — Spinach wilts quickly and loses nutrients if cooked too long. Adding it last preserves its bright color, fresh flavor, and tender texture, balancing the richness of the fried wontons.
  • Use a thermometer to monitor oil temperature. — Maintaining consistent oil temperature is key for even frying. Too hot burns the wontons; too cool results in greasy, soggy texture. A thermometer takes the guesswork out and improves results.
  • Serve wontons immediately after frying for best crunch. — Wontons start to absorb broth and soften quickly. Serving right away ensures you enjoy the satisfying contrast between crispy exterior and juicy filling.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Wontons turning soggy quickly in the soup
    Why: Many assume frying wontons once is enough, but the hot broth softens the crispy wrapper fast, losing the crunch.
    Fix: Keep the fried wontons separate until just before serving. Add them to the broth at the last moment and eat quickly. You can also re-fry leftover wontons briefly to restore crispness.
  • Wontons bursting open during frying
    Why: Trapped air inside the wonton expands when heated, causing it to burst.
    Fix: Press out all air pockets before sealing and use water to tightly seal edges. Avoid overfilling the wontons to prevent leaks.
  • Greasy wontons from low oil temperature
    Why: If the oil isn’t hot enough, wontons absorb oil instead of crisping.
    Fix: Use a thermometer to keep oil at 350°F and fry in small batches to maintain temperature.
  • Flavorless broth overpowering the wontons
    Why: Using plain or bland broth without aromatics can make the soup dull.
    Fix: Simmer broth with ginger and garlic, and season with salt and pepper. Strain before serving for a clean taste.
  • Wontons sticking together when frying
    Why: Crowding the pan or wet wrappers cause wontons to stick.
    Fix: Fry in batches with enough space and pat wrappers dry before filling.

Variations

  • Shrimp and Pork Wontons: Add finely chopped shrimp to the pork filling for a seafood twist. This adds sweetness and a slightly firmer texture. It’s a great way to elevate the flavor while keeping the crispy exterior.
    Shrimp cooks quickly, so chop small and mix thoroughly. Fry and cook wontons the same way.
  • Vegetarian Crispy Wontons: Swap the pork for finely chopped mushrooms, tofu, and shredded cabbage. Season with soy sauce and sesame oil. The mushrooms add umami and moisture, making a delicious vegetarian filling.
    Squeeze excess water from mushrooms and tofu to avoid soggy filling. Fry as usual.
  • Spicy Wonton Soup: Add chili oil or fresh sliced chili peppers to the broth for heat. You can also mix some chili flakes into the filling for a spicy kick. This variation is perfect if you like your soup with a little fire.
    Adjust spice level gradually to avoid overpowering the delicate broth.
  • Baked Crispy Wontons: For a lighter version, brush wontons with oil and bake at 400°F until golden and crisp instead of frying. This reduces oil use but still delivers crunch.
    Flip wontons halfway through baking for even crispness.
  • Asian Herb Wontons: Add fresh herbs like cilantro, mint, or Thai basil to the filling for a fresh, aromatic flavor. This adds complexity and brightness to the pork mixture.
    Chop herbs finely and fold gently into filling to avoid bruising.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Keep the soup covered at room temperature for no more than 2 hours to avoid bacterial growth. Separate fried wontons from broth if not serving immediately to prevent sogginess.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store broth and fried wontons separately in airtight containers for up to 3 days. Reheat broth gently on the stove and re-crisp wontons in a hot pan or oven before serving.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze broth in airtight containers for up to 2 months. Wontons can be frozen before frying; freeze on a tray first then transfer to bags. Thaw in the fridge before frying fresh for best texture.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat broth on the stove over medium heat until steaming. Re-fry wontons in a hot pan with a little oil for 1-2 minutes to restore crispness. Avoid microwaving wontons directly as they become soggy.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare and fill wontons a day ahead, store them covered in the fridge. Fry just before serving for maximum crunch. Broth can be made earlier and reheated gently.

FAQ

Can I use store-bought wontons instead of making my own?
Yes, you can use store-bought wontons, but they may not be as fresh or flavorful as homemade. Also, some pre-made wontons are boiled or steamed rather than fried, so you might want to fry them yourself to get that crispy texture.
How do I keep wontons crispy in the soup?
The best way is to keep the fried wontons separate until just before serving. Add them to the hot broth right before eating to maintain their crunch. Alternatively, you can re-fry leftover wontons briefly to crisp them up again.
Can I freeze wontons after filling them?
Absolutely! Freeze the filled wontons on a baking sheet in a single layer until firm, then transfer to a freezer bag. When ready, fry them straight from frozen—just add a minute or two to the frying time.
What oil is best for frying wontons?
Use a neutral oil with a high smoke point like vegetable, canola, or peanut oil. These oils tolerate the high frying temperature without burning and won’t impart strong flavors.
How can I make the broth more flavorful?
Simmer the broth with aromatics like ginger, garlic, and green onions for at least 10 minutes to infuse flavor. Adding a splash of soy sauce or a few drops of sesame oil before serving also enhances the taste.
Can I bake wontons instead of frying?
Yes, baking is a healthier alternative. Brush wontons lightly with oil and bake at 400°F for about 12-15 minutes, flipping halfway through. They won’t be as deeply crispy but still delicious.
What can I substitute for ground pork?
Ground chicken, turkey, or finely chopped shrimp work well as substitutes. Just adjust cooking time slightly if needed and season to taste.
How do I prevent wontons from sticking when frying?
Make sure the oil is hot enough before frying and don’t overcrowd the pan. Also, pat the wontons dry before frying and fry in small batches to keep the temperature steady.
