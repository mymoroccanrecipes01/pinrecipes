Grandma’s Classic Southern Buttermilk Biscuits That Melt in Your Mouth

Molly Dupont Published on May 30, 2026
15 min prep 15 min cook 8 servings

These flaky, buttery biscuits are a family favorite that bring back cozy mornings. The secret? Cold buttermilk and minimal handling for tender layers. Perfect for breakfast or a comforting side!

Introduction

I first made these buttermilk biscuits years ago after my grandma shared her old recipe with me. I’ve tweaked it over time, but the heart of it remains the same—simple ingredients and a gentle touch. What sets this recipe apart is how I handle the dough: minimal mixing and keeping the butter cold to create those signature flaky layers. I remember the first time I rushed the process and ended up with tough biscuits—lesson learned! Now, I always chill the butter and buttermilk, and I fold the dough just enough to bring it together. The result is tender, golden biscuits that literally melt in your mouth. They’re perfect for slathering with butter and jam or serving alongside a hearty breakfast or dinner. Honestly, these biscuits have become a staple in my kitchen, and I think you’ll love how easy and rewarding they are to make.

Why This Works

What makes these biscuits so special is the interaction between cold butter, buttermilk, and minimal mixing. The cold butter pieces embedded in the dough melt during baking, releasing steam that creates flaky layers. If the butter warms too much or the dough is overworked, those layers won’t form properly, resulting in denser biscuits.

Buttermilk plays a dual role: its acidity reacts with baking soda, producing carbon dioxide bubbles that help the biscuits rise quickly and contribute to their tender crumb. The slight tang from buttermilk also adds depth to the flavor, balancing the richness of the butter.

Folding the dough a few times creates layers by stacking the butter and flour, which puff up when baked. This lamination technique is similar to making puff pastry but much simpler. It’s important to handle the dough gently to avoid developing gluten, which would make the biscuits tough instead of tender.

Finally, baking at a high temperature ensures the biscuits rise fast and get a golden crust without drying out. The quick heat locks in moisture, so you end up with a biscuit that’s crispy outside and soft inside.

  • Choose a good-quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for the best texture. Bleached flour can sometimes affect flavor and color. The protein content in all-purpose flour is just right to give structure without making the biscuits tough.
  • Use fresh baking powder to ensure proper rise. It provides the main leavening, creating bubbles that lighten the dough. Old or expired baking powder might cause flat biscuits.
  • Baking soda reacts with the acidity in buttermilk to produce carbon dioxide, enhancing rise and tenderness. Using too much can cause a soapy taste, so measure carefully.
  • Cold, unsalted butter is crucial. The butter’s water content creates steam which forms flaky layers. Salted butter can be used but adjust salt in the recipe accordingly.
  • Fresh, cold buttermilk adds moisture, acidity, and flavor. It tenderizes the dough and reacts with baking soda to create lift. If unavailable, mix milk with lemon juice or vinegar as a substitute.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 6 tablespoons cold unsalted butter, cubed
  • 1 cup cold buttermilk

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 425°F (220°C). This high temperature is key for a quick rise and golden crust. Line a baking sheet with parchment paper to prevent sticking. Having your oven fully preheated ensures the biscuits bake evenly and develop a nice crust quickly.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Oven is fully heated and ready to bake
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, whisk together the sifted flour, baking powder, baking soda, and salt. Sifting helps aerate the flour and evenly distribute the leavening agents, which is crucial for fluffy biscuits.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Dry ingredients combined with no lumps
  3. Step 3

    Add the cold, cubed butter to the flour mixture. Using a pastry cutter or your fingertips, quickly cut the butter into the flour until the mixture resembles coarse crumbs with pea-sized bits of butter visible. The cold butter creates steam during baking, which forms flaky layers. Avoid overworking the dough to keep the butter cold and intact.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Mixture looks crumbly with small butter pieces
  4. Step 4

    Pour the cold buttermilk into the flour and butter mixture. Stir gently with a fork or spatula just until the dough comes together. The buttermilk reacts with the baking soda to help the biscuits rise and adds a subtle tang. Overmixing will develop gluten and make biscuits tough.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Dough holds together but is still slightly shaggy
  5. Step 5

    Turn the dough out onto a lightly floured surface. Gently pat it into a rectangle about 1 inch thick. Fold the dough over onto itself 2-3 times, then pat it down again. This folding creates layers that make the biscuits flaky. Be gentle to avoid compressing the dough too much.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dough is smooth and layered but not dense
  6. Step 6

    Using a biscuit cutter or a glass, cut out biscuits straight down without twisting to avoid sealing the edges. Place them close but not touching on the prepared baking sheet. Gather scraps, pat together gently, and cut more biscuits. Twisting can prevent proper rising.

    ⏱ 7 minutes✓ Biscuits are evenly cut and placed on the sheet
  7. Step 7

    Brush the tops of the biscuits lightly with buttermilk or melted butter for a golden finish. This step helps create a beautiful crust and adds extra flavor. Be careful not to soak the dough or it may become soggy.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Biscuits have a light sheen on top
  8. Step 8

    Bake the biscuits in the preheated oven for 12-15 minutes until they’re puffed and golden brown. The heat causes the butter to steam and the leavening agents to activate, giving you those perfect layers and rise.

    ⏱ 12-15 minutes✓ Biscuits are golden on top and bottom, and spring back when touched
  9. Step 9

    Remove the biscuits from the oven and let them cool slightly on a wire rack. This prevents the bottoms from getting soggy. Serve warm with butter, honey, or your favorite jam. Enjoy the flaky, tender texture and rich buttery flavor!

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Biscuits are warm and slightly cooled, ready to eat

Pro Tips

  • Keep all ingredients and tools cold before mixing. — Cold ingredients prevent the butter from melting prematurely. This is essential for creating steam pockets during baking that make the biscuits flaky and tender. Warm butter blends too much and results in a denser texture.
  • Use a light hand when mixing and folding the dough. — Overworking the dough develops gluten, which makes biscuits tough and chewy. Gentle mixing just until ingredients come together ensures a soft, tender crumb.
  • Cut biscuits straight down without twisting the cutter. — Twisting seals the edges, restricting rise and preventing layers from forming. Cutting straight allows the biscuits to expand upwards for a fluffy texture.
  • Bake biscuits immediately after cutting. — Waiting too long allows the butter to soften and the dough to warm, which reduces flakiness and rise. Quick baking locks in steam and creates a golden crust.
  • Use parchment paper or a silicone mat to prevent sticking. — This helps biscuits bake evenly and makes cleanup easier. Avoid greasing the pan heavily, which can cause bottoms to get greasy or soggy.
  • Brush tops with buttermilk or melted butter for browning. — This adds flavor and encourages a beautiful golden color. Avoid soaking the tops to keep the crust crisp.
  • Let biscuits cool slightly on a wire rack before serving. — Cooling prevents moisture buildup underneath, which keeps bottoms crisp rather than soggy.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overmixing the dough
    Why: Many home cooks think mixing longer makes a better dough, but it actually develops gluten, making biscuits tough.
    Fix: Mix just until the dough comes together, then stop. Handle gently when folding to maintain tenderness.
  • Using warm butter
    Why: Butter softens quickly at room temperature, especially in warm kitchens, leading to loss of flakiness.
    Fix: Keep butter in the fridge until just before use and handle quickly. Chill dough if needed.
  • Twisting biscuit cutter when cutting
    Why: Twisting seals edges, preventing rise and layer formation.
    Fix: Press straight down and lift the cutter straight up without twisting.
  • Not preheating the oven fully
    Why: Starting in a cool oven causes dense, pale biscuits.
    Fix: Always preheat oven to 425°F before baking.
  • Using expired leavening agents
    Why: Old baking powder or soda loses potency, causing flat biscuits.
    Fix: Check expiration dates and test baking powder by mixing with hot water to see if it fizzes.

Variations

  • Cheddar and Chive Biscuits: Add 1 cup shredded sharp cheddar and 2 tablespoons chopped fresh chives to the dough for a savory twist. These are great for breakfast sandwiches or with soups.
    Fold cheese and herbs in gently during mixing to avoid overworking the dough.
  • Sweet Honey Butter Biscuits: Mix 2 tablespoons honey into the dough and brush tops with honey butter after baking for a sweet, sticky finish perfect for brunch or snacks.
    Reduce salt slightly to balance sweetness.
  • Gluten-Free Buttermilk Biscuits: Substitute all-purpose flour with a gluten-free blend and add 1 teaspoon xanthan gum to maintain structure. These biscuits are tender but require careful folding.
    Gluten-free dough is more delicate; handle with extra care.
  • Herbed Garlic Biscuits: Incorporate 1 teaspoon garlic powder and 1 tablespoon mixed dried herbs into the dry ingredients for a fragrant, savory biscuit that pairs well with dinner.
    Avoid adding fresh garlic to prevent moisture changes.
  • Buttermilk Drop Biscuits: Skip rolling and cutting; drop spoonfuls of dough onto the baking sheet for rustic, quick biscuits. Texture is slightly different—more tender but less flaky.
    Bake a minute longer to ensure doneness inside.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Biscuits can be kept covered at room temperature for up to 2 days. Use a clean kitchen towel or airtight container to prevent drying out. Best eaten fresh but still tasty within this time.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Store biscuits in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. They may lose some flakiness but can be warmed in the oven to refresh texture.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze biscuits wrapped tightly in foil or plastic wrap and placed in a freezer bag for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or at room temperature before reheating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat in a 350°F oven for 5-7 minutes until warmed through. Avoid microwaving as it can make them rubbery. A splash of butter on top before reheating helps restore moisture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: Prepare biscuit dough up to the cutting step, then cover and refrigerate for up to 24 hours. Cut and bake fresh when ready to serve for best rise and flakiness.

FAQ

Can I substitute regular milk for buttermilk?
Yes! If you don’t have buttermilk, mix 1 tablespoon lemon juice or white vinegar with 1 cup milk and let it sit for 5-10 minutes before using. This mimics the acidity of buttermilk, which helps the biscuits rise and tenderize.
Why are my biscuits dense and not fluffy?
This usually happens if the dough is overmixed or the butter is too warm. Overmixing develops gluten, making biscuits tough. Always mix gently and keep butter cold for flaky layers.
Can I freeze the biscuits after baking?
Absolutely! Let them cool completely, then wrap tightly and freeze. Reheat in the oven for best texture. Freezing before baking is also an option if you want fresh biscuits later.
What’s the best flour to use?
All-purpose flour works best for tender but sturdy biscuits. Avoid bread flour, which has too much protein and can make biscuits chewy, and cake flour, which is too soft and may cause them to fall apart.
How do I get flaky layers?
Use cold butter cut into coarse pieces and fold the dough gently a few times. This layering traps steam during baking, causing the dough to puff and create flaky biscuits.
Can I make these biscuits vegan?
You can try substituting plant-based milk with lemon juice for acidity and vegan butter, but results may vary. The texture might be less flaky because of differences in fat content and acidity.
Why do I need to avoid twisting the biscuit cutter?
Twisting seals the dough edges, preventing the biscuits from rising properly. Press straight down and lift straight up to keep edges open for better rise.
Can I add herbs or cheese?
Yes! Adding herbs or cheese is a great way to customize. Fold them in gently with the flour before adding buttermilk to avoid overmixing.
