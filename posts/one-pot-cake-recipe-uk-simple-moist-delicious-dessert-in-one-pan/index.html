One Pot Cake Recipe UK: Simple, Moist & Delicious Dessert in One Pan

Molly Dupont Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 6 servings

Introduction

I first stumbled upon the idea of a one pot cake while trying to cut down on washing up after baking. It sounded too good to be true—could a cake really come together in just one pan without sacrificing taste or texture? After a few experiments, I perfected this UK-style one pot cake recipe that’s moist, fluffy, and so simple. What sets this apart is using melted butter and milk combined right in the mixing bowl, which keeps everything smooth and light without overworking the batter. I also like adding a bit of lemon zest or fresh berries to brighten the flavor, but it’s just as lovely plain. The best part? You don’t have to dirty multiple bowls or spend ages cleaning up. It’s perfect for when you want to bake something sweet but don’t have much time or energy. The cake comes out golden, tender, and with a lovely crumb that’s just right for afternoon tea or a quick dessert. I hope you enjoy making it as much as I do!

This one pot cake is a lifesaver for busy days—no fuss, no mess, just a moist, delicious cake made all in one pan. I love how it uses simple UK pantry staples and comes together quickly. Perfect for afternoon tea or a cozy dessert!

Why This Works

This one pot cake recipe succeeds because it balances simplicity with careful technique to produce a moist, tender crumb without fuss. First, using self-raising flour means the leavening is already mixed in, ensuring even rising without needing additional ingredients. The baking powder complements this by creating carbon dioxide bubbles during baking, which expand and give the cake its lift.

The melted butter plays a crucial role in moisture and texture. Unlike creaming butter and sugar separately, melted butter coats the flour proteins, limiting gluten formation that can toughen cakes. This results in a soft, tender crumb. Whisking the wet ingredients together before combining with dry ensures even distribution and prevents overmixing, which can develop gluten and make the cake dense.

Folding the dry and wet ingredients gently maintains the air bubbles created during mixing, which helps the cake rise and stay fluffy. The baking temperature of 180°C (160°C fan) is ideal to allow gradual rising and browning without drying out the cake. Finally, letting the cake cool in the tin briefly sets its structure, preventing it from breaking when removed.

Overall, this recipe’s success lies in minimal ingredients, a single bowl technique that reduces handling, and attention to mixing and baking details that preserve moisture and texture.

  • Look for a good quality self-raising flour that’s fresh to ensure proper rising. It contains a balanced amount of baking powder and salt, which helps the cake rise without needing extra leavening. Avoid using plain flour unless you add baking powder separately, as this can affect texture.
  • Caster sugar dissolves quickly and evenly, which helps create a fine crumb. Using granulated sugar can work but might result in a coarser texture. Make sure sugar is fresh and not clumped to blend well with the flour.
  • Using unsalted butter allows you to control the salt level in the cake. Melt the butter gently to avoid overheating, which can cook the eggs when mixed. High-quality butter adds richness and moisture that’s key to a tender crumb.
  • Eggs provide structure and moisture. Use large eggs at room temperature for best emulsification with the butter and milk. Cold eggs can cause the batter to seize or mix unevenly, affecting texture.
  • Whole milk adds fat and moisture, contributing to the cake’s softness. You can substitute with semi-skimmed, but whole milk yields a richer flavor. Warm milk blends better with melted butter and eggs, helping create a smooth batter.

Pro Tips

  • Always sift your dry ingredients before mixing. — Sifting aerates the flour and evenly distributes the baking powder, which helps the cake rise uniformly and prevents lumps that can cause uneven texture.
  • Use room temperature eggs for better batter consistency. — Cold eggs can cause the melted butter to solidify or the batter to curdle, resulting in a less smooth mix and a denser cake.
  • Don’t overmix the batter once wet and dry ingredients are combined. — Overmixing develops gluten, making the cake tough rather than tender and fluffy. Fold gently until just combined for the best crumb.
  • Tap the cake tin on the counter before baking. — This releases large air bubbles trapped in the batter, preventing holes or tunnels in the finished cake.
  • Add fresh zest or berries for a flavor boost. — Fresh ingredients brighten the cake and add moisture pockets, making each bite interesting and fresh.
  • Cool the cake in the tin for 10 minutes before removing. — This resting time helps the cake set and prevents it from breaking apart when you transfer it to a cooling rack.
  • Use parchment paper to line your tin for easy removal. — This avoids sticking and keeps the cake’s edges intact, making slicing neater and presentation better.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overmixing the batter
    Why: Many assume mixing more will make the batter smoother, but it actually develops gluten, making the cake tough.
    Fix: Mix gently and only until the ingredients are just combined. Stop as soon as you don’t see dry flour. Use folding motions rather than stirring.
  • Using hot melted butter
    Why: Some melt butter in the microwave or on the stove without checking temperature, leading to hot butter that can cook eggs on contact.
    Fix: Let melted butter cool slightly to warm or room temperature before whisking with eggs to avoid curdling and ensure smooth batter.
  • Opening the oven door too early
    Why: Impatience or checking can cause the cake to collapse if the structure hasn’t set yet.
    Fix: Wait at least 20 minutes before peeking. Use the oven light to check color instead of opening the door.
  • Not preparing the tin properly
    Why: Skipping greasing or lining can cause the cake to stick and break when removed.
    Fix: Always grease the tin well and line with parchment paper if possible for easy release.
  • Using expired baking powder
    Why: Old baking powder loses its potency, resulting in a dense, flat cake.
    Fix: Check the expiry date and test by mixing a small amount with warm water to see if it bubbles before using.

Variations

  • Lemon and Blueberry One Pot Cake: Add zest of one lemon and 100g fresh blueberries to the batter before baking. The lemon zest brightens the flavor, and the blueberries add juicy bursts that make the cake fresh and summery.
    Fold berries in gently to prevent them from breaking and turning the batter purple. Baking time remains the same.
  • Chocolate Chip One Pot Cake: Stir in 100g of chocolate chips or chunks into the batter for a sweet twist. The chocolate melts slightly during baking, creating gooey pockets throughout the cake.
    Use good quality chocolate for best flavor. No change in baking time needed.
  • Spiced Apple One Pot Cake: Add 1 tsp mixed spice and 1 grated apple to the batter for a cozy, autumn-inspired cake. The spice warms the flavor while the apple adds moisture and texture.
    Drain excess juice from grated apple to avoid soggy batter. Baking time may increase by 5 minutes.
  • Vegan One Pot Cake: Replace eggs with 2 flax eggs (2 tbsp ground flaxseed + 6 tbsp water) and use plant-based milk and vegan butter. This keeps the cake moist and tender without animal products.
    Flax eggs need time to thicken before mixing. Baking time may be slightly longer—check doneness carefully.
  • Gluten-Free One Pot Cake: Use a gluten-free self-raising flour blend instead of regular flour. The cake remains soft and fluffy, perfect for those avoiding gluten.
    Ensure the blend contains xanthan gum or similar stabilizer. Baking time and temperature stay the same.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store the cooled cake covered with a clean tea towel or in a cake dome at room temperature for up to 2 days. This helps maintain moisture and prevents it from drying out.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: Keep the cake in an airtight container in the fridge for up to 4 days. The texture may firm up slightly when chilled; it's best to bring to room temperature before serving or warm gently.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Wrap the cake tightly in cling film and then foil to freeze for up to 2 months. To thaw, leave overnight in the fridge or on the counter for a few hours. Avoid microwaving frozen cake directly to keep texture intact.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Warm slices gently in a microwave for 15-20 seconds or in a low oven (150°C) for 5-8 minutes. Adding a small splash of milk before warming can help restore moisture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the batter up to a day ahead and keep it covered in the fridge. Bring to room temperature before baking. Alternatively, bake the cake and freeze it; thaw before serving for a quick dessert.

Ingredients

  • 200g self-raising flour, sifted
  • 150g caster sugar
  • 1 tsp baking powder
  • 100g unsalted butter, melted
  • 2 large eggs
  • 120ml whole milk
  • 1 tsp vanilla extract
  • Optional: zest of 1 lemon or 100g fresh berries

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Preheat your oven to 180°C (160°C fan) and grease an 8-inch round cake tin or line it with parchment paper. This step ensures your cake doesn’t stick and bakes evenly. It’s important to prepare your tin before mixing the batter to avoid delays that can affect the rising.

    ⏱ 10 minutes✓ Tin is fully greased or parchment is snugly fitted without wrinkles
  2. Step 2

    In a large mixing bowl, combine the sifted self-raising flour, caster sugar, and baking powder. Sifting helps aerate the flour and removes lumps, which leads to a lighter cake texture. Mix these dry ingredients well to distribute the baking powder evenly.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Dry ingredients are evenly combined with no visible clumps
  3. Step 3

    In a separate jug or bowl, whisk together the melted butter, eggs, milk, and vanilla extract until smooth. The melted butter should be warm, not hot, so it blends easily without cooking the eggs. This mixture adds moisture and richness to the cake.

    ⏱ 5 minutes✓ Wet ingredients are well combined and slightly frothy
  4. Step 4

    Pour the wet ingredients into the dry ingredients all at once. Using a spatula or wooden spoon, gently fold the mixture together until just combined. Avoid overmixing because that can develop gluten and make the cake tough. A few small lumps are fine.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter is smooth but not overworked, with no large flour pockets
  5. Step 5

    If you’re using lemon zest or berries, fold them in gently now. Be careful with berries—they can break and color the batter if mixed too vigorously. This adds freshness and a burst of flavor to the cake.

    ⏱ 2 minutes✓ Add-ins are evenly distributed without crushing berries
  6. Step 6

    Pour the batter into your prepared cake tin and smooth the top with a spatula. This helps the cake bake evenly and rise uniformly. Tap the tin lightly on the counter to release any large air bubbles trapped in the batter.

    ⏱ 3 minutes✓ Batter surface is level and smooth with no big air pockets
  7. Step 7

    Bake in the preheated oven for 25-30 minutes. Avoid opening the oven door early to prevent the cake from collapsing. The baking powder activates to create air pockets, making the cake rise and become fluffy.

    ⏱ 25-30 minutes✓ Cake is golden on top and springs back when lightly pressed
  8. Step 8

    Test the cake by inserting a skewer or toothpick into the center. If it comes out clean or with a few moist crumbs, it’s done. If wet batter sticks, bake for a few more minutes and test again. This ensures the cake is cooked through without drying out.

    ⏱ 5 minutes (testing included)✓ Skewer comes out clean or with only moist crumbs attached
  9. Step 9

    Allow the cake to cool in the tin for 10 minutes before transferring it to a wire rack. Cooling in the tin first helps the cake set and reduces the risk of breaking. Once on the rack, it cools evenly and prevents sogginess on the bottom.

    ⏱ 10-15 minutes✓ Cake is firm enough to remove from tin without crumbling

FAQ

Can I use plain flour instead of self-raising flour?
You can, but you'll need to add baking powder to help the cake rise. For every 200g plain flour, add about 2 tsp baking powder. Sift them together well to distribute the leavening evenly.
What if I don’t have caster sugar?
Granulated sugar can be used, but it may give a slightly coarser crumb. If you have icing sugar, it’s best to avoid it as it can make the batter too soft and affect texture.
How do I know when the cake is fully baked?
Insert a skewer or toothpick into the center. If it comes out clean or with a few moist crumbs, the cake is done. If wet batter sticks, bake for a few more minutes and test again.
Can I double this recipe for a larger cake?
Yes, but use a larger tin and increase baking time accordingly. Check doneness by skewer testing after the minimum time and add more time in 5-minute increments as needed.
What’s the best way to store leftover cake?
Keep it in an airtight container at room temperature for a couple of days or refrigerate for up to 4 days. For longer storage, freeze wrapped tightly to maintain freshness.
Can I add other flavorings or fruits?
Absolutely! Lemon zest, berries, chocolate chips, or spices like cinnamon work great. Just fold them in gently to avoid overmixing or crushing delicate fruits.
Why is my cake dense instead of fluffy?
This usually happens from overmixing, expired baking powder, or not enough leavening. Be gentle when mixing and use fresh ingredients to ensure a light texture.
Can I make this recipe vegan?
Yes, replace eggs with flax eggs and use plant-based milk and vegan butter. The texture will be slightly different but still moist and tasty.
