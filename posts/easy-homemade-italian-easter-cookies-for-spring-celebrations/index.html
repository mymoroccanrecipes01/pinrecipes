Easy Homemade Italian Easter Cookies for Spring Celebrations

Molly Dupont Published on May 29, 2026
15 min prep 30 min cook 24 servings

Introduction

I first stumbled upon this Italian Easter cookie recipe years ago during a family holiday gathering. My grandmother’s version was a little tricky to get just right, often too dry or crumbly, but I’ve tweaked it to make it easier and more foolproof. What I love about this recipe is how simple the ingredients are, yet the cookies turn out soft, tender, and lightly flavored with anise and lemon zest, giving them a fresh spring vibe. The trick I learned is to cream the butter and sugar thoroughly and not to overwork the dough, which keeps the cookies from becoming tough. These cookies are the perfect companion to a cup of coffee or tea and a lovely way to celebrate the season with family and friends. Plus, they’re quick to whip up, so you can enjoy baking without spending hours in the kitchen. You'll notice they bake up golden and just a bit crisp on the edges, but still soft inside—exactly how I remember my grandma’s, but easier to make!

These Italian Easter cookies are a delightful spring treat, soft and subtly sweet with a hint of anise. I love how the simple dough comes together quickly and bakes into little golden pillows. Perfect for sharing at family gatherings or holiday brunch!

Why This Works

These Italian Easter cookies owe their tender texture and delicate flavor to a few key elements working in harmony. First, the creaming of the butter and sugar is crucial; it traps air in the mixture, which helps the cookies rise gently and stay soft rather than dense. This aeration is what makes the dough light and fluffy before baking.

The use of baking powder as a leavening agent provides a controlled rise, creating a soft crumb without making the cookies cakey or heavy. It reacts during baking to release carbon dioxide, which forms tiny air pockets, giving the cookies their characteristic lightness.

Flavor-wise, the anise extract and lemon zest are traditional touches that brighten the dough and add a subtle aromatic complexity. Anise provides that classic licorice-like note often found in Italian baking, while lemon zest adds fresh citrus oils that cut through the sweetness and enrich the overall taste.

Finally, careful mixing and baking time prevent overdevelopment of gluten and overbaking, both of which can make the cookies tough or dry. The result is a cookie that’s tender, slightly chewy in the center, with a delicate crispness on the edges—perfect for spring celebrations and sharing with loved ones.

  • Choose a good-quality, unbleached all-purpose flour for this recipe. It provides the right balance of protein to create structure without making the cookies tough. Sifting the flour before mixing helps aerate it and removes lumps, which is essential for achieving the light texture characteristic of these cookies.
  • Using unsalted butter lets you control the salt level precisely. Softened butter is key for proper creaming with sugar, which incorporates air and ensures a tender crumb. Avoid melted butter here, as it changes the dough’s consistency and can lead to denser cookies.
  • This is the signature flavor in many Italian Easter cookies. Look for pure anise extract or high-quality anise oil for the best aroma and taste. It adds a subtle licorice note that pairs beautifully with lemon zest. If you can’t find it, a small amount of finely ground anise seeds can be used, but the flavor will be more intense.
  • A double-acting baking powder is preferred because it releases gas during mixing and again when heated, giving the cookies a gentle rise. Make sure it’s fresh as expired baking powder can result in flat, dense cookies. This ingredient is what keeps the texture soft and light rather than heavy.
  • Dusting with powdered sugar adds a delicate sweetness and a pretty finish. It dissolves quickly on the cookie surface and enhances the visual appeal without overpowering the flavor. Use fresh, fine powdered sugar for the best effect.

Pro Tips

  • Use room temperature butter for creaming. — Room temperature butter creams better with sugar, trapping air that helps the cookies rise and become tender. If butter is too cold, it won’t mix well; if too warm, it can become greasy and won’t hold air properly.
  • Don’t overmix the dough once you add flour. — Overmixing develops gluten, which makes cookies tough and dense. Mixing just until combined keeps the texture soft and delicate, which is essential for these Italian Easter cookies.
  • Sift your dry ingredients before mixing. — Sifting eliminates lumps and aerates the flour and baking powder, leading to a lighter dough and more even baking. It helps ensure consistent texture and prevents uneven rising.
  • Space cookies well on the baking sheet. — Giving cookies enough room allows them to spread without merging, which ensures even baking and maintains their individual shape. Crowding can cause uneven cooking and misshapen cookies.
  • Use fresh baking powder for best results. — Expired baking powder loses its potency, resulting in flat, dense cookies. Fresh baking powder ensures proper leavening and a light, tender crumb.
  • Cool cookies on the baking sheet before transferring. — Letting cookies rest briefly on the sheet allows them to firm up slightly, reducing the risk of breakage when moving to a cooling rack. This step helps maintain the perfect shape.
  • Add lemon zest for a fresh, bright flavor. — Lemon zest adds essential oils that brighten the cookie’s flavor, balancing the sweetness and complementing the anise. It’s a small addition that makes a big difference.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using melted butter instead of softened butter
    Why: Many assume melted butter is easier to mix, but it changes the dough’s texture and prevents proper creaming with sugar.
    Fix: Always use butter softened to room temperature and cream it with sugar until fluffy. This traps air and creates a tender crumb. If you accidentally use melted butter, expect denser cookies.
  • Overmixing the dough after adding flour
    Why: It’s tempting to mix thoroughly to combine ingredients, but this develops gluten, making cookies tough.
    Fix: Mix just until the flour disappears. Use a spatula to fold gently if possible. This keeps the dough tender and the cookies soft.
  • Not spacing cookies far enough apart on the baking sheet
    Why: In a rush, cookies get placed too close, causing them to merge during baking.
    Fix: Leave at least 2 inches between each cookie to allow spreading without sticking together, ensuring even baking and nice shapes.
  • Baking too long causing dry cookies
    Why: Cookies can go from perfectly soft to dry quickly if left in the oven too long.
    Fix: Watch for lightly golden edges and soft centers, then remove immediately. They will firm up as they cool.
  • Using old or expired baking powder
    Why: Expired leavening agents lose effectiveness, leading to flat, dense cookies.
    Fix: Check the expiration date and test baking powder by mixing a small amount with hot water—if it bubbles vigorously, it’s fresh.

Variations

  • Glazed Italian Easter Cookies: After baking, dip the cooled cookies in a simple lemon glaze made from powdered sugar and lemon juice. This adds a shiny, sweet finish with a tangy kick that pairs beautifully with the anise flavor.
    Allow cookies to cool completely before glazing to prevent melting. The glaze will set as it dries, adding a slight crispness on top.
  • Almond Italian Easter Cookies: Add 1/2 cup finely ground almonds to the dough for a nutty twist. This variation adds moisture and a subtle crunch, enhancing the cookie’s texture and flavor profile.
    Reduce flour slightly to compensate for the almond meal’s moisture to maintain dough consistency.
  • Chocolate-Dipped Italian Easter Cookies: Dip half of each cooled cookie in melted dark chocolate and let set. The rich chocolate contrasts nicely with the anise and lemon flavors, making these perfect for chocolate lovers.
    Use good-quality chocolate and temper if possible for a shiny finish and snap.
  • Gluten-Free Italian Easter Cookies: Swap all-purpose flour for a gluten-free blend with xanthan gum. These cookies stay tender and flavorful while accommodating gluten sensitivities.
    Monitor dough texture; gluten-free dough may be slightly more delicate, so handle gently when shaping.
  • Spiced Italian Easter Cookies: Add 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon and a pinch of nutmeg to the dry ingredients for a warm, cozy spice note that complements the lemon and anise beautifully.
    Adjust spices to taste; too much can overpower the delicate flavors.

Storage & Tips

  • 🌡️ Room Temperature: Store these cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. Keep them away from heat and humidity to maintain their soft texture. Covering tightly prevents them from drying out.
  • ❄️ Refrigerator: You can refrigerate the cookies for up to a week in an airtight container. The texture will firm up slightly and flavor may deepen. Let them come to room temperature before serving for best taste.
  • 🧊 Freezer: Freeze cookies in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer-safe container or bag for up to 3 months. Wrap well to prevent freezer burn. Thaw at room temperature before eating.
  • ♨️ Reheating: Reheat gently in a low oven (300°F) for 5-7 minutes to refresh softness. Avoid microwaving as it can make them rubbery. Adding a small piece of bread in the container helps retain moisture.
  • 📅 Make Ahead: You can prepare the dough a day ahead and keep it wrapped tightly in the fridge. Shape and bake the cookies on the day you plan to serve them for the freshest texture and flavor.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups all-purpose flour, sifted
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, softened
  • 3 large eggs
  • 2 teaspoons baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon anise extract
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • Zest of 1 lemon
  • Powdered sugar for dusting

How To Do It

  1. Step 1

    Begin by preheating your oven to 350°F (175°C) and lining two baking sheets with parchment paper. This ensures even baking and prevents sticking, which is crucial for delicate cookies like these. Preparing your workspace ahead of time helps the process flow smoothly without interruptions.

    ⏱ 10✓ Oven preheated to correct temperature; baking sheets ready
  2. Step 2

    In a large bowl, cream together the softened butter and granulated sugar until the mixture is light and fluffy. This step is important because it incorporates air into the dough, making the cookies tender and giving them a nice rise. Use a hand mixer or stand mixer for about 3-5 minutes on medium speed, scraping down the sides as needed.

    ⏱ 5✓ Butter and sugar mixture looks pale and fluffy
  3. Step 3

    Add the eggs one at a time, beating well after each addition. This helps the eggs fully incorporate and emulsify with the butter and sugar, ensuring a smooth dough. Then mix in the vanilla and anise extracts along with the lemon zest for that signature fresh, aromatic flavor.

    ⏱ 5✓ Dough is smooth and fragrant with anise and lemon
  4. Step 4

    In a separate bowl, whisk together the sifted flour and baking powder. Sifting is key here to avoid lumps and to aerate the flour, which contributes to the cookies’ light texture. Baking powder gives the cookies a gentle lift so they aren’t dense.

    ⏱ 5✓ Flour mixture is light and lump-free
  5. Step 5

    Gradually add the dry ingredients to the wet mixture, mixing on low speed or folding gently with a spatula. Overmixing at this stage can develop gluten and make the cookies tough, so combine just until no streaks of flour remain. The dough should be soft but not sticky.

    ⏱ 5✓ Dough is uniform and soft, easy to handle
  6. Step 6

    Using a teaspoon or small cookie scoop, drop rounded spoonfuls of dough onto the prepared baking sheets, spacing them about 2 inches apart. This spacing allows the cookies to spread slightly without merging. If you want a more uniform shape, you can gently roll the dough balls in your hands.

    ⏱ 5✓ Cookie dough balls evenly spaced on trays
  7. Step 7

    Bake in the preheated oven for 12-15 minutes, or until the edges are lightly golden but the centers remain soft. Baking too long will dry them out, so keep an eye on the color. The cookies will firm up as they cool but should still feel tender to the touch when you remove them.

    ⏱ 15✓ Edges are golden; centers look set but soft
  8. Step 8

    Remove the cookies from the oven and let them cool on the baking sheets for about 5 minutes. This resting time helps them set and makes transferring easier without breaking. Then carefully move them to a wire rack to cool completely.

    ⏱ 10✓ Cookies firm enough to handle without breaking
  9. Step 9

    Once cooled, dust the cookies lightly with powdered sugar for a classic finish. This adds a touch of sweetness and a pretty look perfect for Easter or spring celebrations. Store the cookies in an airtight container at room temperature to keep them fresh.

    ⏱ 5✓ Cookies are fully cooled and evenly dusted with sugar

FAQ

Can I substitute anise extract with something else?
Yes, if you don’t have anise extract, you can use a small amount of fennel seed or star anise powder, but use sparingly as these are stronger and more pungent. Alternatively, vanilla extract alone will still produce a tasty cookie, just missing that signature licorice note.
Why are my cookies turning out dry and crumbly?
Dry cookies often result from overbaking or using too much flour. Make sure to measure flour correctly by spooning and leveling it, not scooping. Also, watch the baking time closely and remove cookies when edges just turn golden.
Can I make these cookies vegan?
To make these cookies vegan, replace eggs with flax eggs (1 tbsp ground flaxseed + 3 tbsp water per egg) and use dairy-free butter. The texture may be slightly different, but they’ll still be delicious and soft.
How do I store these cookies to keep them fresh?
Store cookies in an airtight container at room temperature for up to 3 days. For longer storage, refrigerate or freeze them as described. Avoid storing in humid areas to prevent sogginess.
Can I add other flavors or spices to this recipe?
Absolutely! Cinnamon, nutmeg, or almond extract work well. Just add small amounts to avoid overpowering the delicate balance of anise and lemon.
Why is creaming butter and sugar important?
Creaming traps air into the mixture, which helps the cookies rise and creates a tender, light texture. Skipping or underdoing this step can lead to dense, flat cookies.
Can I freeze the dough before baking?
Yes, you can freeze shaped dough balls on a baking sheet, then transfer to a bag. Bake from frozen, adding a couple extra minutes to baking time. This is great for making ahead.
What’s the best way to measure flour?
Use the spoon-and-level method: spoon flour into your measuring cup and level off with a knife. Avoid packing the flour, which can add too much and make cookies dry.
