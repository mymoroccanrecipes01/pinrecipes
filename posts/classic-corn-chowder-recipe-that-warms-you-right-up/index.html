Classic Corn Chowder Recipe That Warms You Right Up

This cozy corn chowder is pure comfort in a bowl. Sweet corn, creamy potatoes, and smoky bacon come together in a simple, hearty soup that's perfect for chilly evenings or casual family dinners.

Introduction

I've been making this classic corn chowder for years now, and it’s one of those recipes that feels like a warm hug on a chilly day. The first time I tried it, I was looking for something simple but satisfying, and this chowder fit the bill perfectly. What I love most about this version is how it balances the sweetness of fresh corn with the smoky depth of bacon and the creaminess from the half-and-half. A little trick I picked up is to cook the potatoes just right so they soften enough to thicken the soup naturally, without turning mushy. It’s a subtle thing but it really makes a difference. When it’s done, this chowder is thick, velvety, and comforting with just enough bite from the corn and potatoes. It’s perfect for a cozy family dinner or when you want something easy but special. Plus, it’s easy to customize with whatever you have on hand. I think you’ll love how approachable and flavorful it is!

What You'll Need

Approximately 6 cups, 4 servings

  • 4 cups fresh corn kernels (about 6 ears)
  • 3 medium Yukon Gold potatoes, peeled and diced
  • 4 slices bacon, diced
  • 1 medium onion, finely chopped
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 3 cups vegetable broth
  • 1 cup half-and-half
  • 2 tablespoons all-purpose flour
  • 2 tablespoons unsalted butter
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste
  • 2 tablespoons chopped fresh parsley (optional)

How To Do It

Step 1
Start by cooking the diced bacon in a large pot over medium heat until it’s crispy and golden. This renders out the fat, which you’ll use to sauté the onion and garlic. Be careful not to burn the bacon, as that can add a bitter taste. Once crispy, remove the bacon pieces with a slotted spoon and set aside, leaving the fat in the pot. This step is important because the bacon fat adds a smoky depth to the chowder that you just can’t get from oil or butter alone.
⏱ Time: 8 minutes
✓ Doneness: Bacon is crisp and browned, fat rendered but not burnt
Step 2
Add the chopped onion to the bacon fat and sauté over medium heat until translucent and soft, about 5 minutes. Stir occasionally to prevent browning, which can change the flavor. Then add the minced garlic and cook for another 30 seconds until fragrant. This gentle cooking releases the sweetness of the onion and mellows the garlic, creating a flavorful base for your chowder.
⏱ Time: 6 minutes
✓ Doneness: Onions are soft and translucent, garlic fragrant but not browned
Step 3
Sprinkle the flour over the onion and garlic mixture and stir well to combine. Cook this roux for about 2 minutes to eliminate the raw flour taste. This step is key for thickening the chowder later. Stir constantly to avoid lumps and ensure the flour cooks evenly, which helps create that velvety texture we want.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Flour is absorbed, mixture looks pasty but smooth
Step 4
Slowly pour in the vegetable broth while stirring constantly to prevent lumps. Bring the mixture to a gentle simmer. The broth combines with the roux to start thickening the soup. If you add the broth too fast or skip stirring, lumps can form and ruin the texture. Simmering helps the flour fully cook and the flavors meld.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Soup base thickens slightly and bubbles gently
Step 5
Add the diced potatoes and fresh corn kernels to the pot. Stir to combine and simmer gently until the potatoes are tender but not falling apart, about 15 minutes. This timing is crucial; overcooked potatoes can make the chowder gluey, while undercooked ones won’t soften enough to thicken the soup. Keep the heat moderate to avoid breaking the potatoes down too much.
⏱ Time: 15 minutes
✓ Doneness: Potatoes are fork-tender, corn is cooked but still firm
Step 6
Once the potatoes are tender, stir in the half-and-half and butter. Heat gently but don’t boil, as boiling can cause the dairy to curdle. The butter adds richness and the half-and-half makes the chowder creamy without being too heavy. Stir until the butter melts and the chowder is heated through evenly.
⏱ Time: 3 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chowder is creamy, smooth, and steaming but not boiling
Step 7
Return the cooked bacon to the pot and stir to combine. Season with salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste. Adding the bacon back at this stage keeps it crispy and flavorful, giving the chowder a nice texture contrast. Taste and adjust seasoning carefully; the bacon adds saltiness, so go easy at first.
⏱ Time: 2 minutes
✓ Doneness: Bacon pieces are evenly distributed and chowder is well seasoned
Step 8
Let the chowder rest off the heat for 5 minutes before serving. This resting time allows the flavors to meld and the chowder to thicken slightly as it cools. It’s tempting to dig in right away, but this step really improves the taste and texture.
⏱ Time: 5 minutes
✓ Doneness: Chowder thickens a bit and flavors deepen
Step 9
Serve the corn chowder hot, garnished with chopped fresh parsley if you like. The parsley adds a fresh, bright note that balances the richness. Pair it with crusty bread or a simple salad for a complete meal. Enjoy the comforting warmth and classic flavors!
⏱ Time: Immediate
✓ Doneness: Soup is hot, garnished, and ready to enjoy

Pro Tips

Don’t rush rendering the bacon fat.
Slowly cooking bacon allows the fat to render fully without burning, which prevents bitter flavors. This fat is the flavor base for sautéing the onions and garlic, so it’s worth the extra time to get it right.
Cook potatoes just until tender, not mushy.
Potatoes release starch as they cook, thickening the chowder naturally. Overcooked potatoes break down too much, making the soup gluey, while undercooked ones won’t soften enough to thicken. Timing is key for perfect texture.
Add broth slowly while stirring when making the roux.
This prevents lumps by allowing the flour to absorb liquid gradually. Stirring constantly ensures a smooth base, which is the foundation for creamy chowder.
Heat the chowder gently after adding dairy.
Boiling after adding half-and-half can cause curdling and separate the fat. Keeping the heat low preserves a smooth, creamy texture.
Let the chowder rest before serving.
Resting allows flavors to meld and the chowder to thicken slightly, improving both taste and texture.
Use fresh corn when possible.
Fresh corn provides sweetness and texture that frozen or canned corn can’t match, making the chowder taste vibrant and fresh.
Garnish with fresh parsley for brightness.
A sprinkle of fresh herbs adds color and a fresh note that balances the chowder’s richness.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Adding dairy too early or boiling after adding it.

Why it happens People often add cream or half-and-half too soon or boil the soup afterward, which causes curdling.

Fix Add dairy only after the potatoes are cooked and heat gently without boiling. Stir constantly to keep the texture smooth.

Not rendering bacon fat fully.

Why it happens Rushing the bacon step or cooking at too high heat burns the fat or leaves it greasy.

Fix Cook bacon slowly over medium heat until crisp and fat is fully rendered, then remove bacon pieces and use the fat for sautéing.

Overcooking potatoes.

Why it happens Leaving potatoes simmering too long breaks them down into mush, making the chowder gluey.

Fix Check potatoes frequently and remove from heat as soon as they’re fork-tender but still intact.

Skipping the roux step or adding flour directly to broth.

Why it happens Some skip making a roux and add flour directly, causing lumps and raw flour taste.

Fix Cook flour with butter and aromatics before adding broth to create a smooth, flavorful base.

Using canned corn instead of fresh or frozen.

Why it happens Canned corn is convenient but can be mushy and less flavorful.

Fix Use fresh or frozen corn for better texture and natural sweetness.

Variations

Vegetarian Corn Chowder

Skip the bacon and use olive oil or butter to sauté the onions and garlic. Add smoked paprika or liquid smoke for a smoky flavor without meat. This version is just as creamy and comforting but meat-free.

Note: Without bacon fat, be sure to use good quality broth and season well to boost flavor.

Spicy Corn Chowder

Add diced jalapeños or a pinch of cayenne pepper when sautéing the onions for a gentle kick. Top with a dollop of sour cream or chopped fresh chili for extra heat. This adds warmth and complexity to the classic chowder.

Note: Adjust spice level carefully to balance with the sweetness of the corn.

Cheesy Corn Chowder

Stir in shredded sharp cheddar cheese just before serving for a rich, cheesy twist. The cheese melts into the chowder, adding depth and creaminess that’s irresistible.

Note: Add cheese off heat or on very low heat to prevent separation.

Creamy Corn and Bacon Chowder with Leeks

Replace onions with leeks for a milder, sweeter flavor. Leeks add a subtle oniony note that pairs beautifully with corn and bacon, making the chowder even more flavorful.

Note: Clean leeks thoroughly to remove grit before cooking.

Corn Chowder with Sweet Potatoes

Swap Yukon Gold potatoes for sweet potatoes for a slightly sweeter, earthier chowder. This variation adds color and a different kind of creaminess while keeping the dish comforting.

Note: Sweet potatoes cook faster, so adjust simmer time accordingly.

Storage & Tips

Room Temperature

Corn chowder can safely sit at room temperature for up to 2 hours. Cover it loosely with a lid or foil to protect from contaminants. Avoid leaving it out longer to prevent bacterial growth.

Refrigerator

Store chowder in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavors deepen but the texture may thicken; reheat gently with a splash of broth or water to loosen it up before serving.

Freezer

Freeze chowder in airtight containers or heavy-duty freezer bags for up to 3 months. Cool completely before freezing to prevent ice crystals. Thaw overnight in the fridge and reheat slowly on the stove.

Reheating

Reheat on the stovetop over low heat, stirring frequently. Add a little broth or water if the chowder is too thick. Avoid boiling to prevent curdling of the dairy.

Make Ahead

Prepare the chowder base up to the point before adding dairy and bacon. Store this base in the fridge for 1-2 days. On serving day, reheat gently, add half-and-half and bacon, then finish cooking as directed for fresh flavor.

FAQ

Can I use frozen corn instead of fresh?
Yes, frozen corn works well and is a great substitute when fresh isn’t available. Just thaw it before adding to the chowder. Frozen corn retains much of its sweetness and texture, making it a convenient option without sacrificing flavor.
What can I substitute for bacon if I want a vegetarian version?
You can omit bacon entirely and use olive oil or butter to sauté the aromatics. Adding smoked paprika or a few drops of liquid smoke can mimic the smoky flavor bacon provides, giving your chowder depth without meat.
How do I prevent the chowder from curdling?
Avoid boiling the chowder after adding dairy like half-and-half. Heat it gently over low heat and stir constantly. High heat causes the proteins in dairy to separate, resulting in curdling. Patience is key here.
Can I make this chowder ahead of time?
Absolutely! You can prepare the soup base (without dairy and bacon) a day or two ahead. Store it in the fridge, then add the half-and-half and bacon when reheating for best freshness and texture.
What type of potatoes are best for chowder?
Yukon Gold potatoes are ideal because they hold their shape while releasing enough starch to thicken the chowder naturally. Avoid waxy or overly starchy potatoes which can affect texture.
Is there a way to make this chowder thicker?
Cooking the potatoes until tender releases starch that thickens the chowder naturally. If you want it thicker, you can mash some of the potatoes in the pot or add a bit more flour during the roux step.
Can I use cream instead of half-and-half?
Yes, heavy cream will make the chowder richer and thicker. Just be careful not to boil the chowder after adding cream to prevent curdling. Adjust seasoning as cream adds richness.
How do I store leftovers properly?
Cool the chowder quickly and store in airtight containers in the fridge for up to 4 days. Reheat gently on the stove with a splash of broth to restore creaminess.
